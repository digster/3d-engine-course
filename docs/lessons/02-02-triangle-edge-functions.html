The Triangle: Edge Functions

Lesson 2.1 ended with an error term that was quietly answering a question: which side of this line am I on? Ask it for three edges at once and you have decided whether a point is inside a triangle — which is the whole of filled-shape rasterization, and is very close to what the silicon in your GPU actually does. Along the way we will finally fix the asymmetry that made draw_line disagree with itself.

1The Problem

You can draw the outline of a shape. You cannot draw the shape.

That sounds like a small gap and it is not. Every solid surface you will ever render — every wall, every character, every blade of grass — is a filled shape, and "which pixels are inside this outline?" is a genuinely different question from "which pixels are on this line?" The obvious approach, and the one most people reach for first, is to fill between the outline's left and right edges on each scanline: find where the outline crosses row y, and paint between the crossings.

It works. It is called scanline filling, it is what everybody did until the mid-1980s, and it has three properties that make it the wrong foundation for us:

  • It is inherently sequential. You compute row y's crossings by updating row y−1's. That is a chain, and a chain cannot be split across cores or SIMD lanes — which matters enormously once we care about speed, and matters absolutely when we move to a GPU in Module 4 where thousands of pixels are shaded at once.
  • It needs sorted edges and special cases. Horizontal edges, vertices that are local minima, self-intersections — each is a branch in the setup code, and each is somewhere for a bug to hide.
  • It answers the wrong question. It tells you a span, when what you will shortly need — for depth, for texture, for lighting — is a per-pixel answer to "where in this triangle am I?" Lesson 2.3 needs that number, and scanline filling does not naturally produce it.

So we are going to do something that looks, at first, absurdly wasteful: test every pixel independently. No spans, no sorting, no incremental crossings — just a question asked of each pixel in turn, with the answer depending on nothing but that pixel's coordinates.

"Wasteful" is the wrong axis

Testing every pixel sounds strictly worse than walking spans, and per-pixel it is more work. But the work is independent, and independent work is the only kind that parallelises. This is the trade that modern hardware made: a GPU would rather do ten times the arithmetic in parallel than a tenth of it in sequence.

We will keep the honesty of that claim by measuring it. §3.5 makes the naive version fast enough to ship — a 75× speedup measured on this codebase — without ever giving up the independence.

And there is a second problem, inherited. Lesson 2.1 §3.6 proved that draw_line is asymmetric: a third of all lines light different pixels depending on which end you start from. For a wireframe that is a curiosity. For filled shapes that share edges — which is every mesh, ever — it is a defect you can see, and today we fix it properly.

2Building Intuition

Why a triangle, and nothing else

Before deciding how to fill a shape, notice which shape we are going to bother with. Every renderer built in the last thirty years — every GPU, every game engine, every film renderer at some stage of its pipeline — reduces its geometry to triangles. Not because triangles are pretty, but because of three properties that no other polygon has all of.

Three points are always planar. Take four points in space and there is no reason they lie in a plane; nudge one and your "flat" quad is a saddle, and every question you ask of it — where does this ray hit it, which way does it face — becomes ambiguous. Three points define a plane, always, with nothing to check.

Three points are always convex. A quad can be concave, or worse, can cross itself into a bow-tie. A triangle cannot. That is what lets "inside" be characterised by three independent tests that all have to agree — the trick this whole lesson rests on — and it is what makes the interpolation in Lessons 2.3 and 2.4 well-defined everywhere inside.

Any polygon decomposes into triangles. So nothing is lost. A quad is two triangles, an n-gon is n−2 of them, and a curved surface is as many as your budget allows. Triangles are not a restriction; they are a normal form.

The tessellation you are about to inherit

Decomposing polygons into triangles is exactly what creates shared edges — the quad in Figure 6 becomes two triangles that meet along a diagonal — and shared edges are what make §3.6's fill rule necessary. The normal form and the problem arrive together.

Three half-planes

Now the idea the lesson turns on, and it is worth meeting as a picture first.

Take one edge of the triangle and extend it infinitely in both directions. That line cuts the entire plane into two halves. Every point in the world is on one side or the other (or exactly on the line). Nothing about the triangle is needed to say which — just the line.

Do that for all three edges and you have cut the plane into seven regions (Figure 1). The triangle is one of them: the region on the inner side of all three lines at once. Not "the region bounded by three segments" — the intersection of three half-planes. Same shape, completely different algorithm.

So the entire problem has been reduced to a single sub-problem, asked three times: given a line and a point, which side is the point on? Answer that cheaply and exactly, and you can fill triangles.

3The Theory

The edge function, derived

We want a function of a point that is positive on one side of a directed line, negative on the other, and zero on it. Lesson 1.7 built exactly the machinery for this, and the derivation is three lines long.

Take the directed edge from A to B, and a point P. Form two arrows from the same tail: the edge itself, e = B − A, and the arrow to the point, p = P − A (Figure 2). Now — which side of e does p lie on?

Lesson 1.7's perpendicular(v) = (−v.y, v.x) turns the edge a quarter-turn, giving a direction that points directly off to one side of it. And the dot product measures how much of one arrow lies along another. So dot the point-arrow with the edge's perpendicular and the sign tells you the side — positive if p leans toward the perpendicular, negative if away, zero if exactly along the edge.

\[ E(\mathbf{P}) = \operatorname{perp}(\mathbf{e}) \cdot \mathbf{p}, \qquad \operatorname{perp}(\mathbf{e}) = (-e_y,\; e_x) \] E(P) = dot( perpendicular(B - A), P - A )

In prose: the edge function is the dot product of the point-arrow with the edge turned a quarter turn. Expand it, using e = (Bx − Ax, By − Ay) and p = (Px − Ax, Py − Ay):

\[ E(\mathbf{P}) = -(B_y - A_y)(P_x - A_x) + (B_x - A_x)(P_y - A_y) \] E(P) = -(By - Ay)*(Px - Ax) + (Bx - Ax)*(Py - Ay)

Rearranged into the form we will actually write:

\[ E(\mathbf{P}) = (B_x - A_x)(P_y - A_y) - (B_y - A_y)(P_x - A_x) \] E(P) = (Bx - Ax)*(Py - Ay) - (By - Ay)*(Px - Ax)

Two multiplies, one subtract, and four subtractions of coordinates. No division, no square root, no trigonometry — and if the inputs are integers then so is the output, which by Lesson 2.1 §3.7's argument means the answer is exact and identical on every machine.

You have also just written the 2-D cross product

(Bx − Ax)(Py − Ay) − (By − Ay)(Px − Ax) is the z component of the 3-D cross product (B − A) × (P − A) with both inputs' z set to zero. In 2-D the cross product is not a vector — there is only one direction perpendicular to the plane, so all that survives is a single signed number.

That is worth knowing now because Lesson 3.4 uses the full 3-D cross product for surface normals and back-face culling, and it will help to recognise this as the same object seen flat. The Math Toolbox lists both.

What the sign means (and the y-flip)

Here is where a lesson can quietly ruin a student's next six months, so we are going to be exact, and the numbers below are measured rather than asserted.

Our framebuffer has +y pointing down — row 0 is the top (Lesson 1.5). That single fact inverts the handedness of the plane relative to the y-up convention every geometry textbook uses. So:

The convention, verified

In framebuffer coordinates, a triangle whose vertices appear counter-clockwise on screen has a negative signed area by the formula above. Clockwise on screen gives positive.

Measured: (5,0) → (0,10) → (10,10), which is counter-clockwise on screen, gives −100. The same three vertices in the other order give +100. Swapping any two vertices flips the sign and changes nothing else.

This is not a contradiction of the course's "CCW = front" convention on the Conventions page. That rule is about NDC, where +y points up. The viewport transform — which Lesson 2.11 derives — flips y on its way to pixels, and flipping one axis reverses orientation. A triangle that is counter-clockwise in NDC arrives at the framebuffer clockwise. Both statements are true of the same triangle in different spaces, and knowing which space you are in is the entire skill.

Because of that, fill_triangle deliberately does not care which winding it is given. It measures the sign once and orients the test to match, so a "backwards" triangle fills rather than vanishing. Culling by winding is a separate decision made in a separate space, and Lesson 3.4 makes it.

What the magnitude means

The sign is only half of what the edge function gives you. Its magnitude is twice the area of the triangle ABP.

You can see why without algebra. The dot product with the perpendicular measures how far P stands off the line AB, scaled by the length of AB — that is, base × height. And base × height is twice the area of a triangle. So the same number that tells you which side also tells you how far off, in units that happen to be an area.

\[ E_{AB}(\mathbf{P}) = 2 \cdot \operatorname{area}(\triangle ABP) \] E_AB(P) = 2 * area of triangle ABP

Evaluate it at the triangle's own third vertex and you get twice the area of the whole triangle — one number that answers both "which way round is this?" and "is it degenerate?"

This is the whole of Lesson 2.3, arriving early

Three edge functions, each proportional to the area of a sub-triangle, and their sum is the area of the whole. Divide each by the total and you have three fractions that sum to one and describe exactly where in the triangle a point lies.

Those are barycentric coordinates, and they are what turn a filled shape into a shape with colour, depth and texture across it. You are getting them for free as a by-product of the inside test — which is why edge functions won. Lesson 2.3 does nothing but divide by the area and take the consequences seriously.

A worked example

Worked example — one triangle, four points

Take A = (2, 2), B = (10, 4), C = (4, 10), in framebuffer coordinates. First, the total:

  • EAB(C) = (10−2)(10−2) − (4−2)(4−2) = 8·8 − 2·2 = 64 − 4 = 60

Positive, so this triangle is clockwise on screen, and its area is 60/2 = 30 square pixels. Since it is positive, "inside" means all three edge functions ≥ 0.

Now test the point P = (5, 5) against all three edges:

  • EAB(P) = (10−2)(5−2) − (4−2)(5−2) = 24 − 6 = 18
  • EBC(P) = (4−10)(5−4) − (10−4)(5−10) = −6 + 30 = 24
  • ECA(P) = (2−4)(5−10) − (2−10)(5−4) = 10 + 8 = 18

All three positive, so (5,5) is inside. And notice the sum: 18 + 24 + 18 = 60 — exactly the total area. That is not a coincidence, it is the barycentric identity above, and it is a superb debugging check: if your three edge functions do not sum to the total, one of them is wrong.

Now a point outside, Q = (9, 9):

  • EAB(Q) = 8·7 − 2·7 = 56 − 14 = 42
  • EBC(Q) = (−6)(5) − (6)(−1) = −30 + 6 = −24
  • ECA(Q) = (−2)(−1) − (−8)(5) = 2 + 40 = 42

One negative, so Q is outside — it is beyond the edge from B to C. Sum check: 42 − 24 + 42 = 60 ✓. The identity holds for points outside too; that is what makes barycentric coordinates able to describe the whole plane.

Making it incremental

Two multiplies per edge per pixel, three edges, is six multiplies per pixel. We can do better, and the way we do it is the technique from Lesson 2.1 wearing a different hat.

Look at the edge function again as a function of the pixel position, holding A and B fixed. Every term is either constant or linear in Px and Py — there is no Px², no Px·Py. The function is affine, and an affine function has the property that stepping the input by a fixed amount changes the output by a fixed amount:

\[ E(P_x + 1,\, P_y) - E(P_x,\, P_y) = -(B_y - A_y) = A_y - B_y \] stepping x by 1 changes E by (Ay - By)
\[ E(P_x,\, P_y + 1) - E(P_x,\, P_y) = B_x - A_x \] stepping y by 1 changes E by (Bx - Ax)

In prose: moving one pixel right always changes the edge function by the same constant, and so does moving one pixel down. So evaluate the three edge functions once, at a corner, and after that each pixel costs three additions.

That is precisely Lesson 2.1's move — track a difference instead of recomputing a value — and it is worth noticing that it worked for the same reason both times: the quantity being tracked is affine in the thing being stepped. Figure 3 shows the three lattices of values.

Combine that with a bounding box — only test pixels within the smallest rectangle containing the triangle, since no pixel outside it can possibly be inside — and the "absurdly wasteful" test-every-pixel approach becomes genuinely fast.

Measured — what the two optimisations are worth

Two hundred small triangles drawn into a 512×512 buffer, 400 repetitions, Apple M4 Pro, clang 21, -O2:

  • Full-buffer scan, edge functions evaluated directly at every pixel: 5763 ms
  • Bounding box + incremental stepping: 77 ms
  • Speedup: 75×

Most of that is the bounding box — a small triangle in a large buffer wastes almost all of a full scan — but the incremental stepping is what makes the remaining inner loop tight. And neither optimisation gave up the property that made the approach attractive: every pixel is still decided independently of every other.

The fill rule

One question remains, and it is the one Lesson 2.1 left open. What about a pixel whose centre lies exactly on an edge — where the edge function is exactly zero?

For a single triangle, who cares. For two triangles sharing an edge — which is what a quad is, what a mesh is, what everything is — it matters twice over:

  • Include the boundary in both, and every pixel along the seam is drawn twice. With opaque colours you will not see it; with transparency the seam is visibly darker, and with additive blending it glows. Worse, you paid for the work twice.
  • Exclude it from both, and there is a crack of background between two shapes that are supposed to be flush.

So the boundary must belong to exactly one of them — and the two triangles must agree on which, without talking to each other. Neither knows the other exists; they are rasterised minutes apart, possibly on different threads.

The trick is to make the decision depend only on the edge's geometry, never on which triangle is asking. Two triangles that share an edge traverse it in opposite directions — that is what sharing an edge means (Figure 4). So any rule phrased in terms of the edge's direction automatically gives opposite answers to the two of them.

The rule everybody uses — Direct3D specifies it, OpenGL permits it, GPUs implement it — is the top-left rule: a triangle owns the pixels on its top and left edges, and not those on its bottom and right edges. For a triangle oriented to positive area in our y-down space:

Implementing it costs nothing per pixel. An edge function is zero exactly on its edge, so testing w ≥ 0 includes boundary pixels and w − 1 ≥ 0 excludes them — and that −1 can be folded into the starting value before the loop begins. The inner loop does not change at all.

Verified, exhaustively

A quad split into two triangles: 0 pixels drawn twice, 0 interior pixels missed. A 12-triangle fan around a common centre, exercising the rule at twelve different angles: 0 pixels drawn twice. With the rule switched off, the same quad double-draws its whole seam.

There is a consequence worth stating plainly, because it surprises people and it is not a bug: a lone triangle no longer draws its bottom and right boundary. Coverage becomes half-open, exactly like a [start, end) range in code — and for the same reason. Half-open ranges tile without gaps or overlaps; closed ones cannot.

Measured — precisely what the rule removes

Filling a 37×37 quad as two triangles, with the rule versus without, and comparing which pixels are covered:

  • Pixels dropped by the rule: 73
  • Of those, lying anywhere other than the quad's bottom row or right column: 0
  • Pixels added by the rule: 0

And 37 + 37 − 1 = 73 — the bottom row, plus the right column, minus the corner they share. The rule removes exactly the half-open boundary and nothing else. If you ever wonder whether a fill rule is misbehaving, that arithmetic is the check.

4Implementation

edge_function goes in the header, constexpr, because it is three arithmetic operations and every caller wants it inlined — the same judgement Lesson 1.7 made for vec2:

Integers are exact, but they are not infinite

Those products overflow a 32-bit int once coordinates exceed roughly ±16000 — two values near that limit multiply to about 228, and subtracting two such terms can reach 229–231. Every framebuffer in this course is far inside that bound, and Module 3's clipping keeps it that way by construction.

It is documented in the header rather than left to be discovered, because signed overflow in C++ is undefined behaviour — not a wrapped number, but a licence for the optimiser to assume it cannot happen. That failure would not look like a slightly wrong triangle; it would look like the compiler deleting your bounds check.

fill_triangle then has five jobs, in order. First, measure the area — which doubles as the degeneracy check:

Three collinear points enclose nothing, so a zero area returns immediately. That is not merely an optimisation: with zero area the three half-planes have no common interior, the top-left rule's orientation assumption becomes meaningless, and the code below would be reasoning about a shape that does not exist. Degenerate "sliver" triangles are a real thing to hit the moment meshes arrive, so they are handled rather than assumed away.

Swapping two vertices flips the sign of all three edge functions at once, which is why normalising the winding here — once, before the loop — lets the per-pixel test be a plain >= 0 rather than "the same sign as the area" evaluated three times per pixel.

Then the bounding box, clipped to the framebuffer:

This does two things and it is worth separating them. It bounds the search, which is where most of the 75× came from. And because the box is clipped here, every pixel the loop visits is guaranteed in bounds — which is what lets the inner loop write through fb.row(y) and skip put_pixel's per-pixel bounds check and index multiply entirely. Lesson 1.5 called that "safe by default, fast when you ask"; this is the asking.

Clipping the box is not clipping the triangle

Note what did not happen: the triangle's vertices were not moved. Clamping the search box is exact — it cannot change which pixels are inside, because the pixels it removes were off-screen anyway. Moving a vertex to the screen edge, by contrast, would produce a different triangle. That distinction is why this is three lines here and a whole lesson in Module 3, where geometry behind the camera genuinely must be cut.

Then the fill rule, folded into the starting values so the inner loop never sees it:

Each edge function is paired with the vertex opposite it, which is the convention that makes Lesson 2.3's barycentric weights fall out in the right order — w0 will be the weight of vertex 0. Worth getting right now.

And finally the loop:

No multiplies, no divisions, no branches except the test itself, and nothing carried from one pixel to the next except three running sums that could equally have been recomputed. That last point is the one to hold onto: the incremental form is an optimisation, not a dependency. Delete the += lines and evaluate the three edge functions from scratch at each pixel and you get identical output — which is exactly what the verification harness does, and it agrees pixel-for-pixel across 144 triangles including many that hang off the edge of the buffer.

C++ in place — std::min with an initializer list

std::min({x0, x1, x2}) is a different overload from std::min(a, b): it takes a std::initializer_list and returns the smallest of any number of values. It lives in <algorithm>, and the braces are what select it.

Watch for one trap that is not obvious. The list overload returns by value, while the two-argument form returns a reference — so const auto& m = std::min(a, b); is fine but dangling if the arguments were temporaries. Taking the result by value, as here, sidesteps the whole question.

The demo's most useful mode is half-planes, and it is written the deliberately slow way — direct edge_function calls, no stepping — because there the point is what is being computed rather than how cheaply:

Paint that number and Figure 1 appears on your screen, live, from the same code the engine uses. The seven regions are right there.

The coverage panel needs one more idea, and it is the only honest way to show a fill rule working:

You cannot see a double-drawn pixel — so count instead

Draw the same pixel twice in the same colour and it looks exactly like drawing it once. The defect is invisible because the second write lands on top of the first. So the demo does not draw the two triangles into the framebuffer at all: it draws them into a counter, one integer per pixel, then colours by the count — green for one, red for two.

Generalise the habit: when a bug is idempotent, instrument the operation rather than inspecting the result.

6Build & Run

No new source file this lesson, so there is nothing to add to CMakeLists.txt and no reconfigure needed.

What you should see

A window titled "Triangles — Module 2": a slowly rotating filled triangle on the left, and on the right a square split into two triangles, drawn into a coverage counter (Figure 5).

Checkpoint

Press 3 for the half-plane view. You should see Figure 1 rendered live: a bright triangle where all three tests pass, surrounded by progressively darker bands where only two or one do. Watch it rotate — the bands sweep around, and the bright region stays exactly the triangle.

Now break it

  1. Press R to switch the fill rule off. A red diagonal cuts through the green square, and the readout jumps from 0 px to 40 px. Every pixel along the shared edge is being drawn by both triangles.
  2. Press R again. The seam vanishes, immediately, with nothing else changed.
  3. Press 2 for wireframe and compare it against 1. The outline and the fill do not light the same boundary pixels, and now you know why: an outline wants both endpoints of every edge; a fill wants each shared edge counted once.
Why the demo uses an axis-aligned square, and why that is the honest choice

A pixel can only be drawn twice if its centre lies exactly on the shared edge. For an edge of arbitrary slope, almost no pixel centres do — rotate that square and the defect collapses to two or three stray pixels, which is easy to dismiss as noise.

A 45° diagonal passes through every pixel centre along it, so the whole seam doubles at once. That is not a rigged demonstration: quads split into triangle pairs, terrain grids and UI rectangles are overwhelmingly axis-aligned, so the catastrophic case is also the common one. A bug that is subtle on your test scene and total on the user's is the worst kind — the same shape as Lesson 1.8's tunnelling, which was unreachable at 60 Hz and certain at 10.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Half the triangles are invisible — geometry disappears when it turns
Symptom
A rotating triangle vanishes for half its rotation, or a mesh shows only some faces.
Cause
Testing w0 >= 0 && w1 >= 0 && w2 >= 0 without first normalising the winding. When the triangle turns past edge-on its signed area changes sign, every edge function flips, and nothing passes.
Fix
Measure the area once and swap two vertices if it is negative, as fill_triangle does. If you want back-facing triangles culled, do it deliberately and in the right space — that is Lesson 3.4, not an accident of the inside test.
A hairline crack between two triangles — background showing through a seam
Symptom
Two triangles that should be flush have a one-pixel line of background between them.
Cause
Boundary pixels excluded by both triangles — typically testing w > 0 instead of w >= 0, which excludes the edge from everyone.
Fix
Use >= 0 plus the top-left bias. The diagnostic is the coverage counter from §4: count writes per pixel instead of looking at colours, and a crack shows as zeros where you expect ones.
The seam is darker through transparency — double-drawn edges
Symptom
Everything looks right with opaque colours, but blend the triangles and a lattice of darker lines appears along every shared edge.
Cause
No fill rule. Both triangles claim the boundary, so it is blended twice.
Fix
The top-left rule. And note the general lesson: this defect is invisible under opaque drawing — it costs you performance and correctness for months without a symptom, then appears the day someone enables transparency.
Triangles are correct but rasterising is slow — frame time scales with screen size
Symptom
Frame time depends on the framebuffer's size rather than on how much of it the geometry covers. Small triangles cost as much as large ones.
Cause
No bounding box — every triangle is scanned against every pixel in the buffer.
Fix
Clamp the search to min/max of the three vertices. Measured on this codebase, the bounding box plus incremental stepping is 75× faster.
Occasional garbage pixels or a crash on large coordinates — integer overflow
Symptom
Everything is fine until a vertex is far off-screen, then a triangle fills wrongly, or the whole buffer, or the program dies.
Cause
edge_function's products overflowing 32 bits past about ±16000. Signed overflow is undefined behaviour, so the optimiser may have assumed it could not happen and deleted a check that depended on it.
Fix
Clip before rasterising (Module 3), or widen to 64-bit if you genuinely need enormous coordinates. Do not "fix" it by clamping the vertices — that changes the triangle's shape, which is a different bug wearing the first one's clothes.
A lone triangle looks a pixel short — missing bottom and right edges
Symptom
A single filled triangle appears one pixel smaller along its bottom and right than the wireframe outline of the same vertices.
Cause
Nothing. That is the top-left rule doing its job: coverage is half-open, and a triangle owns its top and left edges only.
Fix
None wanted. It is the same trade as a [start, end) range — the price of tiling without gaps or overlaps. If a single shape must include its full boundary, grow it by half a pixel rather than changing the rule, because changing the rule breaks every shared edge in the scene.

8Exercises

2.2.1Test the sum identitywarm-up

§3.4 noticed that the three edge functions sum to the total area, for points inside and outside. Verify it: pick a triangle, evaluate all three edge functions plus the total at a few dozen scattered points, and assert the sum every time. Then work out why it must be true.

Hint

Think in areas. The three sub-triangles PBC, PCA, PAB tile the original when P is inside. When P is outside, one of them is negatively signed and the tiling still works — which is exactly what a signed area is for.

Solution

Expand the three edge functions symbolically and the P terms cancel: every Px appears once positively and once negatively. What is left is EAB(C), the total — independent of P, which is the strong form of the statement.

This makes an excellent assertion to leave in a debug build. If a rasterizer ever produces weights that do not sum to the area, one edge function has its vertices in the wrong order, and you will find out at the point of the bug rather than three lessons later when the texture looks skewed.

2.2.2Watch the rule with a rotated seamwarm-up

Change the demo's coverage panel to rotate its quad instead of keeping it axis-aligned, and turn the fill rule off. Count the double-drawn pixels as it turns. Explain the pattern.

Hint

A pixel is at risk only when its centre satisfies E == 0 exactly. For which slopes do many integer points satisfy that? You proved the relevant fact in Lesson 2.1 §3.6 — it was about gcd.

Solution sketch

The count spikes whenever the seam's slope is a simple rational — 0, ±1, ±½, ±2 — and falls to nearly nothing in between, for the same reason Lesson 2.1's ties only existed when major/gcd was even. Simple slopes pass through many pixel centres; awkward ones pass through almost none.

The uncomfortable conclusion is the valuable one: the frequency of a bug is not evidence about its severity. This one is rare on arbitrary geometry and total on the geometry people actually build.

2.2.3Give each vertex a colourmedium

You already compute three numbers per pixel that are proportional to the sub-triangle areas. Divide each by the total area and you get three weights that sum to 1. Use them to blend three vertex colours across the triangle.

Hint

w0 pairs with vertex 0 — that is why fill_triangle pairs each edge function with the vertex opposite it. Weight i should be 1 at vertex i and 0 at the other two; check that before blending anything.

Two traps. The bias from the fill rule perturbs the weights by a fraction of a pixel, so use unbiased values for interpolation and biased ones only for the inside test. And blending colours means blending light — Lesson 1.6's mix_linear, not mix_encoded.

Solution sketch

This is Lesson 2.4 done early, and doing it early is a good idea. The thing to notice is that nothing new is computed: the interpolation is a by-product of the inside test you already ran. That is the property that made edge functions beat scanline filling, and it is much more convincing once you have felt it.

When you get to Module 3 you will discover this is subtly wrong in perspective — screen-space linear interpolation is not surface-linear once a triangle is tilted away from the camera. Enjoy it while it lasts; Lesson 3.2 shows the artifact and fixes it with the 1/w trick.

2.2.4Make it wider than one pixel at a timeopen

The inner loop tests one pixel per iteration, but the three step constants are known before the loop starts — so the values for the next four pixels are just as computable. Restructure to evaluate four (or eight) pixels at once, and measure.

Hint

For each edge, the four values are w, w + s, w + 2s, w + 3s. You do not need intrinsics to start: write it as four independent variables and check whether the compiler vectorises it ( -Rpass=loop-vectorize on clang will tell you).

Solution sketch

This is how real rasterizers are built, and it is the pay-off for the independence we insisted on in §1: because no pixel's answer depends on its neighbour's, four can be decided simultaneously. A scanline filler cannot do this at all.

Expect a smaller win than you hope for on small triangles — the setup cost is amortised over fewer pixels, and edge blocks partly outside the triangle waste lanes. Measure across a range of triangle sizes rather than one, and you will rediscover why GPUs rasterise in tiles and why tiny triangles are a well-known performance problem in real engines.

2.2.5Prove the rule, do not just test itopen

The harness checks exactly-once coverage for a quad and a 12-triangle fan. That is evidence, not proof. Argue it properly: for any two triangles sharing an edge, exactly one claims a pixel lying on that edge.

Hint

Start from the fact that the shared edge is traversed as A→B by one triangle and B→A by the other. Show that is_top_left(A,B) and is_top_left(B,A) always differ. Handle the horizontal case (dy == 0) separately from the non-horizontal one, and check what happens when the edge is degenerate.

Solution sketch

For a non-horizontal edge, dy is non-zero and reversing flips its sign, so exactly one direction has dy < 0. For a horizontal edge dy == 0 in both directions and dx flips sign, so exactly one has dx > 0 — provided dx ≠ 0, which is guaranteed because a zero-length edge would mean a degenerate triangle, which we rejected up front.

Note where that proof leans on the degeneracy check. Rejecting zero-area triangles was not housekeeping; the fill rule's correctness depends on it. Finding that kind of load-bearing precondition is most of what "prove it" is worth.

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • It fills triangles — correctly in either winding, with degenerate and off-screen input handled, and every pixel decided independently of every other.
  • The edge function, derived from Lesson 1.7's dot product and perpendicular, giving a side from its sign and an area from its magnitude.
  • The sign convention is pinned down: screen-counter-clockwise is negative in a y-down buffer, and the flip traces to the viewport transform rather than to anything mysterious.
  • The affine-stepping technique, reused. Same move as Bresenham's error term, applied to a different quantity — and 75× faster than the direct form once paired with a bounding box.
  • A fill rule, which closes the asymmetry Lesson 2.1 found: exactly-once coverage on shared edges, verified rather than asserted, at the cost of making coverage half-open.

And one idea that will keep paying: when a defect is idempotent, instrument the operation rather than inspecting the result. A pixel drawn twice looks exactly like a pixel drawn once, so no amount of staring would have found it — counting found it instantly.

Next: 2.3 — Barycentric Coordinates from Signed Areas. We are already computing three numbers proportional to the areas of three sub-triangles, and §3.3 showed they sum to the whole. Divide by that total and they become three fractions summing to one — a coordinate system that describes where inside a triangle a point is, without reference to x or y at all.

That is what turns a flat silhouette into a surface: the same weights carry colour in 2.4, depth in 3.1, texture coordinates in 3.7, and normals for lighting in 3.6. They are not handed down as a definition — they fall out of the areas you already have.

10Further Reading

  • Juan Pineda (1988) A Parallel Algorithm for Polygon Rasterization, SIGGRAPH '88. Four pages, and the origin of everything in this lesson — the paper that replaced scanline filling with edge functions precisely because the latter parallelises. Read it; it is remarkably approachable.
  • Fabian Giesen Optimizing Software Occlusion Culling, especially "The barycentric conspiracy" and "Triangle rasterization in practice". The best modern writing on this material, and it goes considerably further than we have — sub-pixel precision, tiled traversal, SIMD.
  • Real-Time Rendering §23.1 and §23.7 — how the fixed-function rasterizer in real hardware is organised, and where the tile hierarchy comes from.
  • Direct3D 11 spec — the rasterization rules section, which specifies the top-left rule normatively. Worth seeing that this is a documented contract rather than a folk remedy.
  • Scratchapixel Rasterization: a Practical Implementation — a slower, more detailed walk over the same ground, with the barycentric material of Lesson 2.3 woven in.
