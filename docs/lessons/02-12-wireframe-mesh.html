Milestone: A Spinning Wireframe Mesh

Every transform in the geometry pipeline now exists and is understood. This lesson adds almost no new theory — it adds the thing the transforms were for. We introduce indexed geometry, derive a real mesh from the golden ratio, and then light the whole chain up end to end: a solid spinning in perspective, flown with a camera you built, with not one line you cannot explain. Then we take an honest inventory of everything still missing, and hand each item to the Module 3 lesson that fixes it.

1The Problem

There is no bug to fix this time. The pipeline is complete and correct: model, view, projection, divide, viewport, all derived, all verified. And yet the engine has spent seven lessons drawing a box — eight corners typed out by hand, and a hand-written list of twelve edges joining them.

Two things are wrong with stopping there, and neither is a defect exactly; they are both unfinished business.

First, that is not how geometry is stored. Our cube's edge list is a list of lines, and no renderer in the world represents a model as a list of lines. Real meshes are triangles, because triangles are what fills, shades, and textures; and real meshes share their vertices, because a corner belongs to several faces at once and storing it once is both smaller and faster. Our hand-written arrays do neither. They also happen to be exactly the wrong shape for the GPU: Module 4 will want a vertex buffer and an index buffer, and a list of edges is not convertible into one without doing the work we are about to do anyway.

Second, we have never actually seen it all work at once. Every lesson from 2.5 onward verified its own stage — a matrix here, a divide there — but a cube is a poor witness. It is axis-aligned, so a rotation looks like a square wobbling; it has eight vertices, so vertex sharing is invisible; and its silhouette is the same from most angles, so perspective has little to show off. The pipeline deserves a better subject.

What a milestone lesson is for

This one adds a single new idea (indexed geometry) and otherwise spends its length on consolidation: deriving a mesh worth drawing, verifying it properly, walking one vertex through the entire chain with real numbers, and then being honest about the gap between what we have built and a renderer. If a section here feels like revision, that is the intent — the goal is that by the end you could rebuild Module 2 from scratch and defend every decision.

2Building Intuition

A mesh is a dot-to-dot with repeats

Picture a children's dot-to-dot puzzle: numbered dots on a page, and instructions to join 1 to 2 to 3. A mesh is that, with one twist — the instructions are allowed to reuse numbers, and they come in threes.

So there are two lists. The first is the vertices: the actual positions, each written down exactly once. The second is the indices: whole numbers pointing into the first list, taken three at a time, each triple naming one triangle. To draw the mesh you walk the index list, and for each triple you look up three positions and draw a triangle between them.

The reuse is the entire point. On an icosahedron every corner is shared by five triangles. Write each triangle's corners out in full and you store 60 positions; store 12 and point at them and you store 12. That is a five-fold saving in memory — and, far more importantly, a five-fold saving in work, because the expensive thing is not storing a vertex but pushing it through the matrix chain, and with indices you transform each vertex once no matter how many triangles claim it.

Three golden rectangles

Now we need a shape worth drawing, and there is a construction so elegant it feels like a magic trick. Take three identical rectangles. Stand them at right angles to one another, all passing through a common centre, like the three coordinate planes. Their twelve corners are the twelve vertices of a regular icosahedron — twenty identical equilateral triangles, five meeting at every corner.

It only works for one particular rectangle shape. Make them square and the solid is wrong; make them too long and it is wrong the other way. The proportion that works — the one that makes all thirty edges come out the same length — is the golden ratio, and §3.2 shows that it is not chosen for beauty but forced by the requirement. Figure 1 is the construction.

3The Theory

Indexed geometry, and what it saves

Two arrays. The vertex array holds positions in model space; the index array holds whole numbers, in triples:

Two design choices are worth defending explicitly.

Triangles, not edges — even though we are drawing a wireframe. It would be tempting to store an edge list, since edges are what we draw. But a triangle list is what a mesh is: Module 3 fills these same triangles, Lesson 3.4 culls them by winding, Module 4 uploads them. Storing edges would mean throwing away the face information and reconstructing it two lessons later. The cost of choosing triangles now is that a wireframe draws each shared edge twice — 60 line draws for the icosahedron's 30 unique edges. That is real waste, it is 2× here, and it disappears entirely the moment the triangles get filled. We pay it and name it rather than hide it.

Indices are 16-bit. std::uint16_t addresses 65,536 vertices, which is plenty for a mesh of this size and is exactly what GPU index buffers commonly use. Large meshes need 32-bit indices, and choosing between them is a real decision an asset pipeline makes; we take the small one and note the ceiling.

Why phi is forced

Now the derivation. Put three rectangles through the origin, one in each coordinate plane, each of width 2 and height 2h for some height we have not yet chosen. Their corners are all the cyclic permutations of (0, ±1, ±h):

\[ (0,\pm 1,\pm h), \quad (\pm 1,\pm h,0), \quad (\pm h,0,\pm 1) \] the 12 vertices: (0, ±1, ±h), (±1, ±h, 0), (±h, 0, ±1)

That is 4 corners per rectangle × 3 rectangles = 12 points. For this to be a regular icosahedron, every edge must have the same length. There are two kinds of edge, so we get one equation.

Kind one — along a rectangle's short side. The corners (0, 1, h) and (0, −1, h) are joined by the rectangle's width. Their distance is simply 2.

Kind two — between two different rectangles. Take (0, 1, h) and (1, h, 0). Squaring their separation:

\[ d^2 = (0-1)^2 + (1-h)^2 + (h-0)^2 = 1 + 1 - 2h + h^2 + h^2 = 2h^2 - 2h + 2 \] d² = (0-1)² + (1-h)² + (h-0)² = 2h² - 2h + 2

Setting the two kinds equal — d² = 2² = 4 — gives the condition:

\[ 2h^2 - 2h + 2 = 4 \;\Longrightarrow\; h^2 - h - 1 = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; h = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2} = \varphi \] 2h² - 2h + 2 = 4 => h² - h - 1 = 0 => h = (1+√5)/2 = φ

In prose: demanding that all thirty edges be equal produces the equation h² = h + 1, whose positive solution is the golden ratio. φ is not decoration here — it is the unique proportion that makes the solid regular. (That equation, φ² = φ + 1, is the golden ratio's defining property, which is a pleasing place for it to turn up.)

Worked example — the numbers, and the normalisation
  • φ = (1 + √5)/2 = 1.6180340. Check: φ² = 2.6180341 and φ + 1 = 2.6180339. Equal to seven figures. ✓
  • Every vertex is the same distance from the origin: |(0, 1, φ)| = √(1 + φ²) = √3.618034 = 1.9021131.
  • Dividing every coordinate by that puts all twelve on the unit sphere, giving the two constants the code uses: a = 1/1.9021131 = 0.5257311 and b = φ/1.9021131 = 0.8506508.
  • The edge length after normalising is 2 / 1.9021131 = 1.0514622 — and the harness, measuring all thirty edges from the shipped data, reports every one of them as 1.051462. ✓

Normalising to the unit sphere is a deliberate convention: the mesh has a known size (radius 1), so making it bigger or smaller is the transform's job, not the data's. That is Lesson 2.8's separation of "what shape is it" from "how big is it here" honoured in the asset.

Verifying a mesh before you trust it

Sixty hand-typed index numbers is exactly the sort of data that is wrong in one place and gives you a strange-looking hole three lessons later. Mesh data is checkable, and knowing the checks is a genuinely useful engineering skill — asset pipelines run them on every import.

  • Euler's formula. For any closed surface without holes, V − E + F = 2. The icosahedron: 12 − 30 + 20 = 2. ✓ Get an index wrong and this usually breaks, because you have merged or duplicated an edge.
  • Every edge shared by exactly two faces. A closed solid has no boundary, so no edge may belong to one face (a hole) or three (a non-manifold pinch). Collect every edge as an unordered pair and count.
  • Consistent winding. Stronger, and the one that matters for Lesson 3.4: if all faces wind the same way, then each directed edge appears exactly once — face A traverses an edge one way and its neighbour B traverses it the other. A directed edge appearing twice means two adjacent faces disagree about which side is out.
  • Outward winding. For each face, the normal from cross(b − a, c − a) should point away from the mesh's centre — test with a dot product against the face centre. This is what makes back-face culling keep the faces you can see rather than the ones you cannot.
  • Regularity (for this solid). All twelve vertices at the same radius, all thirty edges the same length, every vertex belonging to exactly five faces.
The shipped mesh, checked

The harness runs every check above on the data in mesh.hpp: V=12, E=30, F=20 so V−E+F=2; every edge shared by exactly two faces with consistent winding; all twenty faces wound outward; vertex degree uniformly 5; radius 1.000000 for all twelve; edge length 1.051462 for all thirty. The cube passes the same topology checks (8 − 18 + 12 = 2 — eighteen because triangulating each square face adds a diagonal), though of course its triangles are not equilateral.

The complete chain, one vertex

Here is the payoff of the whole module: one vertex of the icosahedron, carried through every space, with every hop attributable to the lesson that built it. Figure 3 is the pipeline; the worked example below is a real vertex going through it.

Worked example — vertex 0 through all six spaces

The icosahedron is at position (0, 1, 0), uniform scale 0.9, rotated; the camera orbits at radius 7. Vertex 0 of the mesh is (−a, +b, 0) = (−0.5257, +0.8507, 0) — a point on the unit sphere, by construction.

  • model → world (Lesson 2.8, T·R·S): scaled by 0.9, rotated, then moved to (0,1,0) → (−0.77, +1.29, +0.37)
  • world → view (Lesson 2.9, the camera's inverse): → (−0.77, +0.52, −6.42). The z is negative because the camera looks down −z; it is 6.42 units in front of the eye.
  • view → clip (Lesson 2.10, the projection): → (−0.83, +1.00, +6.14, w = 6.42). w is 6.42, which is exactly −z — the projection has stashed the depth for the divide.
  • clip → NDC (Lesson 2.10, the perspective divide): divide all four by w = 6.42 → (−0.130, +0.156, +0.956). In range: x, y ∈ [−1,1] and z ∈ [0,1].
  • NDC → screen (Lesson 2.11, the viewport): → (80.9, 82.5) pixels, depth 0.956. The y of 82.5 is less than the viewport's centre 90 — higher on screen — because ndc.y was positive and the viewport flips y.

Five hops, five lessons, one vertex. Every number above is printed live by the demo's HUD, and every one is reproduced by the verification harness.

4Implementation

The engine gains one header. The mesh type is two views, not two containers:

C++ in place — std::span, a non-owning view

std::span<const T> is C++20's "pointer plus a length": it refers to a contiguous run of elements it does not own. A mesh is therefore four words, copies trivially, and — crucially — cannot outlive the array it points at. That is exactly right for geometry that is static data with program lifetime, and exactly wrong for geometry loaded at runtime, which is why Module 5's asset system introduces handles and real ownership. Treat a span like a reference: perfect for a parameter or a short-lived view, a dangling-pointer bug waiting to happen if you store one whose target dies first.

The related keyword is inline on the data arrays. A constexpr array at namespace scope in a header would be a separate object per translation unit; inline constexpr makes it one object across the whole program, which is what the One Definition Rule requires.

The renderer walks indices three at a time, and the ordering of its two loops is the practical argument for the whole representation:

Twelve matrix multiplications, then sixty cheap lookups — rather than sixty matrix multiplications. On a twelve-vertex mesh that is a rounding error; on a hundred-thousand-triangle character it is the difference between a frame and a slideshow, and it is precisely why GPUs have a post-transform vertex cache. The structure of this loop is the structure Module 4 hands to the hardware.

Finally, the scene grows a notion of "a thing": a transform paired with geometry.

Note what this is not: the mesh did not get bolted onto engine::transform. A transform is a placement, not a thing — and keeping them separate is the shape Module 5's ECS will formalise, where an entity has a transform component and a mesh component, attached independently. Here it is a three-field demo struct, which is the honest amount of machinery for three objects.

7What Is Still Missing

A milestone should be honest about its limits, and this renderer has several — most of them visible on screen right now if you know where to look. Every one is a Module 3 lesson.

You can see through the solid — no depth buffer
What you see
Edges on the far side of the icosahedron are drawn right over the near ones. The brightness cue helps your eye guess, but nothing actually prevents distant geometry from painting over close geometry — draw order is all there is.
Why
We compute a per-vertex depth (the viewport even hands us one) and then throw it away. Nothing compares a pixel's depth against what is already there.
Fixed by
Lesson 3.1 — the z-buffer. Keep a depth per pixel; write a pixel only if it is nearer than what is already there. The lesson opens with exactly this artifact.
Edges vanish near the camera — no clipping
What you see
Nothing, at the shipped camera limits — but drop the dolly clamp and push the camera into the geometry and whole edges wink out instead of being trimmed at the screen edge.
Why
project drops any line with an endpoint at or behind the near plane, because dividing by a w ≤ 0 flings the point to nonsense (Lesson 2.7's warning). That is a crude stand-in: the correct behaviour is to trim the edge where it crosses the plane and keep the visible part.
Fixed by
Lesson 3.3 — near-plane clipping (Sutherland–Hodgman), and why it cannot be skipped.
We draw faces nobody can see — no back-face culling
What you see
All twenty triangles are drawn every frame, though roughly half face away from you. On a wireframe that is the point (you want the far edges dimly visible); on a solid it is pure waste.
Why
Nothing tests which way a triangle faces. The data is ready — every face is wound counter-clockwise seen from outside, and the harness verifies it — but nothing consumes that winding yet.
Fixed by
Lesson 3.4 — back-face culling, where the cross product returns from Lesson 2.9 to produce a surface normal, and winding finally earns its keep.
It is only a wireframe — no filled triangles, no shading, no texture
What you see
Lines. The rasterizer can fill triangles — it has since Lesson 2.2 — but nothing connects the 3-D pipeline to it, and there is no lighting or texturing to make a filled surface look like anything.
Fixed by
Module 3 throughout: filled triangles with correct depth (3.1), perspective-correct interpolation (3.2), normals and Lambert/Blinn-Phong shading (3.6), and texture mapping (3.7).
The mesh is hand-typed — no asset pipeline
What you see
Sixty index numbers in a header. Fine for an icosahedron, impossible for anything an artist made.
Fixed by
Lesson 3.5 — a hand-rolled OBJ loader, which is where meshes start coming from files. (And Module 6 adds glTF, once the OBJ parser has taught the ideas.)

8Common Pitfalls & Debugging

A face is missing or doubled — a hole in a closed solid
Symptom
The wireframe looks nearly right but one triangle is absent, or an edge looks heavier than its neighbours.
Cause
A mistyped index. Sixty hand-entered numbers, and one of them names the wrong vertex — the classic failure of hand-authored geometry.
Fix
Run the checks in §3.3 rather than squinting at the picture: Euler's V − E + F = 2 breaks, or some edge is shared by one face instead of two. Both point straight at the bad triple. This is why asset pipelines validate on import.
Winding is inconsistent — invisible now, fatal in Lesson 3.4
Symptom
Absolutely nothing, today. A wireframe does not care which way a triangle faces. Then you enable back-face culling two lessons later and random triangles disappear.
Cause
One or more faces wound clockwise-from-outside while the rest are counter-clockwise.
Fix
Check it now, while it is cheap: each directed edge must appear exactly once across the whole mesh, and every face's normal must point away from the centre. The harness does both. A latent data error you can detect today is worth far more than a mysterious hole later.
An index runs past the vertex array — garbage geometry or a crash
Symptom
Wild edges shooting off to nowhere, or an outright crash.
Cause
An index larger than vertices.size() - 1 — a typo, or a mesh whose two arrays came from different sources.
Fix
Validate on load: every index must be in range, and the index count must be a multiple of three. draw_mesh bounds-checks defensively because its input is data, which can be wrong in ways code cannot — the same distinction Lesson 2.5 drew between put_pixel (checked; indices come from arithmetic) and at(row, col) (unchecked; indices are literals).
A mesh outlives its data — a dangling span
Symptom
Geometry that renders correctly at first and turns to noise later, or crashes at random.
Cause
std::span does not own. Build a mesh over a local std::vector, return it, and the vector dies while the span still points at its memory.
Fix
Keep spans as views over data that certainly outlives them — here, inline constexpr arrays with program lifetime. The moment meshes are loaded at runtime, something must own them; that is what Module 5's handle-based asset system is for, and the reason it exists.

9Exercises

2.12.1Euler by handwarm-up

The triangulated cube in mesh.hpp has 8 vertices and 12 triangles. Without running anything, work out how many distinct edges it has, and check Euler's formula. Why is the answer not 12?

Hint

Count the edges each triangle contributes (3 × 12 = 36 directed edges), then account for every edge being shared by exactly two faces. Separately: each square face was split by a diagonal.

Solution

36 triangle-edges ÷ 2 (each shared) = 18 distinct edges. Euler: 8 − 18 + 12 = 2 ✓. It is not 12 because triangulating each of the 6 square faces adds one diagonal: 12 + 6 = 18. Those diagonals are real edges of the triangle mesh even though they are not edges of the cube — which is exactly why the wireframe shows a diagonal across every face, and a good reminder that a triangulated model is not quite the shape you drew.

2.12.2Derive another solidguided

Add an octahedron to mesh.hpp: 6 vertices, 8 triangular faces. Its vertices are the simplest of any Platonic solid — work them out yourself, wind all eight faces counter-clockwise from outside, and run the §3.3 checks on your data.

Hint

The octahedron's vertices are the six unit points on the axes: (±1,0,0), (0,±1,0), (0,0,±1). Each face joins one vertex from each axis pair, so there are 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 faces. Getting the winding right on all eight is the actual exercise — check each with the normal-versus-centroid test before you trust it.

Solution sketch

Euler: 6 − 12 + 8 = 2 ✓. The satisfying part is that the winding test catches your mistakes mechanically — flip a face and the dot product against the centroid goes negative, so you never have to eyeball it. That habit is the transferable skill: hand-authored geometry is data, and data gets validated, not trusted.

2.12.3Subdivide into a geodesic spheremedium

An icosahedron is the seed of the classic icosphere. Subdivide: for each triangle, compute the midpoint of each edge, normalise it back onto the unit sphere, and replace the triangle with four smaller ones. Do it once (80 faces), then twice (320). Verify Euler's formula still holds at every level.

Hint

The trap is duplicate vertices: two adjacent triangles share an edge, so the midpoint of that edge would be created twice. Cache them in a map keyed by the sorted pair of the two endpoint indices, so each midpoint gets one index. Without that cache, vertex sharing collapses and you are back to storing everything unshared.

Solution sketch

Each subdivision multiplies faces by 4 and adds one vertex per edge. Starting from 12/30/20: level 1 is 42/120/80, level 2 is 162/480/320 — and V − E + F = 2 at every level. Normalising the midpoints is what makes it a sphere rather than a faceted lump; without it you simply get a finer icosahedron. This is how essentially every engine generates sphere meshes, and the midpoint cache is the whole trick.

2.12.4Stop drawing edges twicemedium

Our wireframe draws each shared edge twice — 60 line draws for 30 edges. Build a deduplicated edge list from the index array (each undirected pair once) and draw from that. Measure the difference with the frame timer.

Hint

Collect each triangle's three edges as sorted index pairs into a std::set or a sorted-then-unique vector. Build it once, not per frame — building it every frame would cost more than the drawing it saves, which is itself the lesson.

Solution sketch

You will halve the line draws and find the frame time barely moves, because at 132 lines the renderer is nowhere near the bottleneck. That is the honest result and worth sitting with: the optimisation is correct and currently pointless, and knowing which is which is what Module 3's profiling lesson is about — measure, then optimise. Note also that the whole problem evaporates once triangles are filled, so the best answer may be "do nothing".

2.12.5Try to fix depth without a z-bufferopen

The far edges drawing over the near ones is the most visible flaw. Try to fix it the old way: sort the triangles back-to-front by their average view-space depth and draw in that order — the painter's algorithm. Get it working, then find a case where it fails.

Solution sketch

Sorting by average depth works for the icosahedron and will convince you the problem is solved. It is not. Three failures break it, and finding one yourself is the exercise: intersecting triangles have no correct order at all; overlapping triangles can form a cycle (A in front of B in front of C in front of A) that no sort can resolve; and a long triangle stretching in depth has an "average" that describes none of it. The painter's algorithm also costs a sort every frame and gets worse as scenes grow. This is precisely the argument Lesson 3.1 makes before introducing the z-buffer, which decides depth per pixel and sidesteps every one of these cases. Doing the doomed version first is the fastest way to understand why the z-buffer won.

10Recap & Next

Module 2 is complete

  • Indexed geometry. A mesh is a vertex array plus triangle indices — the layout that shares vertices, transforms each once, and is exactly what the GPU wants in Module 4.
  • A mesh worth drawing, derived. A regular icosahedron from three golden rectangles, with φ forced by the demand that all thirty edges be equal — and verified by Euler's formula, edge sharing, uniform degree, and outward winding.
  • The whole chain, working. model → world → view → clip → NDC → screen, every hop derived in its own lesson (2.8, 2.9, 2.10, 2.10, 2.11), all of it running on real geometry with a camera you fly.
  • An honest inventory. No depth buffer, no clipping, no culling, no fills, no asset pipeline — each one named, each one visible, each one assigned to the Module 3 lesson that fixes it.

Next: 3.1 — The Painter's Problem and the Z-Buffer. Module 3 begins by attacking the flaw you can see most clearly right now: far geometry painting over near geometry. We will try the obvious fix first — sorting back to front — watch it fail on cases it genuinely cannot handle, and then build the per-pixel depth buffer that every renderer since has used. From there Module 3 fills these triangles in: perspective-correct interpolation, clipping, culling, an OBJ loader, normals and light, and textures — the whole surface of a rendered image, built on the geometry you have just finished.
