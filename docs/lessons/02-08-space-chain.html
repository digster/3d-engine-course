Module 2 — Software Rasterizer I · Lesson 2.8

The Space Chain: Model to World

The machinery is finished. mat4 can express any affine transform, and w says whether a thing is a place or a direction. Today we stop building machinery and start using it — and the subject turns out to be a discipline rather than a formula. Nearly every transform bug in graphics is a coordinate used in the wrong space, the compiler cannot help you, and the fix is almost embarrassingly cheap: name the space, every time, in the variable.

A scene: a ground plane, a world origin, and three objects that share one eight-vertex mesh and differ only in their transform. One keystroke rebuilds every model matrix in the wrong order, and you can watch precisely which objects break and which ones stay perfect.

By the end of this lesson you will be able to…

  • …say what space a coordinate is in, and explain why nothing in the type system can tell you.
  • …name matrices so that a wrong composition looks wrong on the page, before you run it.
  • …derive the order T · R · S from three sentences about what scale, rotation and translation each mean — rather than memorising it.
  • …predict, and then verify on screen, the two distinct failure modes of the two wrong orders: one deforms the object, the other misplaces it.
  • …read a model matrix column by column and say where the object is, which way it faces, and how big it is — without multiplying anything.
  • …explain why getting the order wrong leaves most of a scene looking perfect, and why that makes it a bug that ships.

1The Problem

Here is the demo's cube, as it has stood since Lesson 2.6:

Now answer a question about it that sounds trivial and is not: where is this cube?

You cannot answer, and neither can the compiler. The array holds twenty-four floats. It does not hold a place. Half a unit from what? Those numbers are only meaningful relative to some origin, along some axes, and nothing in the file says which origin or whose axes. Up to now that did not bite, because there was exactly one object and we quietly agreed that its origin and the world's origin were the same point. One object cannot be in the wrong place relative to nothing.

Add a second object and the agreement collapses. Lesson 2.7's demo already showed the strain: it drew two cubes, and to do it, it hand-built two matrices with a hard-coded offset called place and composed them in two different orders to make a point. That works for two. It does not work for a hundred, and it certainly does not work when an artist wants a crate that is wider than it is tall.

So let us try to add exactly that: a slab — a cube scaled to (1.8, 0.35, 0.9) — standing to the left of the world origin and slowly turning. Scale, rotate, translate. Three matrices, and six ways to multiply them together. Pick one.

The failure mode — a solid object that breathes

Compose it as T · S · R and this is what you get. At θ = 0° the slab is a perfect 1.8-by-0.35 box, exactly as intended. Nudge the rotation and it starts to lean — not turn, lean, like a stack of paper pushed sideways. Its long edge shortens as it rises. At θ = 45° the box has become a parallelogram whose corners are 157.99° and 22.01° instead of ninety, and its long edge has shrunk from 1.8 units to 1.2966. Keep turning and at θ = 90° everything snaps back: right angles, straight edges, a perfect box again — a box 0.35 long instead of 1.8.

A rigid object cannot do that. Rotation does not change lengths and does not change angles; whatever is happening here, the object is not being rotated, it is being squashed by something that is not turning with it. Hold that phrasing — it is almost the whole diagnosis.

And now the part that makes this worth a lesson rather than a footnote. In the same scene, with the same broken code, put a second object next to it: a uniformly scaled post, spinning at the same rate. It is flawless. Not approximately flawless — the two matrices agree to the last bit, at every angle. Put a third object down, a non-uniform plinth that does not rotate. Also flawless.

Two objects out of three look perfect. The broken one only looks broken while it is between axis-aligned angles — it is exactly right at 0° and exactly right at 90°, which are the two orientations anybody types in while testing. This is not a bug that announces itself. It is a bug that lurks in a corner of a scene and gets blamed on the artist's mesh for a week.

We could fix it by memorising an order. Instead we are going to derive it, because the derivation also hands us the naming discipline that stops the whole family of bugs — including the ones we have not met yet, in Lesson 2.9's view matrix and Lesson 2.10's projection, where the same mistake is far harder to see.

2Building Intuition

Two rooms: the workshop and the warehouse

Think about how a real crate gets made. Someone builds it in a workshop, on a bench, and while they are working, the natural way to describe any part of it is relative to the crate itself: the lid is 40 cm above the base, the handle is centred on the front face. Nobody designing a crate says "the lid is 12.4 metres from the north wall of the warehouse", because at that moment there is no warehouse and there might eventually be four hundred warehouses.

Then the crate is finished, and it goes somewhere. Now the warehouse coordinates matter — and there are four hundred crates, each with its own answer, all built from the same drawing.

Those are our two spaces, and the analogy is exact rather than decorative.

  • Model space (also called object or local space) is the workshop: the coordinates a mesh was authored in, centred on the object's own origin, aligned with the object's own axes. k_cube_v above is model space, and its numbers are "half a unit from the cube's own centre".
  • World space is the warehouse: one shared frame that everything in the scene agrees on. One origin, one set of axes, one metre. Every object's position, every light, every camera is expressed here — which is precisely what makes it possible for them to be related to one another at all.

The bridge between them is a single mat4, and it has a name that is worth taking seriously from the first time you write it: the model matrix, or better, world_from_model. Figure 1 shows what it buys — one mesh, three objects.

A coordinate is three floats and a room

Here is the uncomfortable fact that makes this lesson necessary. Write down a position:

Is that in model space or world space? There is no answer. The type is vec3 in both cases, the bytes are identical in both cases, and every operation the maths library offers will happily accept it either way. You can add a model-space position to a world-space one and get a number. The number is meaningless, the program does not crash, and the picture is slightly wrong — which is the worst kind of wrong, because slightly wrong looks like a tuning problem rather than a category error.

Lesson 2.7 solved the neighbouring problem — position versus direction — by putting the distinction inside the value, in w. That was possible because the difference genuinely changes the arithmetic. This one cannot be solved that way. Whether a coordinate is in model space or world space changes nothing about how it is multiplied; it changes only what the answer means. There is no fourth component that can carry it.

Could the type system do it?

Yes, in principle — you can make vec3<World> and vec3<Model> distinct types with a template parameter and let the compiler reject the mismatch. Some codebases do exactly this and are happy with it. We are not doing it, for the same reason we did not make position and direction separate types in 2.7: the tag has to be threaded through every function signature in the maths library, it multiplies the surface area several times over, and it evaporates the moment the data crosses into a shader, where the GPU sees sixteen bytes and no types at all. Exercise 2.8.5 asks you to argue the other side.

What is left, then, is naming — and naming turns out to be startlingly effective, because the failure it prevents is a failure of bookkeeping, and bookkeeping is exactly what a good name does for free.

The chain we are about to walk

Model and world are the first two links of a chain that Module 2 spends the rest of its lessons completing. It is worth seeing the whole thing now, once, so that every later lesson has a slot to drop into rather than arriving as a surprise. Figure 2 is that chain, and it carries a real vertex through it with real numbers — the same vertex we will push through by hand in §3.3.

Notice something about the labels under the arrows in Figure 2. They are not called model, view and projection, which is what most tutorials (and, honestly, most engines) call them. They are called world_from_model, view_from_world, clip_from_view. That choice is the subject of the next section, and it is doing more work than it looks like.

3The Theory

Naming the rooms: a_from_b

Recall the one rule of composition from Lesson 2.5 §3.6: in A * B, B is applied first. It reads right to left. That rule is correct, it is forced by the algebra, and it is also the single most common thing to get backwards, because English reads the other way.

So name matrices to read right to left too. A matrix that takes coordinates from space b and produces coordinates in space a is called a_from_b. Then this happens:

\[ M_{\text{view}\leftarrow\text{model}} \;=\; M_{\text{view}\leftarrow\text{world}} \cdot M_{\text{world}\leftarrow\text{model}} \] view_from_model = view_from_world * world_from_model

Read the middle of that product: …world meets world…. The two inner labels match, and the two outer labels are exactly the name of the result. It is the same cancellation you do with units when you multiply metres per second by seconds and get metres. Write the product the other way round and it reads world_from_model * view_from_world — model meets view, which is nonsense you can see without running anything.

Why this is worth the extra characters

A wrongly-ordered matrix product is one of the hardest bugs in graphics to find by looking at the picture, because the result is usually plausible: things are on screen, they move when you move the camera, and everything is subtly, unfixably off. With this convention the bug is a spelling mistake — visible on the page, in the diff, before the program has run. That is the whole return, and it costs nothing but typing.

Apply the same discipline to the values, not just the matrices. A vertex is v_model before the multiply and v_world after it, and then this line is self-checking:

The matrix's right-hand label must match the argument's suffix, and its left-hand label must match the result's. Two agreements to check, both visible in one line, neither requiring you to remember anything. If you take one habit away from this lesson, take this one — it will still be paying off in Module 6, when a normal has to be transformed by an inverse-transpose and there are five spaces in flight at once.

Deriving the order of T, R and S

Now the question the demo failed on. We have three transformations to apply to the mesh — a scale S, a rotation R, and a translation T — and six possible products. Rather than pick one and check, let us state what each transformation means and let the meanings decide.

Sentence one: scale is measured along the object's own axes.

When an artist says the crate is twice as wide, they mean twice as wide across the crate — along its own left-right direction, not along the world's x axis. That is a statement about model space. The only moment at which the coordinates are still in model space is before anything else has happened to them. Therefore S must act first, which puts it rightmost in the product.

Sentence two: rotation is about the object's own origin.

Lesson 2.5 §3.8 established that every linear transformation fixes the origin — a rotation matrix turns space about the point (0,0,0) and has no other option. We want the crate to spin about its own centre, and its own centre is at the origin only while it is still in model space. So R must act before the object is moved away from the origin. That puts R to the left of S and to the right of T.

Sentence three: translation is where the finished object goes.

Placing the object is the last thing that happens to it, because it is the only one of the three that is a statement about the world rather than about the object. So T acts last, which puts it leftmost.

\[ M_{\text{world}\leftarrow\text{model}} \;=\; T \cdot R \cdot S \] world_from_model = T * R * S (S applied first, T applied last)

In prose, because the equation must never be the only carrier of the fact: the model matrix is translation times rotation times scale, and because the rightmost factor is applied first, that means scale happens first, then rotation, then translation. The order the product is written is the reverse of the order things happen, which is exactly the trap Lesson 2.5 warned about and exactly why we bothered with the naming rule.

A worked example, by hand

Take a concrete object and a concrete vertex and push them through. This is the vertex from Figure 2, and the arithmetic below is what the harness in the repository verifies.

Worked example — one vertex, model space to world space

The object: scale = (2, 0.5, 1), rotation = rotation_y(90°), position = (3, 1, −4). The vertex: v_model = (0.5, 0.5, −0.5), a corner of the unit cube.

Step 1 — scale, along the object's own axes. A scale matrix is diagonal, so this is just three multiplications:

  • x: 0.5 × 2 = 1.0
  • y: 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25
  • z: −0.5 × 1 = −0.5

giving (1.0, 0.25, −0.5). The vertex has moved, but only because the whole object got wider — it is still described in the object's own frame.

Step 2 — rotate 90° about y. First, what does that matrix do? Lesson 2.6's rotation_y turns z toward x, so with cos 90° = 0 and sin 90° = 1 its columns — the images of the basis vectors — are:

  • x̂ = (1,0,0) → (0, 0, −1)  (the model's x axis now points along world −z)
  • ŷ = (0,1,0) → (0, 1, 0)  (the axis of rotation; unmoved)
  • ẑ = (0,0,1) → (1, 0, 0)  (the model's z axis now points along world +x)

Applying a matrix is "x of the first column, plus y of the second, plus z of the third" (Lesson 2.5 §3.2):

  • 1.0 · (0,0,−1) = (0, 0, −1)
  • 0.25 · (0,1,0) = (0, 0.25, 0)
  • −0.5 · (1,0,0) = (−0.5, 0, 0)
  • sum: (−0.5, 0.25, −1.0)

Step 3 — translate by (3, 1, −4). Add it:

  • (−0.5 + 3, 0.25 + 1, −1.0 − 4) = (2.5, 1.25, −5.0)

So v_world = (2.5, 1.25, −5.0). That is the number in Figure 2, and if you build the single matrix T·R·S and multiply once, you get the same answer to the last bit — which is the point of having a single matrix at all.

An honest note about cos 90°

The hand-calculation above uses cos 90° = 0 exactly, and the machine does not. In float, std::cos(π/2) comes out as −4.371139e−08, because π/2 itself is not exactly representable. It does not matter here — the final coordinates still print as 2.500000, 1.250000, −5.000000 — but it is worth knowing that the zeros in a rotation matrix are usually almost zeros. It is why comparing matrices for exact equality is a bad habit, and why Lesson 3.1's depth comparisons will need to think about tolerance.

Why the wrong order deforms, exactly

Now we can say precisely what went wrong in §1, and prove it rather than assert it.

The claim is that T·R·S moves the object and T·S·R deforms it. A useful test for "deformed" is this: the model's x and y axes are perpendicular by construction. If a transformation leaves them perpendicular, and leaves their lengths equal to the object's size, then the object has been moved and resized but its shape is intact. If it does not, the object has been sheared.

Send the model's x and y axes through R·S, remembering that S goes first and that S sends x̂ to (sx, 0, 0):

\[ (R S)\hat{x} = R(s_x \hat{x}) = s_x R\hat{x}, \qquad (R S)\hat{y} = s_y R\hat{y} \] (R*S) * x_hat = sx * (R * x_hat) and (R*S) * y_hat = sy * (R * y_hat)

In words: under the correct order, the model's x axis comes out as the rotated x axis, scaled by sx — and likewise for y and z. The rotation is applied to vectors that are already perpendicular, and a rotation preserves both lengths and angles. So the two come out perpendicular, with lengths exactly sx and sy, at every angle. The object is rigid. Its dot product is sx·sy·(R x̂)·(R ŷ) = sx·sy·0 = 0, forever.

Now the wrong order, S·R, where the rotation happens first and the scale is applied afterwards along the world's axes. Take the slab's S = (1.8, 0.35, 0.9) and a rotation of 45° about z:

Worked example — the shear, in numbers

With cos 45° = sin 45° = 0.70711, rotating first:

  • x̂ → R → (0.70711, 0.70711, 0) → S → (1.27279, 0.24749, 0)
  • ŷ → R → (−0.70711, 0.70711, 0) → S → (−1.27279, 0.24749, 0)

Their dot product is no longer zero:

  • (1.27279)(−1.27279) + (0.24749)(0.24749) = −1.61999 + 0.06125 = −1.55874
  • each has length sqrt(1.61999 + 0.06125) = sqrt(1.68124) = 1.29663
  • cos θ = −1.55874 / (1.29663 × 1.29663) = −0.92714
  • θ = 157.99°

A right angle of the mesh has opened to 157.99°, and the model's x axis — which should be 1.8 units long, because that is what sx means — measures 1.29663. The object is not a box any more. That is the leaning stack of paper from §1, quantified.

The mechanism, stated plainly: a non-uniform scale is only meaningful relative to a set of axes. Apply it before the rotation and those axes are the object's own, which is what "1.8 units wide" was always supposed to mean. Apply it after, and the axes are the world's — so the object gets squeezed by a fixed, non-rotating press while it turns inside it. Figure 4 puts the two side by side.

The columns are the object's frame

The algebra in §3.4 quietly handed us something bigger than a proof. Go back to it: (R·S) x̂ = sx · (R x̂). The left-hand side is the first column of the matrix, because Lesson 2.5's founding observation was that a matrix's columns are exactly where the basis vectors land. So:

\[ M_{\text{world}\leftarrow\text{model}} = \begin{bmatrix} s_x R\hat{x} & s_y R\hat{y} & s_z R\hat{z} & \mathbf{t} \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \] columns of world_from_model = [ sx*(R x_hat) | sy*(R y_hat) | sz*(R z_hat) | position ]

Stated in prose, and this is worth committing to memory because it turns debugging from arithmetic into reading: column 0 of a model matrix is the object's own x axis expressed in world space, times its x size; columns 1 and 2 are the same for y and z; and column 3 is the object's origin in world space — which is simply its position.

Look back at the worked example's matrix. Its columns are (0, 0, −2), (0, 0.5, 0), (1, 0, 0), and (3, 1, −4). Without multiplying anything you can now read off: the object sits at (3, 1, −4); its own x axis points along world −z and is 2 units long; its y axis is still world up and is half a unit long; its z axis points along world +x at unit length. That is a complete description of a placed object, obtained by looking. Figure 5 draws the correspondence.

The other wrong order: translate too early

T·S·R deforms. The other common mistake, R·T·S, does something quite different — and distinguishing the two by their symptoms is a genuinely useful debugging skill, because they get confused constantly.

In R·T·S the translation is applied before the rotation. The object is scaled correctly, moved to its place correctly — and then the rotation turns everything, including the displacement, about the world origin. The object is not deformed at all: its angles are still 90.000°, its x axis still measures exactly sx. It is simply in the wrong place, swung around the origin like a stone on a string.

Worked example — the orbit, in numbers

Same object as §3.3: position = (3, 1, −4), rotation_y(90°). Where does R·T·S put its origin?

The object's own origin is (0,0,0) in model space. S leaves it alone (a scale fixes the origin), T moves it to (3, 1, −4), and then R rotates that:

  • 3 · (0,0,−1) = (0, 0, −3)
  • 1 · (0,1,0) = (0, 1, 0)
  • −4 · (1,0,0) = (−4, 0, 0)
  • sum: (−4, 1, −3)

The correct answer was (3, 1, −4). Both are sqrt(9 + 1 + 16) = 5.0990 units from the world origin — the object has kept its distance and lost its place. That is the signature: an object that swings in a circle around the world origin at the same rate it should have been spinning.

This is Exercise 2.5.3, answered

Lesson 2.5 asked how to rotate a shape about a point that is not the origin, and the answer was "move the pivot to the origin, rotate, move it back" — carried out by hand because there was no matrix that could translate. Lesson 2.7 gave us that matrix. What §3.6 is describing is the same machinery viewed from the other side: T·R·S is "rotate at the origin, then move", and R·T·S is "move, then rotate about the origin". Neither is more fundamental. They are different transformations, and picking between them is picking which point the object turns about.

Which means R·T·S is not simply "wrong". It is exactly what you want when the object should orbit — a moon around a planet, a door about its hinge. Lesson 2.7's demo used it deliberately for that reason. The bug is not the order; the bug is using an orbit when you meant a spin.

When the order stops mattering — and why that is the danger

We should be honest about the boundaries of the claim, because the boundaries are where the real-world trouble lives.

If the scale is uniform, T·R·S and T·S·R are the same matrix. Not close — identical. A uniform scale is sI for a single number s, and a scalar multiple of the identity commutes with everything: R(sI) = sR = (sI)R. Sweeping the demo's uniformly-scaled post through a full 360°, one degree at a time, and comparing all sixteen matrix elements, the largest difference is 0.000e+00 — bit-for-bit equality, not agreement to some tolerance.

If the rotation is the identity, likewise. The demo's plinth has a non-uniform scale and no rotation, and every order that keeps T on the left produces the same matrix, exactly.

Why that makes this bug worse, not better

A scene is mostly uniform scales and mostly axis-aligned props. So a codebase with the order wrong renders almost everything perfectly, and gets caught only by the one rotating, non-uniformly-scaled object somebody added in week nine. By then the wrong order is in the middle of a working renderer and the obvious suspect is the new asset.

This is the general shape of the nastiest graphics bugs, and it is worth naming now because it recurs for the rest of the course: a transform error that is invisible in the degenerate cases and wrong everywhere else. The defence is not more testing of the easy cases — it is deriving the thing once and writing it down where it cannot be re-derived wrongly, which is exactly what parent_from_local() is for.

4Implementation

The theory asks for very little code, which is a good sign. We need a place to keep the three authored quantities, and one function that assembles them in the derived order.

Three fields, in the order a person thinks about them rather than the order they are applied — which is a deliberate choice and worth a sentence. The struct is an authoring interface: it is what a level editor edits and what a designer types. parent_from_local() is the only place that has to know about application order, and confining that knowledge to one function is the entire point of having the type.

Note the default values. scale defaults to (1,1,1), not (0,0,0) — a default-constructed transform has to be the transform that does nothing, and for a scale that is one, not zero. A transform whose scale defaulted to zero would collapse every object to a point, and it would do it silently, because a degenerate matrix is still a matrix.

C++ in place — default member initialisers and aggregates

Every member above has a default member initialiser, and the struct still has no constructors. That combination is intentional and it is the same one vec3 and mat3 use. It buys three things at once: a default-constructed transform is always valid (never uninitialised floats); the type stays an aggregate, so brace initialisation like transform{{1,0,0}, mat3::identity(), {2,2,2}} keeps working; and the memory layout is guaranteed to be exactly what it looks like, which will matter in Module 4 when structures like this are uploaded to the GPU as raw bytes.

Adding even one constructor would silently take the second and third of those away. That is not a rule to memorise — it is a consequence of what "aggregate" means in the standard, and it is why this course's small value types consistently have none.

The name deserves its own paragraph. Why parent_from_local and not world_from_local, when in Module 2 the parent is the world?

Naming for the code you are going to write

Because Module 5 adds a transform hierarchy, where an object's transform is relative to another object rather than to the world — a turret on a tank, a hand on an arm. When that lands, this function does not change by a single character; only the meaning of "parent" widens. Had we called it world_from_local we would have to either rename it (churning every call site) or leave a name that lies.

This is not speculative generality — we are not adding a parent pointer today, or any machinery at all for a hierarchy we do not have. We are only declining to hard-code an assumption we already know to be temporary, which costs nothing.

Building a scene rather than an object

The demo needs three things it did not have: more than one object, a visible world, and a way to get the order wrong on purpose.

The objects come first, and the striking part is how little there is to them — the entire difference between a slab, a post and a plinth is nine floats each:

Two of those three are controls, chosen to make §3.7's point impossible to miss rather than to fill the screen. The post has a uniform scale, so the order cannot affect it; the plinth has no rotation, so likewise. Set the order wrong and two thirds of the scene stays flawless.

The transforms are rebuilt from scratch every frame, from one authoritative angle, rather than accumulated:

Why not rotation = rotation * small_delta each frame?

Because it drifts. Every product introduces rounding, the columns slowly stop being unit length and stop being perpendicular, and after a few minutes the "rotation" is quietly a rotation-plus-a-small-shear — the same artifact §3.4 just diagnosed, arriving by a completely different route and much harder to spot because it creeps.

Deriving the whole matrix from a single accumulated scalar cannot drift that way: a float angle accumulates error in the angle, which shows up as the object being at a slightly different rotation than you expected, not as the object ceasing to be rigid. Storing the inputs and deriving the matrix is the pattern the engine keeps — Module 5's transform component stores position, rotation and scale, never a running matrix.

The visible world matters more than it sounds. Until this lesson the demo drew one object at the origin, which made model space and world space indistinguishable on screen — same origin, same axes, nothing to tell them apart. A ground grid and a world triad make world space a place you can see, and they are what turns "the object is at (−1.4, 1, 0.2)" from a claim into something you can check with your eyes.

That forced one change to the projection. Lesson 2.6's to_screen3 simply dropped z, which collapses the entire ground plane onto a single horizontal line:

+z now leans down and to the left, matching the course convention that +z points toward the viewer. This is still an orthographic projection — specifically an oblique one, a cabinet projection — so parallel edges stay parallel and distant things are not smaller. It is a stopgap with a known expiry date: Lesson 2.10 derives real perspective from similar triangles and these two constants go away. Saying so is not a disclaimer; it is the ninety-percent picture, and the missing ten percent has a lesson number.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Objects shear as they rotate — boxes turn into parallelograms
Symptom
Non-uniformly scaled objects lean and stretch as they turn, worst at 45°, correct at 0° and 90°. Uniformly scaled objects are fine.
Cause
The scale is being applied after the rotation — T·S·R — so it acts along world axes instead of the object's. §3.4.
Fix
Build the matrix as T·R·S. Confirm it with the measurement rather than the picture: transform x̂ and ŷ as directions and check their dot product is zero and their lengths are sx and sy. If it is, the object is rigid, whatever it looks like through the projection.
Everything orbits the origin — objects swing instead of spinning
Symptom
Objects keep their shape perfectly but circle the world origin. Objects at the origin look completely correct, which is exactly why this often gets found late.
Cause
The translation is applied before the rotation — R·T·S — so the rotation turns the displacement as well as the object. §3.6.
Fix
Move T to the left of R. To confirm before you look at the picture: column 3 of the model matrix must equal the object's position exactly. If it does not, the translation is being rotated.
The whole scene is scaled and rotated together — objects move when others are edited
Symptom
Changing one object's transform appears to move a different object, or every object shares one orientation.
Cause
One model matrix built outside the per-object loop and reused, or a transform captured by reference and mutated. The giveaway is that all objects agree with each other — a shared matrix cannot produce disagreement.
Fix
Build world_from_model inside the loop, from that object's own transform. In the demo it is a const local declared in the loop body, which makes accidental reuse a compile error rather than a rendering mystery.
Scale of zero, or a negative scale — the object vanishes, or renders inside out
Symptom
An object disappears entirely, or looks correct but is somehow "wrong side out" — a problem you cannot yet see, but will from Lesson 3.4 onward.
Cause
A zero component in scale flattens the object onto a plane: the determinant of the linear part is sx·sy·sz = 0, space has been collapsed, and there is no inverse (Lesson 2.6 §3.5). A negative component makes the determinant negative, which reverses handedness — the object is mirrored, and its triangle winding is reversed with it.
Fix
For the zero case: a default transform has scale = (1,1,1) for exactly this reason, so suspect a struct that was zero-initialised or memset. For the negative case: it is legitimate — mirroring a prop is a real thing to want — but it must be paired with reversed winding, which is Lesson 3.4's subject. Watch for it now so it is not a mystery then.
Normals point the wrong way after a non-uniform scale — lighting is subtly wrong on stretched objects
Symptom
Nothing you can see yet. Flagged here because this lesson is where the cause is introduced, and Module 3 is where it starts producing wrong pictures.
Cause
A surface normal is a direction, so w = 0 keeps it from being translated (2.7) — but that is not enough. Under a non-uniform scale, a vector perpendicular to a surface does not stay perpendicular to it after the transform. Stretch a sloped roof horizontally and its normal has to tilt the other way; the model matrix tilts it the same way.
Fix
Normals need the inverse transpose of the model matrix's linear part, not the matrix itself. Derived properly in Lesson 3.6 when there is lighting to make it visible. Note the escape: for a rotation with a uniform scale, the inverse transpose is the matrix up to a scale factor, so the bug hides in exactly the same cases §3.7 described.
A matrix that "looks right" but places things slightly off — everything is plausible and nothing lines up
Symptom
The scene renders, objects move sensibly, but positions are consistently a little wrong and no single value fixes it.
Cause
Almost always a coordinate used in the wrong space — a world-space position handed to something expecting model space, or two matrices composed in the wrong order.
Fix
Write out the product with a_from_b names and check that adjacent labels match (§3.1). Then check one known point by hand: the object's own origin (0,0,0) must land exactly on its position, and that is column 3 of the matrix — no multiplication required. If column 3 is right and the shape is wrong, the fault is in the linear part; if column 3 is wrong, it is in the translation's placement in the product.

8Exercises

2.8.1Read a matrix without multiplyingwarm-up

Without running anything, describe the object this model matrix places — where it is, which way its own axes point in world space, and how big it is along each of them:

Then say whether this transform is rigid, and how you know from the numbers alone.

Hint

The columns are what matter, and the matrix is written rows-across, so column 0 reads downwards: (0, 0, −1). Its length is the scale along that axis.

Solution

Column 3 is (5, 0.5, −2), so the object is at (5, 0.5, −2) in world space. Column 0 is (0, 0, −1): the object's own x axis points along world −z, and its length is 1, so sx = 1. Column 1 is (0, 2, 0): its y axis is world up, and sy = 2. Column 2 is (3, 0, 0): its z axis points along world +x, and sz = 3.

It is not rigid — it scales by different amounts on different axes (1, 2 and 3) — but it is not sheared either: the three columns are mutually perpendicular (every pairwise dot product is zero), so a box goes in and a box comes out. That is exactly what a correctly-ordered T·R·S with a non-uniform scale looks like, and it is the check the demo's HUD is performing.

2.8.2Make the plinth reveal the bugguided

The plinth has a non-uniform scale but no rotation, so it is immune to the T·S·R error. Give it a fixed rotation — not animated, just a constant 30° about z — and predict, before you build, what the HUD's corner reading will be under the wrong order. Then check.

Hint

Do the §3.4 arithmetic with sx = 1.2, sy = 0.25 and θ = 30°. Transform x̂ and ŷ by R first, then multiply componentwise by the scale, then take the dot product and divide by the two lengths.

Solution

With cos 30° = 0.86603 and sin 30° = 0.5: x̂ → (0.86603, 0.5, 0) → (1.03923, 0.125, 0) and ŷ → (−0.5, 0.86603, 0) → (−0.6, 0.21651, 0). The dot product is −0.62354 + 0.02706 = −0.59647; the lengths are 1.04673 and 0.63788; so cos θ = −0.89344 and the corner opens to about 153.3°.

The real lesson is the one you get by then setting the rotation back to identity and watching the reading snap back to exactly 90°. A single line of asset data decided whether this bug was visible.

2.8.3Add local_from_parentmedium

transform.hpp deliberately has no inverse. Write one. The general 4×4 inversion is a substantial piece of code, but this particular shape is not: undoing T·R·S is S⁻¹ · Rᵀ · T⁻¹, and every factor there is cheap. Derive why each of the three is what it is, implement it, and verify that local_from_parent(t) * parent_from_local(t) is the identity to within a small tolerance for a few hundred random transforms.

Hint

Two facts do the work. A rotation's inverse is its transpose (Lesson 2.5 §3.7). A scale's inverse is the reciprocals of its components — which is precisely why the function must refuse, or at least flag, a zero component. For the order: reversing a composition reverses the factors, because to undo "S then R then T" you must undo T first.

On tolerance: do not test for exact equality. §3.3's note on cos 90° explains why. Compare each element against the identity's with an epsilon of around 1e-5.

Solution sketch

Build the linear part as transpose(rotation) with row k divided by scale[k] — that composes S⁻¹·Rᵀ in one pass — and the translation as that linear part applied to −position. Then affine(linear, offset). The whole thing is about six lines and needs no determinant.

The design question worth weighing is what to do about a zero scale. Returning a zero matrix (the choice mat3::inverse and normalised() already make) keeps the failure a value you can test rather than a NaN that spreads silently through the frame; that consistency is worth more than cleverness here. Lesson 2.9 needs exactly this shape for the view matrix, so a version you trust is not wasted work.

2.8.4Two levels of hierarchymedium

Give the post a child: a small cube whose transform is expressed relative to the post — say position = (0, 1.2, 0), sitting above it. Compute its world matrix as world_from_post * post_from_child and draw it. Then rotate the post and confirm the child rides along, orbiting the post rather than the world origin.

Now scale the post non-uniformly and describe what happens to the child. Is it what you want?

Hint

No new code is needed for the first half — parent_from_local already returns the right matrix for both, and mat4's operator* composes them. The naming rule from §3.1 tells you which way round the product goes.

Solution sketch

The composition works immediately, which is the point: hierarchy is not a new mechanism, it is the same matrices multiplied one more time. The bottom row stays (0,0,0,1) through the product, as Lesson 2.7 §3.3 proved it must.

The non-uniform scale is where it gets interesting, and there is no single right answer. The parent's scale propagates into the child's world matrix, so a post squashed flat squashes its child too — and if the child is rotated relative to the parent, that inherited scale is being applied along the parent's axes, not the child's, which is §3.4's shear arriving through the back door. Real engines split on this: some propagate scale and accept the shear, some propagate only uniform scale, some (Unity, notably) detect the case and warn about it. Module 5 has to pick one and defend it; noticing the problem now is the whole exercise.

2.8.5Make the compiler check the spaceopen

§2.2 argued that naming is the practical defence against space confusion, and mentioned the alternative: make the space part of the type, so that adding a model-space position to a world-space one will not compile. Sketch that design — a vec3<Space>, or a wrapper — and work out honestly what it costs. Then argue for or against adopting it in this engine.

Solution sketch

A good answer engages with the real costs rather than declaring a winner. Every operation must state which space combinations it accepts, and some of the answers are fiddly: a matrix's type has to carry two space tags, and a_from_b * b_from_c needs the compiler to know that the inner tags match — which is achievable and is, in effect, the naming rule enforced mechanically. That part is genuinely attractive.

Against: the tags do not survive the GPU boundary, where a shader sees sixteen bytes; they do not survive serialisation; and they add a template parameter to every signature in a library that is currently readable by a beginner in an afternoon — which is a real cost for a teaching codebase in a way it would not be for a shipping one.

The strongest version of the argument probably lands on "not the whole library, but yes for the matrices", since a mistyped matrix product is both the most damaging error and the one a type can catch most cheaply. If you reach that conclusion independently, you have rediscovered what several production engines actually do.

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • Place things. There is a transform type — position, rotation, scale — and one function that turns it into the matrix carrying a mesh from its own space into the shared world. That is the first link of the space chain, and the first time the word "scene" has meant anything.
  • Reuse a mesh. Three objects on screen share one eight-vertex array and differ only in nine floats each. This is why a game ships one crate and places four hundred.
  • Derive the order rather than recall it. T·R·S follows from three sentences about what scale, rotation and translation each mean, and both wrong orders have distinct, nameable, on-screen symptoms: T·S·R deforms, R·T·S misplaces.
  • Read a matrix instead of running it. Columns 0–2 are the object's own axes in world space, scaled by its size; column 3 is its position. Debugging a transform is now usually a matter of looking at four columns.
  • Name spaces so mistakes are visible. a_from_b, and the rule that adjacent labels must match in a product. This one habit will carry through the view matrix, projection, normal transforms, shadow maps and skinning — every place the course gets harder.

Next: 2.9 — The View Matrix: Deriving Look-At. We can put objects in a world, and we are still looking at that world from a fixed, unremarkable spot — because there is no camera. The next link in the chain is view_from_world, and it has a lovely twist: a camera is just another object with a transform, and looking through it means applying that transform backwards. Moving the camera left is moving the world right. Which means 2.9 needs an inverse — and the transform we need to invert has exactly the structured shape that Exercise 2.8.3 just asked you to work out.
