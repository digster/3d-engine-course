Module 1 — The Loop, the Pixel, and Time · Lesson 1.3

Frames, Delta Time, and Why Naive Loops Lie

The box we built in 1.2 moves six pixels per frame, which means its speed is a property of your monitor rather than of your game. Fixing that takes one multiplication. Then we spend the rest of the lesson finding out how much that fix does not fix — because the honest answer is "less than every tutorial on the internet implies", and knowing exactly where it stops working is what makes Lesson 1.4 feel inevitable instead of arbitrary.

1The Problem

Here is the line we wrote in 1.2, and flagged at the time as deliberately wrong:

Six pixels, once per frame. Read that as a physical claim and it says: the box moves six pixels every time the machine finishes drawing. Nothing in it refers to time, so its speed is whatever number of frames per second the machine happens to achieve — a number that depends on the monitor, the GPU, the scene, the weather inside the laptop, and whether the player has vsync on.

Worked example — the same build, three machines

Six pixels per frame, across a 1280-pixel window:

Frame rateActual speedTime to cross the window
30 fps6 × 30 = 180 px/s1280 / 180 = 7.1 s
60 fps6 × 60 = 360 px/s1280 / 360 = 3.6 s
144 fps6 × 144 = 864 px/s1280 / 864 = 1.5 s

The 144 Hz player's box is 4.8× faster than the 30 fps player's. Not "smoother" — faster. If this were a platformer, the two players are playing games with different physics, and any jump the designer tuned at 60 fps is either unmakeable or trivial for everyone else. Figure 1 is the same fact drawn.

You may object that this was true of the games you grew up with, and you are right. On a NES, every machine ran at the same fixed refresh rate, so "per frame" was a unit of time — which is exactly why those games run at comic speed on emulators that do not throttle, and why old PC games tied to the CPU clock became unplayable the moment CPUs got faster. The assumption was load-bearing, and then it broke. We are writing for machines that run at 30, 60, 120, 144, 165 and 240 Hz, sometimes changing mid-session when the laptop switches to battery.

So: multiply by elapsed time. That is the fix, it is one line, and by §3.3 we will have it. The reason this lesson keeps going afterwards is that the fix has a precise domain of validity, and the failures outside it are the ones that get shipped.

2Building Intuition

2.1 A speed with no time in it

Ask anyone how fast a car goes and the answer has two units in it: kilometres per hour. Distance over time. You cannot state a speed without naming a duration, because speed is a ratio — that is what the word means.

6.0f pixels per frame has a distance and no time. It is not a speed; it is a distance per event, and the event happens at a rate nobody promised anything about. It is like agreeing to pay rent "per visit from the landlord": a perfectly well-defined arrangement whose annual cost is entirely up to someone else.

Once you see it that way, the fix writes itself. If you want 360 pixels per second, then in a frame that lasted dt seconds you should move 360 × dt pixels. A frame that took twice as long moves the box twice as far, so the box covers the same ground per second on every machine. The frames cancel out, leaving a quantity in real units.

2.2 The ruler is part of the experiment

Before we can multiply by elapsed time, we have to measure elapsed time — and this is where a surprising number of engines go wrong, because measurement feels like it should be free. It is not. Three questions have to be answered deliberately, and getting any one of them wrong corrupts every number downstream.

Which clock? Your computer has more than one, and they disagree on purpose. One tells you what time it is in the world — and gets corrected, by NTP, by daylight saving, by the user dragging it. The other only counts forward from some arbitrary start and never gets corrected. Ask the first one for a duration and you can get a negative answer.

Which resolution? A ruler marked only in centimetres cannot measure a millimetre, and it will not tell you it cannot — it will just answer 0 or 1, confidently. A clock that reports whole milliseconds, asked about a frame that took 3.3 ms, has the same problem.

Which number type? A duration and a timestamp are different animals. Durations are small and want to be convenient; timestamps grow without bound and want to be exact. Storing the second in a type sized for the first is a bug that takes hours of uptime to appear, and then freezes time completely.

Take those three in order, and then we can talk about motion.

3The Theory

3.1 Which clock

The clock on your wall is a wall clock — it tries to tell you the truth about the world, and to keep doing so it must occasionally be corrected. NTP nudges it constantly, in both directions. Daylight saving moves it an hour. A user setting their date moves it a year. In C++, std::chrono::system_clock, time() and ::GetSystemTime are all wall clocks.

A monotonic clock makes a different promise: it counts forward from an arbitrary zero and never goes backwards or jumps. It cannot tell you what day it is. It is the only correct choice for measuring a duration, and Figure 2 shows why in one picture.

SDL gives us the right clock, and it is worth knowing that we checked rather than assumed:

Verified in SDL's source

The headers say "since SDL was initialised" but do not use the word monotonic, so we followed it down. SDL_GetTicksNS is implemented on top of SDL_GetPerformanceCounter (src/timer/SDL_timer.c), which uses CLOCK_MONOTONIC_RAW where available — the Unix source carries the comment that it chose that clock because it "is not subject to adjustment by NTP" — falling back to CLOCK_MONOTONIC, and using mach_absolute_time on Apple platforms and QueryPerformanceCounter on Windows. All monotonic. Figure 2's disaster cannot reach us.

3.2 Which unit, and which type

SDL offers the same instant in milliseconds and in nanoseconds. Milliseconds feel like plenty — a frame is about 16 of them — and they are not, because a frame is about 16 of them and the interesting part is the "about".

Worked example — measuring with a ruler that is too coarse
Frame rateTrue frame timeAs whole millisecondsMeasurement error
60 fps16.667 ms16 or 17up to 2%
300 fps3.333 ms3 or 4up to 20%
1000 fps1.000 ms1 or 2up to 100%
2000 fps0.500 ms0total — nothing moves

At 300 fps the box moves 20% further on some frames than others, purely because of how the clock rounds — the measurement itself is producing judder, on a machine that is performing beautifully. And above 1000 fps, which an uncapped 2D loop reaches trivially, truncation reaches zero: dt is 0, the multiplication yields nothing, and the game freezes on the fastest hardware you own. Nanoseconds cost nothing extra and make the whole category of problem disappear.

Now the type, which is the subtler decision. There are two different quantities here and they want opposite things:

  • A delta is tiny (0.016), used in arithmetic constantly, and thrown away at the end of the frame. float is ideal.
  • A timestamp grows for as long as the program runs and must stay exact, because everything else is derived from differences between timestamps.

A float has a 24-bit significand — roughly seven decimal digits, total. It does not lose a fixed amount of precision; it loses precision proportionally, so the bigger the number gets, the coarser its steps become. Keep your elapsed time in a float and the resolution of your clock quietly degrades all day.

Worked example — the day time stopped

The gap between representable float values (the ulp) at two magnitudes:

  • At 3600.0 (1 hour of uptime): ulp = 0.000244 s. A 60 fps frame of 0.016667 s is stored as 0.0166016 — already 0.4% wrong, every frame, forever.
  • At 86400.0 (24 hours): ulp = 0.0078125 s, which is 7.8 milliseconds.

A frame at 500 fps is 0.002 s. That is less than half of one ulp at 86 400, so the addition rounds to nothing:

ExpressionResult
float(86400.0f) + float(1.0f/500)86400.0f — unchanged
Uint64(86400 s in ns) + Uint64(1/500 s in ns)advanced by exactly 2 000 000 ns

Time simply stops. Not "drifts" — stops, permanently, for a program that has been up a day. This is the bug behind arcade cabinets and kiosk builds that go strange overnight, and it is invisible in any test that runs for less than a few hours. Both rows above were checked by compiling and running them, not by reasoning about IEEE-754.

Hence the rule the clock class is built around: absolute time is Uint64 nanoseconds; only the small delta is ever converted to float. A Uint64 of nanoseconds is exact, and it does not overflow for about 584 years, which comfortably outlasts the session.

3.3 Delta time, and where it is exactly right

With a good measurement in hand, the fix is the one you already derived in §2.1. Express speed in units of distance per second, and multiply by the seconds that actually elapsed:

Worked example — the frames cancel

360 px/s, one second of running, at four wildly different frame rates:

Frame ratedtMovement per frameFrames in 1 sTotal distance
30 fps0.033333 s360 × 0.033333 = 12.0 px30360.0 px
60 fps0.016667 s360 × 0.016667 = 6.0 px60360.0 px
144 fps0.006944 s360 × 0.006944 = 2.5 px144360.0 px
1000 fps0.001000 s360 × 0.001 = 0.36 px1000360.0 px

Identical, to the last decimal, on every machine. And this is not approximately right — for constant velocity it is exact, because distance really is velocity times time and the sum of the parts is the whole: Σ(v × dtᵢ) = v × Σdtᵢ = v × T, whatever the individual dtᵢ happen to be. That last identity is worth pausing on, because it is the entire reason the technique works, and it is also the reason it stops working in the next section.

Note precisely why it worked: v could be pulled out of the sum, because it never changed. Anything that makes the velocity change during the interval breaks that step — and gravity, thrust, friction, drag and springs are all exactly that.

This is explicit Euler integration, and it is doing something specific and visible: it pretends the velocity was constant for the whole step. It was not — it was growing the entire time — so every step falls a little short. Figure 3 draws exactly that.

Worked example — the error is proportional to the step

Free fall from rest, g = 10 m/s², over exactly T = 1 s. The true answer is ½ g T² = 5.0 m. Running the two lines above with different step sizes:

Step hStepsEuler's answerError

Read the error column: every time the step halves, the error halves. That is the signature of a first-order method, and here it has an exact closed form — the sum of the staircase works out to g·T·(T − h)/2, so the shortfall is ½·g·T·h. Substituting h = 0.25 gives ½ × 10 × 1 × 0.25 = 1.25 m, matching the table and Figure 3's dashed gap.

Now read that formula as a statement about your game: the error is proportional to h, and h is the player's frame rate. The 144 Hz player and the 30 Hz player are not running the same physics. They fall at different rates, their jumps peak at different heights, and no amount of dt-scaling will reconcile them, because dt-scaling is what produced the discrepancy.

Drag the slider below to watch the staircase converge. Notice that it never touches the curve — it only gets closer, and it approaches from the same side every time.

h = 0.2500 s Euler: 3.7500 m error: 1.2500 m

Green is the truth, p = ½gt², reaching 5 m. Red is what your engine computes. The readout tracks ½·g·T·h exactly — halve the step, halve the error, and never reach zero.

The other order, and why we are not fixing it here

Swap the two lines — update velocity first, then move with the new one — and you get semi-implicit Euler, which for the same one-second fall gives 10.0 m at h = 1 and 5.0833 m at h = 1/60: it overshoots by exactly as much as explicit Euler undershoots. It is strictly better behaved for games (it conserves energy far better in oscillating systems), and swapping two lines is a real improvement you could make today. But it is still first-order, its error is still proportional to h, and the frame rate still changes the answer — so it does not solve today's problem, and it deserves the proper treatment it gets in Module 7 rather than a footnote here.

3.5 The four betrayals of a variable step

§3.4 is the deep problem. Four more concrete ones travel with it, and each has a characteristic bug report attached.

One: determinism is gone. dt is a measurement of a noisy physical process — OS scheduling, cache state, the GPU, another process waking up. Run the same build with the same inputs twice and you get two different sequences of dt, and therefore two different outcomes. That makes replays impossible to store as input logs, lockstep multiplayer impossible outright, and physics bugs impossible to reproduce: the report says "my car flipped at that corner" and it will never flip there for you, because your machine sliced time differently. Determinism is not a feature you add later. It is a property you either designed for or destroyed.

Two: tunnelling. Collision detection typically asks "is the object inside the wall now?" — a question asked only at step boundaries. Make the step long enough and the object is on one side at the end of one step and past it at the end of the next, having never been detected inside it.

Three: dt can be zero. Two ticks inside the same clock resolution, a first frame measured before anything happened, a paused-and-resumed loop. Anything of the form something / dt — deriving a velocity from positions, an average, a smoothing factor — becomes a division by zero, and IEEE-754 does not stop you: you get inf, then NaN as soon as it meets a subtraction. NaN then propagates through every arithmetic operation it touches, silently, and since NaN != NaN, even your comparisons stop behaving. The classic symptom is an entire scene vanishing at once, several frames after the actual mistake.

Four: dt can be enormous. You hit a breakpoint, look at a variable for forty seconds, and continue. The next dt is 40.0. A character walking at 5 m/s teleports 200 metres, through the level, into the void — and this is the very first thing that happens after every debugging session, which is a special kind of infuriating. A laptop resuming from sleep does the same thing without a debugger involved.

3.6 The patches, and what each one costs

Each betrayal has an obvious patch. None of them is free, and being able to state the price is the difference between engineering and cargo-culting.

PatchWhat it fixesWhat it costs
Clamp dt to a ceiling (0.25 s) The breakpoint spike, sleep/resume, the worst tunnelling Simulated time now runs slower than real time whenever it triggers. The game enters slow motion rather than teleporting — usually the right trade, but it is a trade, and it means your clock and the wall clock have silently diverged.
Floor dt to a small epsilon Division by zero Cheap and worth doing, but it papers over the real question of why you are dividing by a measured duration at all.
Smooth dt over several frames Visible judder from frame-time noise Adds latency, and the simulation now advances by a time that did not happen. Still not deterministic — it is a filtered version of the same noise.
Cap the frame rate with a sleep Wasted power; wildly varying dt SDL_Delay is documented to wait at least the requested time, "but possibly longer due to OS scheduling" — so you get a noisy approximation of a cap. And it does nothing for determinism: every machine still measures its own slightly different frame times.

Look down the "costs" column and one word keeps recurring: determinism. Not one of these patches restores it, because they all keep the same broken premise — that the amount of time the simulation advances should be decided by how long rendering happened to take. Those two quantities have nothing to do with each other. One is a property of the physics you designed; the other is a property of the hardware you are running on.

So the real answer is not another patch. It is to stop deriving one from the other: let the simulation advance in fixed, identical steps that you chose, and let rendering happen as often as the machine can manage, showing whatever the simulation's state currently is. Figure 5 is the idea in outline; deriving it properly is Lesson 1.4.

The spiral of death — meet it before it meets you

That decoupling has one famous trap, and it is worth naming now so it is not a surprise. If the loop has fallen behind, it must run several fixed steps to catch up. Suppose each 1/60 s step costs 20 ms of real time to compute:

  • Each step simulates 16.67 ms of game time…
  • …but consumes 20 ms of real time doing it.
  • So every step puts the engine 3.33 ms further behind, which means more steps next frame, which means more real time, which means further behind.

The frame time grows without bound and the application locks solid. The condition is simply cost per step ÷ time simulated per step > 1 — here 20 / 16.67 = 1.2. This is the spiral of death, and it is the reason 1.4 is a full lesson rather than a five-line snippet.

4Implementation: the clock

The engine gains its second subsystem, alongside input, and it is built on the three decisions from §3.1–3.2: a monotonic source, nanosecond resolution, and integers for absolute time.

C++ in place — namespaces, and why engine::clock is safe

There is already a clock in C++: std::clock() from <ctime>, which most standard headers drag in transitively. Naming a class clock sounds like asking for trouble, and it is not, because ours lives in namespace engine.

A namespace is a named scope for declarations. engine::clock and ::clock are different names in the way that two files in different directories are different files, and the compiler resolves an unqualified clock by searching outward from the current scope — so inside namespace engine, ours is found first and the global one is never reached. This is precisely why the course never writes using namespace std; at file scope: that instruction drags every standard name into the global scope and re-creates the collisions namespaces exist to prevent.

There is a second, smaller namespace in clock.cpp — an anonymous one, namespace { … }, holding the ns_to_seconds helper. Anything inside it has internal linkage: it exists only in that translation unit and is invisible to the linker, so another file may define its own ns_to_seconds without a clash. That is the modern replacement for C's static at file scope, and it is where every helper that is not part of the interface belongs.

4.1 tick(), and the three numbers it produces

The whole of the measurement is four lines, and every one of them is a decision:

The subtraction is on Uint64, which would be a landmine if the clock could ever run backwards: 0 - 1 in unsigned arithmetic is not -1, it is about 18 quintillion, and one such frame would fling every object in the scene into the void. It cannot happen, because §3.1 established the source is monotonic. Note the shape of that reasoning, because it recurs throughout engine work: the safety comes from an invariant established elsewhere, so the code carries a comment naming the invariant it depends on. Silent reliance is how an invariant gets broken by someone who did not know it existed.

Then ns_to_seconds, whose one subtlety is the order of operations:

Divide in double, then narrow the small result to float. Converting the nanosecond count to float first would destroy it before the division: a float cannot represent one second's worth of nanoseconds — 1 000 000 000 — to the nearest integer, so the numerator would already be wrong. This is the same proportional-precision fact from §3.2, appearing one layer down.

4.2 The clamp, and telling the truth about it

dt() is clamped and raw_dt() is not. Two accessors instead of one, because they answer different questions and only one of them is safe to simulate with:

The clamp is a deliberate falsehood, so the class exposes the fact that it lied. That is the principle worth taking from this section: when a system has to distort reality to stay safe, it should be able to say so. A profiler that read the clamped value would report a healthy 4 ms frame during a catastrophic stall, and the one moment you most need the truth is the moment the safety mechanism engaged. was_clamped() is why the demo can print CLAMPED on screen the instant it happens.

fps() has the same character. An instantaneous 1/dt is unreadable — it flickers across hundreds of values a second — so the clock averages over a half-second window. That makes it a statement about the recent past rather than about this frame, which is ideal for a human reading a number and disqualifying for a simulation. The doc comment says so in as many words.

4.3 Wiring it into the frame

The loop gains one call, in a position that matters:

Once per frame, after the drain, before anything reads it — the same frame-coherence rule input follows, for the same reason. Tick it twice and the second dt is almost zero; tick it inside the simulation and two systems integrate with two different ideas of how long the frame was. Capturing dt into a local const float at the top makes that guarantee visible at a glance.

4.4 A demo built to fail in three different ways

The demo replaces 1.2's box (which has served its purpose) with three moving things, chosen so that changing the frame rate affects each one differently:

The bounce is perfectly elastic — ball_vy = -ball_vy, with no energy lost — on purpose. With a physically exact simulation the ball would return to the grey line it was dropped from, forever. It does not: it climbs higher after every bounce, because explicit Euler injects energy that does not exist. Since the size of that error is proportional to dt (§3.4), the climb is barely perceptible at a thousand frames a second and violent at twenty. Anything the ball does that is not "return to the line" is the integrator's error made visible.

Note that this is 1.2's distinction earning its keep in ordinary code, with no ceremony: toggling is an edge, stalling is a level, and writing either one the other way would be immediately, visibly wrong. The throttle sits at the very end of the loop body, after SDL_RenderPresent, so the stall lands between presenting one frame and measuring the next — exactly where a real hitch would land.

Vsync starts off, which is not how a game should ship but is the honest default for a measuring instrument. With vsync on, every dt is your monitor's refresh interval, and a bug about frame-rate dependence is invisible because only one frame rate is available.

Where the on-screen text comes from

The readout uses SDL_RenderDebugTextFormat, SDL's built-in bitmap font: a single hardcoded 8×8 typeface, ASCII only, no wrapping, drawn in the current draw colour. It is not a text-rendering system and does not pretend to be — SDL's own header points you at SDL_ttf or stb_truetype for anything real, and we build proper text in Module 6. For numbers you stare at while debugging, with zero assets and zero dependencies, it is exactly right. We scale it 2× with SDL_SetRenderScale to make it legible, which also multiplies the coordinates — hence the halved positions in the listing.

Checkpoint — verified before publishing

Built warning-clean at -Wall -Wextra with Apple Clang against SDL 3.4.12, run, and shut down cleanly. The clock was then driven by a test harness against real elapsed time, confirming: a 50 ms frame measures as 50 ms with no clamping; a 400 ms frame reports dt() == 0.25 exactly while raw_dt() reports ≈0.40 and was_clamped() is true; a custom 0.05 s ceiling clamps to 0.05; elapsed() tracks real time across clamped frames; fps() converges on the true rate over its window; and dt() is never negative.

§3.2's float claim was compiled and run rather than argued: 86400.0f + (1.0f/500) evaluates to exactly 86400.0f — time frozen — while the same sum in Uint64 nanoseconds advances by exactly 2 000 000 ns.

The ball's behaviour was predicted numerically before it was coded. Dropped from y = 260 onto a floor at y = 620 with g = 1200 px/s² and a perfectly elastic bounce, successive apex heights are: at 2000 fps 259, 258, 257 (barely drifting); at 60 fps 236, 204, 171 (visibly climbing); at 20 fps 188, 83, −34 — off the top of the window on the third bounce. If yours escapes at a different bounce, your frame rate differs from the model's; the direction is what matters.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Everything teleports the moment you resume from a breakpoint — objects vanish or land outside the level
Symptom
You break, inspect a variable, continue — and the character is gone. Reliably, every debugging session.
Cause
The frame you paused in lasted as long as you were paused, so dt is 40 seconds and one step moved everything 40 seconds' worth (§3.5).
Fix
Clamp dt to a ceiling — clock does this by default at 0.25 s. Expose was_clamped() so the distortion is visible rather than silent.
Movement is choppy at high frame rates, smooth at low ones — the faster machine looks worse
Symptom
Uncapped, the game judders. Turn vsync on and it smooths out. Backwards from every intuition.
Cause
dt is being measured in whole milliseconds. At 300 fps the true frame is 3.33 ms and the clock reports 3 or 4 — a ±20% error, frame to frame, generated by the measurement itself (§3.2).
Fix
SDL_GetTicksNS(), not SDL_GetTicks(). If you must keep a millisecond API, at least accumulate the remainder rather than discarding it every frame.
The whole scene disappears, several frames after nothing in particular — every position is NaN
Symptom
Objects vanish all at once. In the debugger, positions read nan or -nan, and comparisons behave impossibly — x == x is false.
Cause
Something divided by dt on a frame where dt was zero, producing inf, and a later subtraction turned inf - inf into NaN. NaN then spread through every operation it touched (§3.5).
Fix
Guard the division rather than the symptom: if (dt > 0.0f). To find the origin, test std::isnan at a few checkpoints and bisect — the crash site is never where the NaN was born, because NaN propagates silently for a long time first.
Time runs slightly slow, and slightly slower the longer the game is open — a timer that drifts by minutes
Symptom
An in-game clock loses time compared with a stopwatch. The discrepancy grows with uptime, and on a long-running build eventually stops advancing altogether.
Cause
Elapsed time is accumulated in a float. Its precision degrades in proportion to its magnitude, and beyond about a day of uptime a short frame is smaller than one ulp and adds nothing at all (§3.2).
Fix
Keep absolute time in Uint64 nanoseconds and derive seconds on demand. Only the per-frame delta should ever be a float.
Fast objects pass through thin walls, but only sometimes — unreproducible; worse on slow machines
Symptom
A bullet or a falling player occasionally goes through geometry. Impossible to reproduce on demand, and much more common under load.
Cause
Tunnelling. Collision is tested at step boundaries, and a long dt makes the step longer than the wall is thick (§3.5, Figure 4).
Fix
Bound the step — a fixed timestep does this by construction, which is one of its main attractions (1.4). Genuinely fast objects also want swept/continuous collision, which is Module 7. Clamping dt reduces the worst case but does not eliminate it.
The game runs at the right speed but the physics differs between machines — "that jump is impossible on my PC"
Symptom
Movement speed is correctly frame-rate independent, yet jump heights, fall times, or vehicle handling differ between players — and between two runs on the same machine.
Cause
Anything accelerating is integrated with error proportional to dt, so a different frame rate is a different simulation (§3.4). This is not fixable by scaling harder; it is inherent to stepping a variable amount.
Fix
Give the simulation a step size of its own, independent of the frame rate. That is Lesson 1.4, and this pitfall is the reason it exists.

8Exercises

1.3.1Make the measurement worsewarm-up

In clock::tick(), swap SDL_GetTicksNS() for SDL_GetTicks() * 1000000 — the same instant, measured in whole milliseconds. Rebuild and run with vsync off. What happens to the sage box, and to the readout?

Solution

The sage box develops a visible stutter and the dt readout snaps between a few discrete values instead of varying smoothly. At a high enough frame rate it stops entirely: every frame measures 0 ms, dt is 0, and 360 × 0 = 0. Nothing has gone wrong with the machine — the ruler simply has no marks between the numbers being measured. Restore SDL_GetTicksNS afterwards.

1.3.2Find the clampwarm-up

Put a breakpoint inside the main loop, run, wait ten seconds at the breakpoint, then continue. What do dt() and raw_dt() report on the next frame, and what does the readout show? Then set the ceiling to 10.0f in the constructor and repeat.

Solution

With the default ceiling, raw_dt() is about 10.0 and dt() is exactly 0.25, with CLAMPED on the readout. The sage box jumps 90 pixels (a quarter-second's worth) — noticeable but harmless. With the ceiling raised to 10 seconds the clamp does nothing: the box jumps 3600 pixels, wrapping clean off the screen, and the ball is launched out of the window entirely. That is what every frame after a breakpoint would do without the clamp, which is why 0.25 s is the conventional value.

1.3.3Measure the integrator's error yourselfguided

Add a small program (or a temporary block in main) that simulates one second of free fall with g = 10 using explicit Euler at h = 1, 1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/60, printing the final position and its error against ½ g T² = 5.0. Confirm the error halves as the step halves, and check your numbers against ½·g·T·h.

Hint

Two nested loops: the outer over step sizes, the inner running 1/h steps of p += v*h; v += g*h;. Print with enough decimals to see the pattern — four is plenty.

Solution

You should reproduce §3.4's table exactly: 0.0, 2.5, 3.75, 4.375, 4.9167, with errors 5.0, 2.5, 1.25, 0.625, 0.0833. Each error is ½ × 10 × 1 × h.

Then try the interesting variation: swap the two lines so velocity updates first. The errors become −5.0, −2.5, −1.25, … — the same magnitudes, opposite sign. Explicit Euler undershoots and semi-implicit overshoots by exactly the same amount, which is a strong hint that averaging them would be better still. That hint is worth remembering; it is where Module 7's better integrators come from.

1.3.4Prove the ball gains energyguided

The bounce is perfectly elastic, so total energy should be constant. Compute it each frame — kinetic plus potential, ½v² + g·h per unit mass, with h the height above the floor — and log it once a second. Watch it while you hold T. Where is the energy coming from?

Solution sketch

Energy climbs steadily, and much faster while throttled. Nothing in the code adds energy on purpose: it comes from the integrator. During each step, position is advanced with the velocity from the start of the step, so on the way up the ball is credited with more upward travel than it earned — and the discrepancy scales with dt.

The point worth carrying forward is that "conserved" quantities in a simulation are only ever conserved to the accuracy of the integrator. When something in a game slowly gains or loses energy for no reason, the integrator is the first suspect, and a conservation check like this one is the cheapest instrument you can build to catch it.

1.3.5Cap the frame rate, honestlyopen

Add a frame-rate cap: after presenting, sleep until the frame has lasted at least 1/target seconds. Use SDL_DelayNS, then try SDL_DelayPrecise. Measure what you actually get — log raw_dt() for a few hundred frames and look at the spread, not the average. Is the cap doing what you asked?

Hint

Compute the target frame length in nanoseconds, compare it against the time since clk last ticked, and sleep the difference — never a fixed amount. And read the header docs for both delay functions before you decide which result surprised you.

Solution sketch

You will overshoot, and the amount varies. SDL_Delay and SDL_DelayNS are documented to wait at least the requested time "but possibly longer due to OS scheduling" — you are asking the scheduler for a favour, not setting a timer. SDL_DelayPrecise is documented to get as close as it can, "busy waiting if necessary", which is tighter and costs CPU (and battery) to achieve. That tradeoff is the whole of frame pacing in one pair of functions.

Worth noticing while measuring: even the tightest cap does not make two machines agree, since each still measures its own jittery frame times and simulates by them. A cap is a power-and-heat tool and a smoothness tool. It is not a determinism tool, and no amount of precision turns it into one.

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • It knows what time it is — from a monotonic clock, in nanoseconds, with absolute time kept in integers so it stays exact for as long as anyone will ever run it.
  • Its motion is expressed in real units. Speeds are per second, not per frame, and for constant velocity the result is identical on every machine — exactly, not approximately.
  • It is honest about the lies it tells. dt() is clamped for safety, raw_dt() reports what really happened, and was_clamped() says which you are looking at.
  • You can see the remaining problem, on screen, on demand. Two keys change the frame rate; one box tracks it, one ignores it, and a ball that ignores it is nevertheless launched out of the window.
  • You can state the limit precisely: dt-scaling is exact for constant velocity and first-order for everything else, with error proportional to dt — so the frame rate is still an input to the simulation.

The idea to carry forward: how long the last frame took and how far the simulation should advance are two unrelated questions, and every problem in §3.5 comes from answering the second with the first.

Next: 1.4 — The Fixed Timestep with Interpolation, Derived. We stop letting the hardware choose the step size. The simulation advances in identical slices we picked; rendering happens whenever the machine is ready; and the leftover time between the two gets smoothed by interpolating between the last two states. Along the way we defuse the spiral of death, and the engine gains a loop it will keep for the rest of the course.
