Building mat4 by Hand

This is the shortest-feeling lesson in the module and it produces the most code, because Lesson 2.5 did the thinking. The question was never about how many axes there are: ask where do the basis vectors land? in three dimensions and you get three columns instead of two, with every rule surviving word for word. Then we build the 4×4 — and find that its fourth column does absolutely nothing. That is not a bug, it is the honest state of the arithmetic, and it is where this lesson stops.

1The Problem

Two problems, and they are of very different sizes.

The small one: this is a 3-D engine and everything we have built is two-dimensional. The rasterizer draws triangles on a plane, vec2 has two components, mat2 transforms a plane. Module 3 loads meshes; Module 4 hands them to a GPU. Neither is possible until the maths has a third axis.

That one turns out to be nearly free, and that is the interesting thing about it. Lesson 2.5's central argument — a linear transformation is determined by where it sends the basis vectors — was proved in three lines that never mentioned how many basis vectors there were. So it holds in 3-D, in 4-D, in any dimension you like, and this lesson is mostly a matter of transcription. If you understood 2.5, you have already done most of 2.6.

The large one: we still cannot translate.

Lesson 2.5 §3.8 proved that no matrix can move the origin, and the proof did not depend on the size of the matrix either. Put c = 0 into the scaling rule: T(0·v) = 0·T(v), so T(origin) = origin, always. A 3×3 is no help. A 100×100 would be no help. Verified over 500 assorted 3×3 matrices in the harness: not one moved the origin, because none can.

And this is not a small inconvenience

Rotation is about the origin. Scaling is about the origin. But a scene is a collection of objects that are somewhere — and you want each one to rotate about its own centre, standing where it stands. Exercise 2.5.3 had you do that by hand with M*(v − c) + c, and asked you to notice what was unsatisfying about it: the transformation is no longer a matrix, it is a matrix and a vector and a rule for combining them, and composing two of them means deriving (M₂M₁, M₂t₁ + t₂) and carrying it around yourself.

Ten objects, each with its own position, each parented to something else that also moves — that is an ordinary scene graph, and it is unmanageable this way.

So the obvious move is to make the matrix bigger and put the translation in the extra room. We are going to do exactly that, carefully, and it is going to fail — visibly, on screen, with a keypress. Understanding why it fails is what makes Lesson 2.7's fix feel like the only possible answer rather than a trick somebody pulled out of a hat.

2Building Intuition

One more arrow

In 2-D, a vector was a recipe: v = x·î + y·ĵ. In 3-D there is a third basis vector, conventionally k̂, pointing along z — and a vector is the same kind of recipe with one more ingredient.

\[ \mathbf{v} = x\,\hat{\imath} + y\,\hat{\jmath} + z\,\hat{k} \] v = x * i-hat + y * j-hat + z * k-hat

And a linear transformation preserves recipes, so knowing where the three basis vectors land determines everything. Three answers instead of two; three columns instead of two. Figure 1 is Lesson 2.5's Figure 2 with an axis added, and there is deliberately nothing else new in it.

Rotation needs an axis

Now the one thing in this lesson that is genuinely new, and it is worth a moment because it is the seed of a great deal of later difficulty.

In 2-D, "rotate by 40°" is a complete instruction. There is only one plane to turn in, so there is nothing more to say. In 3-D it is not a complete instruction at all — you have to say about what. A rotation in 3-D picks a line that stays exactly where it is, and turns everything else around it.

Hold a pen upright and spin it between your fingers: the pen is the axis, and it does not move. Now lay it flat and roll it: the pen is still the axis, still not moving, but everything else goes somewhere different. Same angle, different axis, different result.

The useful way to see each one

A rotation about an axis is Lesson 2.5's 2-D rotation, acting in the plane of the other two axes, with the axis's own column left completely alone.

Rotating about z turns x toward y and leaves z untouched — so it is literally the 2×2 rotation matrix in the top-left corner with a row and column of the identity added. That is what "the z axis points out of the 2-D page" means, made concrete.

Three axes, three rotations, and each one built by the same question we have been asking since Lesson 2.5. §3.3 does all three, and shows why one of them has its minus sign somewhere surprising.

3The Theory

vec3: what carries over, and the one thing that does not

Read Lesson 1.7's definitions again and notice what they never said. "Adding is tip-to-tail." "Scaling stretches without turning." "The dot product is how much of a points along b." "Normalising separates direction from length." Not one of those mentions a number of components — so all of them are true in 3-D with no re-derivation at all, and vec3.hpp is vec2.hpp with a z.

The dot product is worth one sentence of reassurance because it is the one people expect to change. It does not. Two arrows in 3-D still span a plane between them, the angle between them still lives in that plane, and Lesson 1.7 §3.4's projection argument runs word for word. One more multiply, no new idea:

\[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_x b_x + a_y b_y + a_z b_z = \|\mathbf{a}\|\,\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta \] dot(a,b) = a.x*b.x + a.y*b.y + a.z*b.z = |a| |b| cos(theta)

Now the thing that does not carry over, because noticing it is worth more than the twelve functions that do.

vec2 has perpendicular(v), which turns an arrow a quarter turn. In 2-D that is a sensible function: there is essentially one direction at right angles to a given one, up to sign. In 3-D there is an entire plane of them. "What is perpendicular to this arrow?" has no single answer, so the 2-D routine has no honest generalisation and vec3.hpp does not contain one.

To get a specific perpendicular you need a second vector to pin down which one you mean — and the operation that takes two vectors and returns something perpendicular to both is the cross product. It is not here either. Lesson 3.4 introduces it at the moment a triangle supplies that second vector and back-face culling needs a surface normal, so it can be derived from the need instead of declared in advance.

Why an absent function is worth a section

A maths library that contains everything anyone might want is a library nobody can read, and one where you cannot tell which functions have been thought about. Every function in src/math/ exists because a lesson needed it, which is also why every one of them has a derivation you can go and find.

mat3: the same three sentences

Three columns, one per basis vector. Applying is the recipe with three terms. Composing is "send each of B's columns through A". That is the entire type:

\[ M\mathbf{v} = x\,\mathbf{c_0} + y\,\mathbf{c_1} + z\,\mathbf{c_2}, \qquad (AB)\text{'s column } j = A\,\mathbf{b}_j \] M*v = x*c0 + y*c1 + z*c2; column j of A*B = A applied to B's column j

Same storage decision as mat2, for the same reason: three vec3 columns, so the nine floats sit in memory as c0.x, c0.y, c0.z, c1.x, … — the left column first, read downwards. Written on paper it is still rows. The trap from Lesson 2.5 §3.3 is unchanged except that there are now three chances to fall into it.

Worked example — the 2.5 example, one axis wider

Let î land at (2,0,0), ĵ at (1,3,0) and k̂ at (0,0,4). Those are the columns:

\[ M = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \] M = | 2 1 0 ; 0 3 0 ; 0 0 4 | -- columns (2,0,0), (1,3,0), (0,0,4)

Transform v = (3, 2, 1) — three of the first column, two of the second, one of the third:

\[ M\mathbf{v} = 3(2,0,0) + 2(1,3,0) + 1(0,0,4) = (8, 6, 4) \] M*v = 3*(2,0,0) + 2*(1,3,0) + 1*(0,0,4) = (8, 6, 4)

The first two components are Lesson 2.5's answer unchanged, because the third column and the third row of this particular matrix do not interfere with them. That is not a coincidence — it is what "this matrix leaves z alone" looks like, and it is exactly the structure the rotations in §3.3 have.

The determinant is 2 × 3 × 4 = 24, and the nine floats really do come out of memory in the order 2 0 0 1 3 0 0 0 4. Both asserted in the harness.

The three rotations, from the cycle

Each one is built by the usual question, and each one is Lesson 2.5's rotation acting in the plane of the other two axes. The only thing to be careful about is which two, and in which order — and there is a rule that settles it without memorisation.

Write the axes in a cycle: x → y → z → x. When you rotate about one of them, the other two play the roles that î and ĵ played in 2-D, in the order the cycle gives them. Rotating about x, the cycle continues y → z, so y is the "first" axis and z is the "second". Rotating about y it continues z → x. Rotating about z it continues x → y.

So, applying the usual question three times:

\[ R_x = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ 0 & \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix} \quad R_y = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & 0 & \sin\theta \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -\sin\theta & 0 & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix} \quad R_z = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \] Rx = |1,0,0; 0,cos,-sin; 0,sin,cos| Ry = |cos,0,sin; 0,1,0; -sin,0,cos| <- note the minus sign is on the BOTTOM left Rz = |cos,-sin,0; sin,cos,0; 0,0,1|
Why Ry's minus sign is somewhere else

Look at the three matrices and you will see that Rₓ and R_z have their minus sign above the diagonal, while R_y has it below. It looks like a typo. It is not, and it is not arbitrary either.

Rotating about y turns z toward x — the cycle wraps around, so the "first" axis is z and the "second" is x. But the matrix is written with x's row above z's row, so the pair appears in the opposite order to the one the rotation acts in, and the minus lands in the mirrored place.

Derive it from the cycle and it comes out right every time. Recall it from the shape of the other two and you will get it wrong. This is one of the two or three most common sign errors in all of graphics programming.

Two properties worth knowing about all of them, both verified in the harness across 121 angles each: a rotation has determinant 1 (it moves things without changing volume), and it preserves lengths — a probe vector of length 1.1225 comes out at 1.1225 for every rotation and every angle tested. Consequently transpose(R) == inverse(R), exactly as in 2-D.

And the sanity check that ties the whole section to Lesson 2.5: strike out the third row and column of R_z and what is left is the 2-D rotation matrix. Checked over 121 angles, zero mismatches. It is the same transformation; we have only stopped pretending the page has no thickness.

Why the order matters more in 3-D

Lesson 2.5 §3.6 established that composition is not commutative, using a rotation and a non-uniform scale. In 3-D something sharper is true: two rotations do not commute with each other, even though in 2-D any two rotations always did.

In 2-D, rotating by a and then b is rotating by a + b whichever way round you do it — there is only one plane, and angles just add. In 3-D that is only true when both rotations share an axis. The moment the axes differ, order changes the answer, and there is an example so clean you can do it in your head.

Worked example — the numbers behind Figure 3

Take Rₓ(0.6) and R_y(0.8), and the point (1, 0, 0).

Rotate about x first. The point lies on the x axis, and a rotation about x leaves the x axis fixed — so nothing happens at all. Then rotate about y, which turns z toward x, so (1,0,0) goes to (cos 0.8, 0, −sin 0.8):

\[ (1,0,0) \xrightarrow{\;R_x\;} (1,0,0) \xrightarrow{\;R_y\;} (0.6967,\ 0.0000,\ -0.7174) \] Rx first: (1,0,0) -> (1,0,0) -> (0.6967, 0.0000, -0.7174)

Rotate about y first. Now the point moves to (0.6967, 0, −0.7174) straight away — and it is no longer on the x axis, so the rotation about x has something to act on. It swings the z component up into y:

\[ (1,0,0) \xrightarrow{\;R_y\;} (0.6967, 0, -0.7174) \xrightarrow{\;R_x\;} (0.6967,\ 0.4050,\ -0.5921) \] Ry first: (1,0,0) -> (0.6967, 0, -0.7174) -> (0.6967, 0.4050, -0.5921)

The y component is 0 one way and 0.4050 the other. Both are correct; they are answers to different questions. In matrix terms Rₓ·R_y ≠ R_y·Rₓ — and remember that the rightmost factor is applied first, so Rₓ·R_y is the "y first" case.

Rotations about the same axis do still add, exactly as in 2-D: R_y(0.4)·R_y(0.9) == R_y(1.3), verified.

Where this is going, so it does not ambush you

Non-commuting rotations are the reason "store an object's orientation as three angles" — a pitch, a yaw and a roll — is a design that works until it suddenly does not. The three angles have to be applied in some fixed order, and at certain orientations two of the three axes line up and you lose a degree of freedom. That is gimbal lock, and Module 7 derives quaternions as the answer.

Nothing in Module 2 will hit it, because we compose rotations directly into a matrix rather than storing angles. Worth knowing that the trap exists and that it starts here.

The determinant is a volume factor

Everything in Lesson 2.5 §3.7 lifts, with "area" replaced by "volume".

The unit cube has the three basis vectors as its edges, so after the transformation it is the box spanned by the three columns, and the determinant is that box's signed volume. Every region scales by the same factor, because every region can be approximated by small cubes.

\[ \det M = m_{00}(m_{11}m_{22} - m_{12}m_{21}) - m_{01}(m_{10}m_{22} - m_{12}m_{20}) + m_{02}(m_{10}m_{21} - m_{11}m_{20}) \] det M = m00*(m11*m22 - m12*m21) - m01*(m10*m22 - m12*m20) + m02*(m10*m21 - m11*m20)

That is a cofactor expansion along the top row: each element of the row, times the 2×2 determinant of what is left after deleting its row and column, with alternating signs. It is less memorable than the 2×2 formula and there is no point pretending otherwise — what matters is what it means, and that has not changed.

One thing has changed, and it is worth naming. In 2-D a negative determinant meant the plane had been flipped over. In 3-D it means handedness has reversed: a right-handed set of axes has become a left-handed one. Our world is right-handed (Conventions page), so a model scaled by (−1, 1, 1) comes out mirrored and — once Lesson 3.4 adds back-face culling — renders inside-out, with every surface you should see culled and every surface you should not see drawn.

Measured, not asserted — by counting lattice points

Lesson 2.5 checked the area claim by rasterising a square and counting pixels. The 3-D version of that is to count points of a fine lattice that land inside the transformed unit cube — a point p is inside exactly when M⁻¹p has all three components in [0,1), which is a nice use for the inverse we just built.

transformation|det|lattice volumeerror

120 lattice steps per unit, so between one and four million sample points per row — which is why this is tighter than 2.5's pixel count. The determinant does not approximately scale volumes.

And, as in 2-D, determinants multiply: det(A·B) = det(A)·det(B), verified. A chain of transformations scales volume by the product of the individual factors, which is exactly what you would expect from doing one after another.

The 4×4, and the column that does nothing

Now the part this lesson has been walking toward.

We want translation. A 3×3 cannot do it — §1 gave the one-line proof and the harness confirmed it over 500 matrices. The natural move is to make the matrix bigger and put the translation in the extra space. So: four columns of four components each, with the 3×3 sitting in the top-left corner and the translation written into the new fourth column.

Let us do exactly that and watch what happens.

Worked example — the failure, measured

Take a rotation-and-scale R, embed it in a 4×4, and write the translation (1.5, −2, 0.75) into the fourth column. Then transform the point (0.40, −0.30, 0.90), carried as w = 0:

fourth columnresult
left as (0,0,0,1)(0.9882, −0.2700, 0.5235)
set to (1.5, −2, 0.75, 1)(0.9882, −0.2700, 0.5235)
difference(0.000000, 0.000000, 0.000000)

Not approximately zero. Exactly zero, and it would be exactly zero for any translation you cared to write there. The 4×4 gives the same answer as the 3×3 did — verified — so all we have built so far is a larger container for the same transformation.

So the bigger matrix did not help. It is worth being precise about why, because the reason contains the answer.

The precise diagnosis

A matrix multiplication can only ever scale each column by a component of the input and add the results up. That is all it does. To add a constant — the same offset regardless of the input — you would need a column whose multiplier is the same number every time.

Every component of our input varies with the point being transformed. Except one. The fourth one has no job, no meaning and no reason to be anything in particular.

That is where Lesson 2.6 stops.

You may already be able to see the next move — and if so, sit with the question rather than jumping to the answer, because the answer is not the interesting part. The interesting parts are: what should that fourth number be, is it allowed to be different for different kinds of thing, what happens to it when two of these matrices are composed, and what does it mean geometrically? Lesson 2.7 answers all four, and the reason it is a whole lesson rather than a footnote is that the fourth component turns out to be the mechanism behind perspective as well as translation.

Why we built the 4×4 anyway

Because none of it is wasted. mat4 composes correctly, embeds a mat3 faithfully — to_mat4(A)·to_mat4(B) == to_mat4(A·B), verified — and stores its sixteen floats in exactly the layout SDL_GPU and HLSL expect, so Module 4 will upload one with a plain memcpy and no transpose at the boundary. Every line of it becomes useful in Lesson 2.7 without a single change.

There is deliberately no 4×4 determinant or inverse. Both are considerably more work than the 3×3 versions and nothing has needed either; when an inverse is finally wanted, in Lesson 2.9's view matrix, it will be for a matrix of a specific structured form whose inverse can be written down directly and far more cheaply than the general formula.

4Implementation

Four new headers, one modified, and a demo. Most of it you could write yourself from Lesson 2.5 at this point, so this section covers only the decisions — the places where "the same thing, one dimension wider" was not quite the whole story.

4.1  The shape is the same all the way up

Put that beside mat2.hpp and the only difference is the number of terms. That is the payoff for having derived the rules in 2.5 rather than looking them up: there was nothing to re-derive, because none of the arguments counted the axes.

Storage stays column-major for the same three reasons, all now slightly stronger: the columns are the meaningful objects, the layout falls out of naming them rather than being imposed, and it is exactly what SDL_GPU and HLSL constant buffers expect — so Module 4 uploads a mat4 with a plain memcpy. Verified: sizeof(mat3) is nine floats, sizeof(mat4) is sixteen, both with no padding, and the floats come out in column order.

4.2  Adding a type broke an API — in a way worth showing

Lesson 2.5 shipped a free function:

Adding mat3 made that impossible. A second identity() would take the same arguments — none — and differ only in return type, and C++ cannot overload on return type. Not "should not": cannot. The call identity() would be ambiguous with no way for the compiler to guess.

Everything else survived, which is worth noticing because the pattern tells you where the fragility was. transpose, inverse and determinant all take a matrix, so they overload on the parameter type without trouble. rotation versus rotation_x/y/z differ by name. scale(sx, sy) and scale(sx, sy, sz) differ by arity. Only the function with no arguments had nothing to disambiguate it.

A static member names the type at the call site — mat2::identity(), mat3::identity(), mat4::identity() — which turns out to read better than the free function ever did, and scales to as many matrix types as we like.

Lesson 2.5's uniform scale(float) went with it. It was unused, and it would have become a trap the moment somebody wanted a uniform 3-D scale: scale(2.0f) silently meaning "the 2-D one" is exactly the kind of quietly wrong that costs an afternoon. Component counts are now always explicit.

C++ in place — what you can and cannot overload on

C++ picks an overload from the arguments at the call site: their number, their types, and how well each candidate matches. The return type is never consulted, because at the point of decision the compiler may not know what return type is wanted — auto x = identity(); has no answer to give it.

The practical rule this leaves you with: a function that takes no arguments cannot be overloaded at all. If you have one that will need per-type versions later, make it a static member or give it a distinct name now, while it costs nothing to change.

4.3  Writing the 3×3 inverse so it can be checked

The 2×2 inverse was four elements and a memorable shape. The 3×3 is nine cofactors, a transpose and a division, and transcribing it straight into column-stored members is how sign and index errors get in. So the implementation names the elements in written notation first:

Nine extra lines, and now every subsequent expression can be compared directly against any textbook derivation without translating between row and column indexing in your head. The determinant then falls out of the first three cofactors, which we needed anyway — a cofactor expansion along the top row is exactly m00·c00 + m01·c01 + m02·c02.

I will be honest that the first version of this function was wrong. Written directly in terms of c0.x, c1.y and so on, it had two cofactors using the wrong component, and it looked entirely plausible. The harness caught it immediately — M * inverse(M) == I over 300 assorted matrices is not a check you can pass by accident — and the rewrite above is the fix for the class of error rather than the instance.

4.4  What is deliberately missing

Three absences, each a decision:

  • No cross product on vec3. §3.1 gave the reason: in 3-D there is a whole plane of perpendiculars, so getting a specific one needs a second vector, and Lesson 3.4 is where a triangle supplies it. Introducing it now would mean declaring a formula and hoping it lands later.
  • No perpendicular on vec3. Same reason. The 2-D function has no honest generalisation, and providing one that silently picks an arbitrary perpendicular would be worse than not providing it.
  • No 4×4 determinant or inverse. Both are substantially more code than the 3×3 versions and nothing has needed either. Lesson 2.9's view matrix will want an inverse, and it will be for a matrix of a specific structured form whose inverse can be written down directly — far cheaper than the general formula, and far easier to be sure of.

The general principle, worth stating once: a maths library that contains everything anyone might want is one nobody can read and where you cannot tell which parts have been thought about. Every function in src/math/ exists because a lesson needed it, which is also why every one of them has a derivation you can go and find.

4.5  The demo: a cube, and a translation that does nothing

The new view draws a wireframe cube, the three columns of its matrix as arrows, and a readout. Two details are load-bearing.

The cube is centred on the origin, not sitting with a corner there. Rotation is always about the origin (Lesson 2.5 §3.8), so a corner-at-origin cube would swing around in a huge arc rather than spinning in place — which is a fine demonstration of §1's complaint but a poor demonstration of rotation. Centring it is the workaround Exercise 2.5.3 asked you to find unsatisfying, applied here in its simplest form: choose coordinates where the thing you want to rotate about is already the origin.

The projection is "drop z", and that is the entire projection:

Keep x and y, throw the depth away. That is an orthographic projection, and it is enough to see that the transformations work while being deliberately not enough to look right: parallel edges of the cube stay exactly parallel on screen, so the far face is drawn the same size as the near one. Real vision does not do that, which is why the wireframe reads as ambiguous — your eye can flip between two interpretations of which face is nearer.

The edges are drawn dimmer with depth as a reading aid. That is a cue, not a calculation: there is no depth buffer (Lesson 3.1) and no lighting (Lesson 3.6). Making distant things smaller — which is what would actually fix the ambiguity — is Lesson 2.10's subject.

And the punchline is on a key:

T writes a translation into the fourth column, and the displacement it produces is measured by comparing the 4×4's answer against the 3×3's rather than assumed. The HUD prints that measurement, so it reports what actually happened. It reads 0.00 px, and it will keep reading 0.00 px however large a translation you put there.

What you should see

The engine opens on the cube view. A wireframe cube tumbling in place, three coloured arrows growing out of its centre, and a readout on the right.

The arrows are the matrix. Red is where (1,0,0) landed, green where (0,1,0) landed, blue where (0,0,1) landed — the three columns, drawn. Whatever the cube is doing, those three arrows are the complete explanation, and the numbers beside them are the same three columns written out.

Checkpoint — five things to try
  1. Each single-axis rotation. Stop the spin and step through Z. In each case one arrow stays perfectly still — that is the axis — while the other two swing around it. Watch which one.
  2. The determinant. It reads +1.0000 for every rotation at every angle. Rotations move things without changing volume.
  3. The two tumble modes. They use the same two rotations and differ only in which is applied first. Stop the animation, set the same t in both, and compare — the cube sits at a visibly different orientation. That is §3.4, on screen.
  4. Look for the ambiguity. Stare at the tumbling wireframe and let your eye relax. Which face is nearer? The picture genuinely does not say, because orthographic projection has thrown that away. The brightness cue is the only thing stopping it flipping.
  5. Press T. This is the one.

T writes the translation (1.2, 0, 0) into the fourth column of the 4×4 — the obvious place, the place it belongs. The readout turns red to tell you the column is set. And the cube does not move.

The line underneath says how far it moved, and it is measured rather than assumed: the demo transforms a probe point both ways and subtracts. It reads 0.00 px. Turn T on and off a few times and watch nothing at all happen; make the translation larger in the source and watch nothing happen more emphatically.

This is the lesson

A correct translation, correctly placed in a correctly-built 4×4, moving a cube by exactly zero pixels. Every part of that sentence is true at once, and the reason is one multiplication by zero (§3.6).

When you get to Lesson 2.7, this is the demo that should start working — with no change to mat4.hpp at all.

The other four demos are still on Tab: 2.5's basis transform, 2.2–2.4's triangles, 2.1's line fan, and Pong.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Rotation about y goes the wrong way, but x and z are fine — the mirrored minus sign
Symptom
Two of the three axis rotations behave, and the third turns the opposite way. Composing them produces orientations that are almost right and drift further wrong the more rotations you chain.
Cause
R_y transcribed to match the visual shape of Rₓ and R_z, with the minus sign above the diagonal. It belongs below. Rotating about y turns z toward x, and the matrix lists x's row above z's, so the pair appears in the opposite order to the one the rotation acts in (§3.3).
Fix
Derive it from the cycle x → y → z → x rather than from the shape of the other two. To catch it in a test: rotation_y(θ) applied to (0,0,1) must give (sin θ, 0, cos θ) — z swinging toward x, with a positive x component for a positive angle.
An object orbits the origin instead of spinning in place — rotation is always about the origin
Symptom
A model at (40, 0, 12) swings through a huge arc when you rotate it, or leaves the screen entirely.
Cause
Not a bug. Every linear transformation fixes the origin (Lesson 2.5 §3.8, and §1 here), so "rotate" means "rotate about the origin" and always will until Lesson 2.7.
Fix
Either choose model coordinates centred on the thing you want to spin — which is what this lesson's cube does, and what asset pipelines generally arrange — or do M*(v − c) + c by hand and accept that the result is no longer a single matrix. Exercise 2.5.3 explored the cost of the second option; Lesson 2.7 removes it.
Chained rotations give a different result than expected — order, and only in 3-D
Symptom
Rₓ(a) · R_y(b) and R_y(b) · Rₓ(a) disagree, which feels wrong if your intuition was formed in 2-D where rotations simply added.
Cause
Rotations about different axes do not commute (§3.4). About the same axis they still do. In 2-D there is only one axis, which is why the 2-D intuition does not warn you.
Fix
Fix an order, document it, and be consistent — and remember that the rightmost factor is applied first. The test that makes the behaviour concrete rather than mysterious is Figure 3's: use a point lying on one of the two axes, so one of the rotations provably does nothing and the asymmetry is obvious.
A model renders inside-out after mirroring — a negative determinant
Symptom
Not yet — this one is waiting for Lesson 3.4, and it is here so you recognise it. A model scaled by (−1, 1, 1) to mirror it shows its interior: the faces you should see are culled and the ones you should not are drawn.
Cause
A negative determinant reverses handedness (§3.5), which reverses the winding order of every triangle. Back-face culling decides visibility from that winding, so every decision it makes is inverted.
Fix
Detect it — determinant(m) < 0 — and flip the culling mode for that object, which is what real engines do. The general lesson is that the determinant's sign is not trivia: it is a property of the transformation that downstream stages depend on.
The 4×4 quietly behaves like a 3×3 — the fourth component is zero
Symptom
Translations written into the fourth column have no effect whatsoever. Rotation and scale work perfectly. Nothing errors.
Cause
The fourth column is multiplied by the input's fourth component, and that component is zero (§3.6). This is the deliberate state of affairs at the end of this lesson — but it is also a real bug you will hit later, when a point built with w = 0 gets fed through a transform that has a translation in it.
Fix
For now: nothing to fix, this is where the lesson stops. From Lesson 2.7 onward the check is to look at what w you built the vector with — and the reason to_vec4(v, w) is an explicit named function rather than an implicit conversion is precisely so that value is always something somebody chose.

8Exercises

2.6.1Derive the third rotationwarm-up

Cover up rotation_y in the source. Using only the cycle x → y → z → x and the fact that rotating about an axis turns the next two axes one toward the other, write it out. Then check.

Hint

Ask the three questions in order. Where does (1,0,0) go? Where does (0,1,0) go — it is on the axis, so this one is free. Where does (0,0,1) go? Each answer is a column.

Solution

ĵ is the axis, so it stays at (0,1,0) — that is the middle column and it is the identity's. The rotation turns z toward x, so k̂ goes to (sin θ, 0, cos θ) and î, a quarter turn ahead in the cycle, goes to (cos θ, 0, −sin θ).

Written out, that puts the minus sign in the bottom-left rather than the top-right — the mirror image of where it sits in Rₓ and R_z. Deriving it takes thirty seconds and gets it right; copying the shape of the other two takes ten and gets it wrong.

2.6.2Predict the non-commuting resultwarm-up

Take the point (0, 1, 0) — on the y axis this time — and the rotations Rₓ(0.6) and R_y(0.8). Predict both orders in your head before computing anything. Which order leaves the point in a coordinate plane?

Solution

R_y leaves (0,1,0) alone, because the point is on y's axis. So y first wastes that rotation and only Rₓ acts: the point ends at (0, cos 0.6, sin 0.6) = (0, 0.8253, 0.5646), still in the y-z plane.

x first moves it to (0, 0.8253, 0.5646) and then R_y has a non-zero z component to work on, swinging some of it into x — so the result has all three components non-zero.

Same structure as Figure 3, with the roles swapped. The transferable trick: to make a non-commutativity argument concrete, pick a point on one of the axes so that one of the four operations provably does nothing.

2.6.3Measure a volumemedium

Reproduce §3.5's lattice-count measurement, then break it deliberately: use a transformation with determinant zero and see what your counter reports. Explain what happened to the volume, and to the inverse you needed to do the counting.

Hint

A point is inside the transformed unit cube exactly when M⁻¹p has all three components in [0,1). That test needs an inverse. What does inverse return when the determinant is zero, and what does the test then say about every point?

Solution

The count comes out at zero, and for a satisfying reason rather than an accident. A zero-determinant transformation flattens the cube onto a plane — a shape with genuinely zero volume — so "zero" is the right answer to the volume question.

Our inverse returns all zeros in that case, so M⁻¹p is the origin for every p, which passes the [0,1) test — meaning a naive counter would report every sampled point as inside. Whether you get 0 or "everything" depends on how you wrote the bounds, which is the real lesson: a degenerate input does not politely produce a degenerate answer. Check determinant(m) != 0 before relying on an inverse, every time.

2.6.4Make the fourth column work — the wrong waymedium

Without changing mat4.hpp, get the demo's cube to actually translate. Then list everything wrong with what you did.

Hint

§3.6 says the fourth column is multiplied by the input's fourth component. You control that component at the call site.

Solution sketch

Pass to_vec4(p, 1.0f) instead of 0.0f and it works immediately — the cube moves by exactly the translation, verified in the harness.

Now the problems, and they are the agenda for Lesson 2.7. Why 1? What justifies it beyond "it makes the arithmetic come out"? Should a direction — a surface normal, a velocity — also carry 1, and if not, what breaks? What is w after two of these matrices are composed and applied; is it still 1, and is it guaranteed? And is there any circumstance where w comes out as something other than 0 or 1 — and if so, what could that possibly mean?

You can make it work today. Being able to answer those four questions is what Lesson 2.7 is for, and the fourth one is the door to perspective projection.

2.6.5Why a wireframe cube is ambiguousopen

Turn off the depth-brightness cue and watch the cube tumble. It should become genuinely ambiguous — your eye flips between two readings. Work out precisely what information is missing, then implement the cheapest fix you can that is not perspective projection.

Hint

Orthographic projection throws away z. List everything your visual system normally uses to recover depth, then ask which of those a computer can supply from the data we still have.

Solution sketch

The figure you are seeing is a Necker cube, and its ambiguity is a real property of the projection rather than a failing of the renderer: two different 3-D cubes produce exactly this image, and nothing in it distinguishes them.

Cheap fixes, roughly in order of cost: the brightness cue we ship (one line, works surprisingly well); drawing near edges thicker; hidden-line removal, which is correct and much more work; and painter's-algorithm face filling, which is Lesson 3.1's z-buffer arriving early and badly.

The real fix is perspective — making distant things smaller, so the far face is drawn visibly smaller than the near one and the two readings stop being equally consistent. That is Lesson 2.10, and it is worth noticing now that perspective is not merely a cosmetic effect: it is the projection that puts back the information orthographic discards.

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • It is three-dimensional. vec3, vec4, mat3 and mat4 exist, and the first genuinely 3-D thing this engine has drawn is on screen and spinning.
  • Nothing had to be re-derived. Every argument in Lesson 2.5 was made without counting the axes, so all of them held. That is what a good derivation buys you: the next dimension is transcription, not rework.
  • The three rotations come from the cycle x → y → z → x, which puts R_y's minus sign where it actually belongs rather than where the other two would suggest.
  • Rotations do not commute — a genuinely new fact in 3-D — and the sharpest demonstration uses a point lying on one of the axes so that one rotation provably does nothing.
  • The determinant is a volume factor whose sign is handedness, measured to within 0.01% by counting lattice points inside the transformed cube.
  • And the 4×4 does not translate. A correct translation in the correct column moves the cube by exactly zero pixels, because that column is multiplied by a component that is zero.

The habit this lesson is really about: when the next case is a special case of an argument you already made, do not remake the argument — check that it never depended on what changed. Lesson 2.5's proof mentioned "the basis vectors" and never said how many. That is why this lesson is short and why its code is a transcription.

Next: 2.7 — Homogeneous Coordinates and What w Really Means. The fourth component gets a job. Translation falls inside the matrix, chains compose into a single object, and Exercise 2.5.3's awkward (M₂M₁, M₂t₁ + t₂) bookkeeping disappears entirely.

And then the part that makes w worth a whole lesson rather than a footnote: the same fourth component, allowed to be something other than 0 or 1, turns out to be the mechanism behind perspective. Lesson 2.10 could not exist without it.

decisions:
  - identity() moved to a STATIC MEMBER on every matrix type. Forced: a free
    identity() takes no arguments, so mat2's and mat3's could differ only by return
    type. Everything else survived (transpose/inverse/determinant overload on the
    parameter; rotation vs rotation_x/y/z differ by name; scale differs by arity) —
    the function with NOTHING to disambiguate it was the one that broke. General
    rule now: a zero-argument function cannot be overloaded at all, so make it a
    static member or give it a distinct name while that is still free.
  - mat2's uniform scale(float) REMOVED. Unused, and it would have become a trap
    the moment someone wanted a uniform 3-D scale: scale(2.0f) silently meaning
    "the 2-D one". Component counts are always explicit now.
  - mat3::inverse names its elements in WRITTEN notation (m00..m22) before doing
    anything. The first draft transcribed the adjugate straight into column members
    and had two cofactors using the wrong component — plausible-looking and wrong.
    The rewrite fixes the CLASS of error, not the instance. M*inverse(M)==I over
    300 matrices is the check that caught it.
  - vec3 has NO perpendicular() and NO cross(). In 3-D there is a whole plane of
    perpendiculars, so the 2-D function has no honest generalisation; getting a
    specific one needs a second vector, which is exactly what cross takes and
    exactly why 3.4 introduces it when a triangle supplies one.
  - to_vec4(v, w) is an explicit named function, NOT an implicit conversion —
    otherwise a vec3 silently acquires a w nobody chose, which is the precise shape
    of the bug 2.7 is about to spend a lesson on.

    a corner-at-origin cube would orbit rather than spin. That is the "choose
