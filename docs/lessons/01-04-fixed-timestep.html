Module 1 — The Loop, the Pixel, and Time · Lesson 1.4

The Fixed Timestep with Interpolation, Derived

Lesson 1.3 ended with a ball that gains energy from nowhere, at a rate decided by your monitor. The cure is to stop letting the hardware choose how far the simulation advances — which sounds obvious and turns out to need four separate ideas, each one fixing what the previous one broke. We derive all four, in order, and end with the loop this engine keeps for the rest of the course.

1The Problem

We finished 1.3 with a precise diagnosis. Scaling movement by dt is exact for constant velocity, and only first-order for anything that accelerates — the error is ½·g·T·h, proportional to the step. Since the step is the frame time, the frame rate is an input to the physics.

The demo made that concrete: a perfectly elastic ball, whose energy therefore cannot change, climbing higher after every bounce at a rate that depended entirely on the frame rate.

Frame rateApex height after each bounce (px from the top; it was dropped from 260)
2000 fps259, 258, 257 — barely drifting
60 fps236, 204, 171 — visibly climbing
20 fps188, 83, −34 — off the top of the window on the third bounce

Three different games. And notice what is not wrong here: the collision code is fine, the gravity constant is fine, the arithmetic is fine. The defect is structural. We asked "how long did the last frame take?" and used the answer to decide how far the world should move, and those two quantities have nothing to do with one another. One is a property of the physics we designed. The other is a property of the machine, the driver, the thermal state of the laptop, and whether a browser is open.

So this lesson removes the frame rate from the simulation entirely. The simulation will advance in steps of a size we choose, identical on every machine, and rendering will happen whenever the machine is ready — separately, and at whatever rate it likes.

That is the whole idea, and stated like that it sounds like a five-line change. It is not, because each of the obvious implementations breaks something new: the first runs the game at the wrong speed, the second stutters visibly, and the third can lock your application solid. We will meet all three, in that order, and the loop we end with is the one that survives.

2Building Intuition

2.1 The projector and the actors

A film camera exposes 24 frames every second. That rate is a property of the camera, chosen by people, and it has nothing to do with how fast the actors moved. The actors' motion is continuous and real; the camera samples it 24 times a second. If you later transfer the film to a projector that runs at 25, the actors do not start acting differently — you are simply sampling the same performance at a different rate.

That separation is exactly what we want. The simulation is the performance: it evolves by rules we wrote, on a schedule we control. Rendering is the camera: it samples whatever the simulation currently looks like, as often as the hardware allows. Right now our engine has them fused — it is as if the actors could only move when the shutter clicked, so a faster camera made everyone move faster.

Once separated, two questions appear that did not exist before, and the rest of the lesson is those two questions:

  • How many simulation steps should a given frame run? Real time does not arrive in neat 16.67 ms parcels, so the answer is usually "not a whole number".
  • What exactly do we draw, given that the moment we are drawing almost never coincides with a completed simulation step?

2.2 Paying a debt in fixed coins

Here is the picture to keep for the first question. Every frame, real time hands you some money — 16.4 ms, then 9.2 ms, then 41 ms, whatever the machine managed. The simulation only accepts payment in coins of one fixed denomination: 16.67 ms each.

So you keep a purse. Each frame you add what you were given, then hand over as many whole coins as the purse can cover — sometimes none, sometimes one, occasionally three. What is left over is smaller than a coin, so it stays in the purse and counts towards next frame.

Two things fall straight out of that picture, and both matter enormously:

  • No time is ever lost. The leftover is carried, not discarded, so over any stretch the simulation advances by the real elapsed time, to within one coin.
  • There is always a leftover. It is what remains after taking out whole coins, so it lives strictly between zero and one coin — never more, never negative.

That leftover is the answer to the second question too, though it takes §3.3 to see why. It measures how far past the last completed step the current moment is — and if you know you are 40% of the way from one step to the next, you know how to draw the in-between.

3The Theory

3.1 Attempt one: just use a constant

The most direct reading of "the simulation should not depend on the frame rate" is to stop passing dt in:

The physics is now genuinely machine-independent: every step is identical, so the ball's trajectory is identical everywhere. We have solved the problem in the title of §1.

And the game is now unplayable, because it runs at the wrong speed. Each frame advances the world by 1/60 of a second regardless of how much time actually passed, so the simulation's clock runs at (frame rate ÷ 60) times real time.

Worked example — the simulation's clock versus the wall clock

After ten seconds of real time, with h = 1/60 s:

Frame rateFrames in 10 sSimulated timeResult
30 fps300300 × 1/60 = 5.0 sHalf speed — slow motion
60 fps600600 × 1/60 = 10.0 sCorrect, by coincidence
144 fps14401440 × 1/60 = 24.0 s2.4× too fast

This is the same 4.8× spread between 30 and 144 fps that Lesson 1.3 opened with. We have not fixed the bug; we have moved it from "different physics" to "different speed", which is arguably worse because it affects everything uniformly and obviously.

So we need both properties at once: every step the same size (for identical physics) and simulated time tracking real time (for correct speed). The only free variable left is the number of steps per frame.

3.2 Attempt two: the accumulator

Which is §2.2's purse. Bank the elapsed real time; spend it in whole steps; carry the change.

Read the inner loop carefully, because it is doing something people find surprising the first time: a frame may run zero steps, or one, or several. A machine rendering faster than the simulation rate will frequently have nothing to simulate at all — it renders the same state twice, which is correct. A machine that stalled will run several steps to catch up.

Two properties are worth stating precisely, because everything later depends on them.

The invariant. After the inner loop finishes, 0 ≤ accumulator < h. The loop condition guarantees it: it runs for as long as a whole step is affordable, so it can only exit when one is not. The accumulator is therefore bounded — which, incidentally, is why it may safely be a float, even though Lesson 1.3 insisted that absolute time must be a Uint64. That rule was about quantities that grow without limit. This one is emptied every frame by construction.

No time is lost or invented. Every unit of real time added to the accumulator either becomes a simulation step or stays banked for the next frame. So simulated time tracks real time to within one step, permanently — the property Figure 1 shows attempt one lacking.

The physics is now identical on every machine and runs at the right speed. If we stopped here we would have most of what people mean by "fixed timestep", and the engine would be measurably better than it was an hour ago.

And it would visibly stutter.

3.3 The leftover, and the judder it causes

Here is the part most treatments skate over, so we will do it with numbers.

render() draws the simulation's current state. That state is the result of the last completed step — which happened at some moment in the past, because the leftover in the accumulator is precisely the time that has elapsed since. So every frame is drawn stale, by an amount equal to the accumulator.

Staleness by itself is not the problem. A constant delay is imperceptible: everything is uniformly 8 ms behind and nobody can tell. The problem is that the staleness varies — between zero and one full step — and it varies in a pattern that has nothing to do with the object's real motion.

Worked example — a 60 Hz simulation rendered at 100 fps

h = 16.667 ms, frames arriving every 10 ms. Following the accumulator frame by frame:

FrameReal timeBankedStepsLeftoverSimulated toRender is stale by

The step pattern is 0, 1, 0, 1, 1 and then repeats — 3 steps every 5 frames, which is right, since 5 × 10 ms = 50 ms = 3 × 16.67 ms. Over twelve frames you get seven steps.

Now read the last column as an animation. A box moving at a constant 360 px/s is drawn 3.6 px behind, then 1.2, then 4.8, then 2.4, then 0, then 3.6 again. Its true velocity is perfectly constant; its drawn velocity lurches between zero and double, five times a second. That is the judder, and it is not a bug in the simulation — the simulation is exactly right. It is a sampling artifact, introduced entirely by drawing a stale state without accounting for how stale it is.

Notice the trap this sets. The judder appears the moment you adopt a fixed timestep, so it is easy to conclude the fixed timestep was a mistake and go back. It was not — the fix is one more idea, and it is the reason this technique is always described as "fixed timestep with interpolation" rather than just "fixed timestep".

3.4 Interpolation: rendering between two states

We know exactly how stale the render is: it is the accumulator. We know how long a step is: it is h. So we know the render's position between two simulation steps as a fraction:

An alpha of 0 means a step has just completed. An alpha of 0.75 means three quarters of the way from the last completed step towards the next one. And if we keep the state from before the last step as well as after it, we can draw the position in between by simple linear interpolation:

Now the claim that makes the whole technique work, and it is worth proving rather than asserting.

Worked example — interpolation turns a varying lag into a constant one

Write T for real elapsed time and S for simulated time. By the accumulator's construction:

  • T = S + accumulator — real time is simulated time plus what is still banked.
  • previous is the state at time S − h; current is at S.
  • Interpolating by alpha draws the state at (S − h) + alpha·h = S − h + accumulator.
  • Substituting S + accumulator = T, the drawn moment is exactly T − h.

The accumulator cancels. Whatever it happens to be — 0.1 ms or 16 ms — the render depicts the world exactly one fixed step behind real time. Not approximately: exactly, every frame. Confirming it against §3.3's table:

FrameStale by, rawStale by, interpolated

313.33 ms16.67 ms

610.00 ms16.67 ms

We have traded a lag that swings between 0 and 16.67 ms for one that is always 16.67 ms. On average the interpolated version is further behind — and it looks dramatically better, because human vision is exquisitely sensitive to changes in velocity and almost blind to a constant delay. Smoothness is consistency, not immediacy.

Drag the slider below through the frame rates. Watch the red dots leave the true line by different amounts at different frame rates — and watch the interpolated dots stay on their parallel line no matter what you do.

Green is the true position of a box at constant speed. Red is what gets drawn without interpolation — uneven spacing is judder. Amber is with interpolation: evenly spaced, on the dashed line one step below the truth. Simulation is fixed at 60 Hz throughout.

Why not extrapolate instead, and render at T exactly?

You can: let alpha exceed 1 and project past current, removing the one-step lag. The catch is that extrapolation guesses at a future that has not been simulated, and it guesses wrong at every discontinuity — a collision, a direction change, a button press. The object visibly overshoots and snaps back, and rubber-banding is far more objectionable than a steady 16 ms delay. Interpolation only ever draws between two states that actually happened, so it can never be wrong about anything. Competitive games sometimes accept extrapolation's artifacts to claw back latency; we do not, and if you ever want to, do it as a deliberate decision rather than by forgetting to clamp alpha.

3.5 The spiral of death, and the guard

One flaw remains, and it is the one that takes down applications rather than merely making them ugly. Look again at the inner loop:

Simulating a step takes real time. If simulating h seconds of game costs more than h seconds of reality, then every step you run adds more to next frame's debt than it removes from this one.

Worked example — the runaway

A 60 Hz simulation, so each step represents 16.67 ms of game time. Suppose the scene has grown heavy and one step costs 20 ms of real time to compute:

  • Each step simulates 16.67 ms but consumes 20 ms.
  • Net: the engine falls 20 − 16.67 = 3.33 ms further behind per step.
  • Falling behind means more steps owed next frame, which consumes more real time, which means falling further behind.

Frame time grows without bound; the window stops responding while the CPU works flat out. The condition is simply cost per step ÷ time simulated per step > 1 — here 20 / 16.67 = 1.20. At exactly 1.0 the engine is on a knife edge; below it, any backlog is eventually paid off. This is the spiral of death.

There are two places to intervene, and we already have one of them.

Clamping dt before it enters the accumulator is the defence Lesson 1.3 built without naming it. clock::dt() caps a frame at 0.25 s, so at h = 1/60 no single frame can ever ask for more than 0.25 / 0.0167 = 15 steps. That alone bounds the work per frame, and it is why the clamp existed before we needed it.

Capping the steps per frame is the second, and it protects against the case the clamp cannot: a machine that is simply too slow, frame after frame, where each individual dt is small and legitimate but the backlog still grows. Past the cap we discard the outstanding time. The simulation then falls behind real time permanently — the game runs in slow motion — which is a genuine loss, not a repair. Our implementation records exactly how much time was thrown away, because a silent slow-motion mode is a support ticket nobody can diagnose.

3.6 Choosing h — and what determinism really buys

The step size is now a design decision rather than an accident, so it deserves one.

RateStepCharacter
30 Hz33.3 msCheap. Visible integration error; fast objects tunnel; input can sit for a third of a frame. Common on consoles for gameplay logic, with physics run separately.
60 Hz16.7 msThe default, and ours. Matches the commonest refresh rate, so alpha is usually near 0 or 1 and the interpolation lag is one frame on the majority of machines.
120 Hz8.3 msNoticeably better physics — remember the error is proportional to h — for double the simulation cost. A good choice for physics-heavy games.
240+ Hz≤4.2 msDiminishing returns for most gameplay, and the spiral gets much easier to trigger because each step buys so little time.

Notice the connection back to 1.3: explicit Euler's error is ½·g·T·h, and h is now a number in our source code. We have not made the integrator accurate — we have made its inaccuracy a constant we control instead of a property of the player's hardware. The demo shows exactly this: at 10 Hz the ball still flies out of the window, but it does so identically on every machine. Fixing the integrator itself is Module 7's work.

What a fixed timestep does and does not guarantee

"Fixed timestep gives you determinism" is true in a narrower sense than it sounds, and the difference matters if you ever attempt replays or lockstep multiplayer.

What you get: the same binary, on the same machine, fed the same sequence of inputs at the same simulation steps, produces bit-identical results — regardless of frame rate, background load, or how long you spent at a breakpoint. That is a real and valuable property. It makes a bug reproducible, and it is what makes an input-log replay possible at all.

What you do not get: identical results across different machines, compilers, or optimisation levels. Floating-point results can differ through fused multiply-add contraction, x87 80-bit intermediates, different libm implementations of sin/cos, and vectorisation changing the order of a summation. A fixed timestep is necessary for cross-machine determinism and nowhere near sufficient; games that need it use fixed-point arithmetic or a tightly constrained floating-point subset. Do not promise lockstep on the strength of this lesson alone.

4Implementation: the accumulator, and two states

Two pieces: a type that owns the accumulator, and a change in how the demo stores its state.

4.1 Why the accumulator gets its own type

The loop from §3.2 is four lines. Making it a class is not ceremony; it is because those four lines have an invariant that one careless edit destroys:

Forget accumulator_ -= h_ in a hand-written loop and the condition never becomes false: the application hangs inside a single frame, with no crash and no output. Putting the subtraction inside the type that owns the accumulator means no call site can skip it. The invariant "after the loop, 0 ≤ accumulator < h" then holds by construction, and alpha() can rely on it.

The guard clause deserves its shape too. On hitting the cap it does not simply stop — it drains the remaining whole steps into dropped_. If it just returned, the accumulator would stay above h, alpha would exceed 1, and the renderer would silently start extrapolating at exactly the moment the machine is already struggling.

Two smaller decisions are worth naming. clamp_rate refuses a rate that would give a zero or negative step, because a zero step is not a slow simulation — it is a loop whose condition can never go false. Written as if (!(rate_hz >= k_min_rate)) rather than if (rate_hz < k_min_rate), it also catches NaN, since every comparison against NaN is false. And alpha() clamps its result to 1: the invariant makes that unreachable in normal use, but a value above 1 turns a lerp into extrapolation without any error, and one comparison removes the possibility.

4.2 Two states, and what they cost

Interpolation needs the state as it was one step ago as well as now. That has a real architectural consequence: everything the renderer draws must exist twice.

Until now the demo's state was a handful of loose local variables. It cannot stay that way: "the previous value of everything" is only expressible if "everything" is a thing with a name. This is the first time the course has been pushed towards grouping state by an external requirement rather than by taste, and it is a small preview of Module 5, where the same pressure — applied to thousands of entities instead of two floats — produces the ECS.

C++ in place — value semantics, and why previous = current is enough

Assigning one struct to another performs a member-wise copy: the compiler generates an assignment operator that copies each member in turn. After previous = current; the two are independent objects that merely happen to hold equal values — changing one cannot affect the other.

That is the same distinction Lesson 1.2 drew when we refused to store SDL's keyboard pointer, and it is doing the same work here. If previous and current were pointers or references to one object, lerp(previous, current, alpha) would interpolate between a value and itself, returning that value for every alpha — and you would have written all of §3.4 for nothing, with no error message to show for it.

Two conveniences come along for free. The = 0.0f in the struct are default member initialisers, so sim_state current; is fully initialised with no constructor to write — no uninitialised float can reach the simulation. And current = sim_state{}; resets every field to those defaults in one line, which is exactly what the demo's reset key does.

4.3 The loop, in final form

Three details in that fragment are load-bearing.

previous = current lives inside the loop, not before it. A frame may take several steps; previous must end up holding the second-newest state, not the state from the start of the frame. Hoisting it out is a real bug that only appears when the frame rate drops below the simulation rate — so it works perfectly on the developer's machine and stutters on everyone else's.

step_simulation receives stepper.h(), never clk.dt(). The simulation has no way to find out how long the frame took, which is the entire point of the exercise expressed as a function signature.

Rate changes happen before begin_frame. set_rate deliberately keeps whatever is in the accumulator — that is real time the simulation still owes, and discarding it would make every rate change a small silent time skip — so the new step size must be settled before the stepping starts.

4.4 A demo you can see all three behaviours in

Three boxes, one speed, three rules — plus the ball from 1.3, now on the fixed step:

BoxRuleWhat it demonstrates
CoralVariable dt (Lesson 1.3)Perfectly smooth — and a different simulation on every machine, every run.
AmberFixed step, drawn rawIdentical everywhere, and it judders (§3.3).
SageFixed step, interpolatedIdentical everywhere and smooth. The goal.

The box bounces off the window edges rather than wrapping around, and that is a deliberate choice with a lesson in it. Wrapping is a teleport: the position jumps from one edge to the other in a single step, and interpolating across that jump would draw the box sliding smoothly backwards across the entire screen over one frame. Interpolation assumes the two states it is given are a short continuous motion apart. Bouncing keeps position continuous; wrapping does not. Exercise 1.4.4 asks you to try it and watch.

The readout exposes the mechanism, because an accumulator you cannot see is an accumulator you cannot debug:

steps flickering between 0, 1 and 2 is the accumulator doing its job. alpha sweeping through [0,1) is the leftover. And behind — real elapsed time minus simulated time — should sit near one step and stay there. If it climbs, the step cap is firing and the simulation is losing ground permanently, which is the one failure in this lesson that no amount of interpolation will hide.

6Build & Run

What you should see

Three boxes tracking left and right, a ball bouncing between two guide lines, and a readout. Work through these in order — the whole lesson is visible in about ninety seconds:

  1. Press 3 for a 60 Hz simulation, then V to enable vsync. All three boxes look about the same, because at 60 fps rendering a 60 Hz simulation almost every frame runs exactly one step. This is the configuration in which the bug is invisible — which is precisely why developers ship it.
  2. Press 1 for 10 Hz. Now the amber box lurches — it jumps once every six frames and sits still in between — while the sage box glides at a constant, even speed. Same simulation, same numbers, same frames. The only difference is alpha. That is §3.3 and §3.4, side by side.
  3. Watch alpha in the readout while you do it. At 10 Hz it sweeps smoothly from 0 to 1 and resets, six times a second, with steps reading 0 for five frames and 1 for the sixth.
  4. Now the payoff: press 3, then hold T. The frame rate collapses to about 20, and the ball keeps bouncing to exactly the same heights it did at thousands of frames per second. In Lesson 1.3 the same key threw it out of the window in three bounces. The frame rate is no longer an input to the physics.
  5. Press 1 and watch the ball instead. At a 10 Hz step it escapes the window again — because the integration error is proportional to h, and h is now large. The difference is that this is our choice, identical for every player, rather than a property of their hardware.
Checkpoint — verified before publishing

Built warning-clean at -Wall -Wextra with Apple Clang against SDL 3.4.12; runs and shuts down cleanly. fixed_step was then driven through a test harness covering fifteen assertions, including:

  • A 60 Hz simulation fed 10 ms frames consumes 7 steps over 12 frames, in an uneven pattern — §3.3's table, reproduced by the real code.
  • alpha stayed within [0, 1) across 700 frames of deliberately irregular durations including zero-length ones.
  • After 6 s of simulated running, simulated time trailed real time by less than one step.
  • A worst-case 0.25 s frame (the clock's clamp ceiling) capped at 8 steps, reported 116.67 ms discarded — exactly seven steps' worth — and still produced a valid alpha.
  • One hundred consecutive one-second frames — a machine hopelessly behind — still ran exactly 8 steps each, terminating every time.
  • set_rate preserved banked time across a rate change, and rates of 0 and NaN were clamped to a finite step rather than producing an unterminating loop.

The ball's frame-rate independence was modelled before it was coded: at a 60 Hz step, apex heights are 236, 204, 171, 137 at render rates of 2000, 240, 60 and 20 fps — identical to the digit. Changing the simulation rate does change them (10 Hz: 56, −196; 240 Hz: 253, 245, 238), which is the integrator error of §3.6, now under our control.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

The window freezes solid under load, CPU pinned — not a hang; unbounded work
Symptom
Under a heavy scene the application stops responding entirely. It is not deadlocked — a profiler shows it working hard — and it never recovers.
Cause
The spiral of death (§3.5): each step costs more real time than it simulates, so the backlog grows every frame and the inner loop runs longer each time round.
Fix
Both guards. Clamp dt before it enters the accumulator (clock does this at 0.25 s) and cap the steps per frame. Then surface the dropped time — an engine silently in slow motion is much harder to diagnose than one that says so.
Motion stutters after switching to a fixed timestep — it looked better before
Symptom
Objects that moved smoothly with variable dt now advance in visible jerks, worst when the frame rate is not a multiple of the simulation rate.
Cause
You are rendering the raw simulation state. It is stale by the accumulator's contents, which varies from 0 to h every frame, and varying lateness reads as stutter (§3.3).
Fix
Interpolate: keep the previous state and draw lerp(previous, current, alpha). Do not conclude the fixed timestep was a mistake — this stutter is the expected intermediate state, and it is why the technique's full name includes "with interpolation".
Interpolation seems to do nothing — the code is right and it still judders
Symptom
lerp(previous, current, alpha) is called correctly, alpha looks sensible in the debugger, and the motion is unchanged.
Cause
previous and current are the same object — aliased through a pointer or reference, or previous assigned after the step instead of before. lerp(x, x, alpha) returns x for every alpha, silently.
Fix
Two distinct struct values, copied with previous = current; immediately before the step. To confirm the diagnosis, print both — if they are never different, that is the bug in one line.
Teleporting objects slide across the screen — respawns and portals animate
Symptom
An object that should jump instantly — a respawn, a portal, a screen wrap, a camera cut — glides smoothly from its old position to its new one over one frame.
Cause
Interpolation assumes the two states are a short continuous motion apart. A teleport violates that, so the lerp faithfully draws the intervening positions, which never existed.
Fix
Snap previous to current whenever a discontinuity happens, so there is nothing to interpolate. Production engines carry an explicit "teleported this step" flag on transforms for exactly this. Notice this is a positive argument for bouncing rather than wrapping in the demo.
A frame hangs forever with no crash — one frame never ends
Symptom
The application locks up in the first frame, or immediately after a settings change. Breaking in the debugger lands you inside the step loop, every time.
Cause
Either the accumulator subtraction was skipped or omitted, or h is zero or negative — from a rate of 0, a division by an uninitialised variable, or a NaN that made every comparison false. The loop condition can then never become false.
Fix
Keep the subtraction inside the type that owns the accumulator, and validate the rate on the way in — writing the check as if (!(rate >= min)) so that NaN is caught too.
Physics is stable at high frame rates and wrong at low ones — the fixed step did not fix it
Symptom
After adopting a fixed timestep, results still differ between machines — or the simulation behaves differently when a scene is heavy.
Cause
Something is still being fed the frame's real duration instead of h. The usual culprits are effects, camera smoothing, animation, or a system added later by someone who reached for the nearest available dt.
Fix
Give the simulation's entry point a signature that takes h and nothing else, so the frame duration is not in scope. If the step cap is also firing, the simulation is genuinely falling behind — that shows up as the behind figure climbing, not as a physics bug.

8Exercises

1.4.1Make the judder unmistakablewarm-up

Enable vsync, set the simulation to 10 Hz, and watch the amber and sage boxes for thirty seconds. Count how many rendered frames pass between amber's jumps. Predict the number first, from your refresh rate and the simulation rate.

Solution

At 60 fps rendering a 10 Hz simulation, one step is taken every sixth frame, so amber holds still for five frames and then jumps six frames' worth of distance at once. Sage covers the same ground at a constant rate, because alpha sweeps 0 → 1 across those six frames and the lerp fills in the positions between. Both boxes are at the identical simulated position at every step boundary — they arrive together, and only the journey differs.

1.4.2Break it on purposewarm-up

Move previous = current; from inside the step loop to just before it. Rebuild and run at 60 Hz simulation with vsync on — it will look perfect. Now hold T. What happens, and why did the bug hide until then?

Solution

With at most one step per frame the two placements are identical, so the bug is invisible in the common case. Under the throttle a frame runs several steps, and previous now holds the state from the start of the frame rather than one step back — so it is several steps behind current and the lerp interpolates across a gap far larger than alpha assumes. The sage box lags and rubber-bands.

The lesson is about the failure's shape, not the fix: it is invisible on a fast machine and only appears on slow ones or under load. That is the signature of an entire family of timestep bugs, and it is why the throttle key is worth keeping in the demo permanently.

1.4.3Measure the spiralguided

Add an artificial cost to step_simulation — SDL_Delay(20) at 60 Hz — so each step costs more real time than it simulates. Watch steps, behind, and the dropped-time log. Then remove the step cap (raise it to 10000) and watch what happens instead. Have a way to kill the process ready.

Hint

20 / 16.67 = 1.2, so the engine should fall about 3.3 ms further behind per step. With the cap in place the loss is bounded and reported; without it, it is not.

Solution

With the cap: steps pins at 8, the dropped-time log fires every frame, and behind climbs steadily — the simulation is in permanent slow motion, but the window still responds and you can quit normally. Without the cap: frame time grows every frame, the window stops responding within seconds, and only the process manager gets you out.

Both are failures. The difference is that one of them lets the player quit, tells you what happened in the log, and would let a real game drop quality settings and recover — the guard does not save the frame, it preserves your ability to respond to losing it.

1.4.4Interpolate across a teleportguided

Change the box from bouncing to wrapping: when box_x passes the right edge, set it to -k_box_size instead of reversing. Run at 10 Hz. What does the sage box do that the amber box does not, and what is the general fix?

Solution

Amber vanishes at the right edge and reappears at the left, which is correct. Sage slides rapidly backwards across the whole window, because previous is at the right edge, current is at the left, and the lerp dutifully draws the positions in between — positions the simulation never occupied. At 10 Hz the slide takes a tenth of a second and is impossible to miss.

The general fix is to tell the renderer that no interpolation is meaningful this step, by setting previous = current at the moment of the teleport. Every engine that interpolates carries some version of this flag; in Unity it is Rigidbody.position versus MovePosition, and the same trap catches people there for the same reason.

1.4.5Prove the determinism you actually haveopen

Record a run: log the simulation state every 60 steps to a file, along with the total step count. Run twice — once uncapped, once with the throttle held for part of the run — and diff the logs. They should be identical. Then find something that does break it.

Hint

The demo has no input affecting the simulation, so two runs are only comparable if you do not press the rate keys. To break determinism deliberately, feed anything frame-dependent into the simulation: clk.dt(), the frame counter, or the wall clock.

Solution sketch

The logs match exactly, including under the throttle, because the simulation only ever sees h. That is a genuinely useful property: a bug that reproduces at step 4211 reproduces at step 4211 every time, whatever the frame rate — which is what makes an input-log replay viable at all.

Then break it. Pass clk.dt() into step_simulation alongside h and use it for anything at all, and the two runs diverge immediately. A subtler version: make the *number* of something depend on the frame rate — spawn a particle per frame rather than per step — and watch the two runs stay identical in physics but differ in content.

Worth doing afterwards: re-read §3.6's warning and convince yourself the logs would not necessarily match on a different machine or compiler. What you have demonstrated is same-binary determinism, which is real and valuable and is not lockstep.

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • Its simulation is independent of the hardware. Steps are a fixed size we chose; the frame rate cannot reach the physics. The ball bounces to the same heights at 2000 fps and at 20.
  • Simulated time tracks real time, to within one step, at any frame rate — the property attempt one lacked and the accumulator restored.
  • It renders smoothly anyway, by interpolating between the last two states. A lag that swung from 0 to h is now exactly h, every frame, and constant lateness is invisible.
  • It cannot spiral. Two bounds — the clamp on dt and the cap on steps — turn an unbounded failure into a bounded, reported one.
  • It is deterministic in the sense you can actually rely on, and you know precisely which sense that is.

The idea to carry forward: simulation time and render time are different clocks, and alpha is the bridge between them. Everything in this lesson follows from taking that separation seriously — including the judder, which is what you get when you separate them and then forget to build the bridge.

This is the loop the engine keeps. Systems will be added inside it for the next eighty lessons — a renderer, an ECS, physics, animation, audio — but its shape does not change again.

Next: 1.5 — The Framebuffer: Your First Owned Pixels. Time is solved; now we take over drawing. So far every pixel has been SDL's doing. We allocate a block of memory, decide what every byte in it means, hand it to the GPU through a streaming texture, and write putpixel — the first of our own. From there everything on screen for the next two modules is code you wrote.

10Further Reading

  • Gaffer on Games Fix Your Timestep! — Glenn Fiedler's article, the origin of the phrase "spiral of death" and the canonical presentation of this loop. Read it now that you have derived the thing: the value is in seeing the same four ideas arrive in the same order, from someone who had to discover them.
  • Article Robert Nystrom, "Game Loop" from Game Programming Patterns — the same problem framed as a design pattern, with the intermediate variants laid out side by side. Free online, and a good second angle on §3.1 through §3.4.
  • Book Jason Gregory, Game Engine Architecture — the game loop and time chapter, which goes on to the things we have not needed yet: pausing, time scaling, single-stepping, and multiple independent clocks for gameplay, effects and the debug camera.
  • Article Deterministic Lockstep — the follow-up that takes §3.6's warning seriously, and shows what cross-machine determinism actually costs once you want it.
  • Reference Bruce Dawson, "Floating-Point Determinism" — why the same source can give different float results on different machines: FMA contraction, x87 intermediates, and library differences. The rigorous version of the warning in §3.6.
