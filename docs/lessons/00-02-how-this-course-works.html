1The Problem

Here is the plan you did not agree to. Modules 1 through 3 — 132 hours, a third of the course — build a software rasterizer: a renderer that computes every pixel on the CPU. Then Module 4 opens by putting it aside and starting again on the GPU.

No 3D game has shipped with a software rasterizer as its primary renderer since roughly Quake II. The technique is, in the commercial sense, dead. And I want a third of your course on it.

If that does not raise an eyebrow, raise one deliberately. Because here is what happens if I simply assert that it is worth it and move on:

The failure mode: skimming to "the real stuff"

You do not quite believe Module 2 matters. So you read it rather than do it — you follow the derivations with your eyes, you skip the worked examples because the arithmetic looks tedious, and you copy the rasterizer code rather than writing it. You reach Module 4 in half the time. You feel efficient.

Then SDL_GPU asks you to fill in a SDL_GPUDepthStencilState, and you do not know what compare_op should be, because you never wrote a depth test — you read about one. So you copy a struct from a tutorial. It works. You do not know why.

Now every bug is a wall. Not because the GPU is hard, but because you have no model of what it is doing, and a GPU gives you no feedback — no printf, no stack trace, just a black screen with no error. The people who find that stage impossible are, almost without exception, the people who skipped the stage that builds the model.

So this lesson is not throat-clearing. It is the argument that earns the next hundred hours — and if the argument fails, you should not spend them. I do not want your trust. I want your agreement, which is a sturdier thing, because trust evaporates at 2 a.m. in front of a black screen and agreement does not.

2Building Intuition

2.1 The dissection argument

Medical students dissect cadavers. Nobody objects that the patient cannot be saved. The dissection is not treatment — it is the hours in which anatomy stops being a diagram in a book and becomes a thing the student's hands know. Afterwards, when they open a living patient, they are not meeting the liver for the first time.

That is the software rasterizer. Its output — a slow renderer — is worthless. Its process is the only hundred hours in your career when the entire graphics pipeline will be small enough to hold in your head and open enough to step through in a debugger.

If the analogy feels like special pleading, here is one closer to home. Consider two programmers who both use std::sort:

Read the docsWrote quicksort once
Can call sortYesYes
Knows it is O(n log n)YesYes
Knows why it degrades on their dataNoYes
Can tell whether sorting is even their bottleneckNoYes
Can pick a better algorithm when it isNoYes

Both can use it. Only one can reason about it. The gap does not show up on a good day — it shows up on the day something is wrong, which in graphics is every day.

That is the whole bet: Modules 2 and 3 are how those enums become sentences instead of spells.

3The Theory

3.1 What actually transfers

"It builds understanding" is exactly the kind of hand-waving this course bans. So let us be specific and countable. Everything you write in Modules 1–3 lands in one of three buckets, and Figure 1 sorts them.

Read the top lane again, because it is the part people do not expect. The maths library you write in Module 2 — vec3, mat4, the projection matrix — is not a teaching exercise you discard. It is the maths library of the finished engine. It is in the capstone game in Module 8. You will have written every line of it, and it will still be there four hundred hours later.

So the honest accounting is not "a hundred hours to throw away". It is: most of it you keep as code, a quarter of it you keep as the ability to configure the GPU knowingly, and about four hundred lines — the ones that teach the most — you discard.

3.2 The hinge: NDC parity

The top lane of Figure 1 is not free. It is bought by one specific engineering decision, made before the first line of code, and it is the highest-leverage choice in this course.

The decision

Our software rasterizer targets SDL_GPU's exact normalized device coordinates — +Y up, depth z ∈ [0,1] with 0 at the near plane — even though nothing forces a CPU rasterizer to use any particular convention.

Consequence: the projection matrix, the viewport transform, and the depth test that you derive in Module 2 are numerically identical to the ones the GPU needs. Module 4's port becomes an API change, not a maths change.

A CPU rasterizer could use any convention it liked — it is your code, drawing into your buffer. Most tutorials pick OpenGL's z ∈ [-1,1], because most tutorials teach OpenGL. If we did that, then in Module 4 — at the exact moment you are drowning in a new API with no printf and no stack trace — you would also have to re-derive your projection matrix. Two hard things at once, and no way to tell which one is broken.

Instead, the maths is already right. When your first GPU triangle appears in the wrong place, you can be certain it is the API, because the matrix is the one that has been working for a hundred hours. That certainty is the whole gift. Debugging is the art of shrinking the set of things that might be wrong, and NDC parity halves it before you start.

Worked example — the same numbers, both stages

Take a point 5 metres in front of the camera, half a metre up: v_view = (0, 0.5, −5). Camera: 60° vertical field of view, 16:9, n = 0.1, f = 100.

From Conventions §5: t = 1/tan(30°) = 1.7321, a = 1.7778, so t/a = 0.9743. And R = f/(n−f) = 100/(−99.9) = −1.001001.

Push it through the projection matrix:

  • x_clip = 0.9743 · 0 = 0
  • y_clip = 1.7321 · 0.5 = 0.8660
  • z_clip = R·(−5) + R·n = 5.005005 − 0.100100 = 4.904905
  • w_clip = −z_view = 5

Divide by w:

  • x_ndc = 0 / 5 = 0
  • y_ndc = 0.8660 / 5 = 0.1732
  • z_ndc = 4.904905 / 5 = 0.980981

Now the point of the exercise. In Module 3, your CPU code computes (0, 0.1732, 0.9810) and your viewport transform turns it into a pixel. In Module 4, your HLSL vertex shader returns (0, 0.8660, 4.9049, 5) and the GPU divides — landing on (0, 0.1732, 0.9810). The same three numbers. Same matrix, same arithmetic, different executor.

Sanity-check z_ndc = 0.98: the point is 5 m out of a 100 m far plane, yet it is already 98% of the way through the depth range. That is not an error — it is the famous non-linearity of the depth buffer, and it is why depth precision is lavished near the camera and starved far away. We meet it properly in 3.1, and fix it with reverse-Z in Module 8.

The cost, stated honestly

NDC parity is not free. Our projection matrix has a different third row from the one in LearnOpenGL, Scratchapixel, and most of what you will find by searching. Ours targets z ∈ [0,1]; theirs targets z ∈ [-1,1].

So when you cross-reference — and you should — the matrix will not match, and for a moment you will think one of us is wrong. Neither is. We flag it every time it comes up, and Conventions §5 states it permanently. I would rather you meet this in a callout than at midnight.

3.3 The six promises

The pedagogy in the master prompt is binding on me, not advisory. Stated as promises you can hold me to — each with the symptom that means I have broken it:

#The promiseYou should call it broken when…
1 Intuition before formalism. A formula never appears before its meaning. Perspective arrives via similar triangles before any matrix; quaternions via 2D complex numbers before any 4D. …you meet a formula you cannot draw a picture of.
2 Derive, don't decree. "It just works this way" is banned. Where a derivation is genuinely out of scope, I say so explicitly, give the intuition anyway, and name a reference that finishes it. …a step happens and you cannot say why it is allowed.
3 Just-in-time maths. There is no maths chapter. Each tool arrives the moment the engine needs it, in the engine's context — and accumulates in the Math Toolbox. …you are learning something with no visible use yet.
4 Worked numbers. Every mathematical idea gets real numbers pushed through by hand — like §3.2 above. …a formula appears with no example you can check.
5 Show the failure mode. You see the artifact before the fix: overdraw chaos before the z-buffer, swimming textures before perspective correction, gimbal lock before quaternions. …a fix arrives for a problem you never witnessed.
6 You could explain every line. Handholding is not shallowness: every step, always with the why. …you typed something you could not defend.

Promise 5 deserves a note, because it is the one that looks like showmanship and is not. Showing you the broken picture first costs a page and feels indulgent. It works because a fix for a problem you have never seen is a ritual, while a fix for a bug that just wasted your afternoon is a tool. You remember tools. And when the artifact reappears in five years, in someone else's engine, you will recognise it on sight — which is most of what a senior graphics programmer is.

3.4 The spiral

You will meet lighting four times and memory four times. That is not repetition or padding — it is the shape of the course, and Figure 2 draws it.

The obvious objection: if Cook–Torrance is the correct model, why not teach it in Module 3 and skip the wrong ones?

Because Cook–Torrance answers a question you do not have yet. Handed to a student in Module 3, it is a formula with five Greek letters to copy. Handed to a student who has spent Module 3 watching Blinn-Phong's highlight look like plastic no matter what they do, it is an answer — and microfacet theory is the explanation of a frustration they have personally felt.

Same equation. Different meaning entirely, because of what came before. Wrong models taught knowingly, in order, are not a waste — they are the questions that make the right model legible. Every "wrong" step in Figure 2 is labelled as such at the time; we never pretend Blinn-Phong is physically correct.

3.5 Build vs buy

We hand-roll a maths library but import stb_image. We hand-roll an ECS but import Dear ImGui. That looks arbitrary until you see the rule:

The rule

We build anything whose construction is the lesson. We import anything whose construction is a different course.

ThingVerdictWhy
Maths libraryBuildUnderstanding matrices is the graphics course. GLM would hand you the answer to the exam.
ECSBuildData-oriented design is the architecture course. EnTT would hide the very tradeoff we are studying.
OBJ loaderBuildParsing a real format with real edge cases is a skill, and it is small enough to do properly.
Collision & rigid bodiesBuildThe SAT is one genuinely beautiful idea. Jolt would skip it.
stb_imageBuyJPEG decoding is a course in compression. Nothing about it teaches engines.
stb_truetypeBuyFont rasterization is a course in typography and hinting.
Dear ImGuiBuyImmediate-mode UI is a course in UI. Tooling and debug only — never gameplay UI.
cgltfBuy — laterOnly after you hand-roll OBJ, so you know exactly what it is doing for you.
SDL_shadercrossBuyWriting a shader cross-compiler is a course in compilers. (Honest: it is still 3.0.0-preview. We say so in 4.3.)

Note the cgltf row: the order carries the pedagogy. Import it first and it is a black box forever. Hand-roll OBJ first and cgltf becomes a library whose job you understand and whose bugs you could diagnose.

Honest: this is a pedagogical line, not an engineering one

If you are shipping a game next year, use GLM. Use Jolt. Use EnTT. They are written by specialists, they are tested against cases you have not imagined, and reimplementing them on a deadline is malpractice.

Our list is drawn for a course whose product is you, not the engine. Do not carry "we hand-rolled it here" into a professional argument for hand-rolling it there — that is cargo-culting, and it is the exact sin we warned about in 0.1's pitfalls. The value of building your own mat4 is that afterwards you can read GLM's.

The pencil thing is not a figure of speech

Every worked example in this course is designed to be re-done by hand — that is why the numbers are ugly (R = −1.001001) rather than convenient. Ugly numbers cannot be pattern-matched; they have to be computed.

Reading a derivation produces the feeling of understanding, which is a different thing from understanding and is almost indistinguishable from it until you are asked to reproduce it. Push the numbers through §3.2 yourself. It takes four minutes and it is worth more than re-reading the section three times.

4Implementation: the pace planner

The last lesson with no code

As in 0.1, there is no C++ here — you still have no compiler. That changes in 0.3, and from there every lesson ships files.

So this lesson's artifact addresses the other thing that decides whether you finish: a plan you will actually keep.

433 hours is a number that means nothing. "Twenty months at five hours a week" means something — you can look at that and know whether it is true. Most people who abandon a project this size do not hit a wall; they quietly discover that the pace they imagined was never real, and drift. So let us find out now.

Two things are worth noticing:

  • The first real milestone is closer than the end. At six hours a week you have spinning 3D wireframes you wrote every line of in about four months — a quarter of the way in. The course is not four hundred hours of nothing followed by a payoff; it is a chain of things that work.
  • Consistency beats intensity. Five hours a week for twenty months finishes. Twenty hours a week for two months, then nothing, does not — and that is the more common failure by a wide margin. This is the same lesson as 0.1 §2.3, oddly enough: the average is not what matters, the worst stretch is. An engine that averages 10 ms and spikes to 40 is unshippable; a schedule that averages 10 hours and spikes to zero for six weeks is abandoned.

Expected result

Checkpoint — you should be able to say these five things

  2. NDC parity means my projection matrix is numerically identical in both stages, so Module 4 is an API change, not a maths change — and when the first GPU triangle is wrong, I know it is the API.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Reading instead of doing — "I understood it, but I couldn't write it"
Symptom
You follow every derivation, nod along, and feel good. Then a blank file defeats you — or worse, Module 4 does, months later.
Cause
The feeling of understanding is produced by fluency, and reading someone else's clear explanation is maximally fluent. It is almost indistinguishable from understanding, right up until you have to reproduce it. This is the single most common way to fail this course, and it does not feel like failing at the time — it feels like efficiency.
Fix
Type the code; do not paste it. Do the worked examples with a pencil. Attempt the warm-up exercise before reading its solution. Each of these deliberately reintroduces friction — the friction is not an obstacle to learning, it is a substantial part of the mechanism.
Skipping to Module 4 — "the GPU stuff is the real content"
Symptom
Module 4 is a wall. Nothing renders, there is no error message, and you cannot form a hypothesis about why.
Cause
You have no model of the pipeline, so you cannot generate candidate explanations — and the GPU volunteers nothing. A CPU bug gives you a stack trace; a GPU bug gives you a black rectangle. Debugging is hypothesis generation, and hypotheses come from a model. Modules 2–3 are where the model is built.
Fix
If you are already stuck here: go back and write the software rasterizer. It is faster than flailing — genuinely, measured in days. And RenderDoc (4.9) only helps someone who knows what they are looking at.
Planning the pace you wish you had — the schedule that quietly dies
Symptom
Twenty hours in week one, twelve in week two, four in week three, zero from week five. The project is not abandoned — it is just never the thing you do today.
Cause
The plan was built from your best week rather than your median one. Long projects are decided by the median week, and by whether the bad weeks are survivable.
Fix
Halve your first instinct in the planner. A pace you keep for a year beats one you keep for a month, and the planner is honest about how little a sustainable pace costs: even three hours a week finishes, it just takes a while.
Taking the build-vs-buy list to work — "we should write our own physics"
Symptom
You argue in a professional setting that a team should hand-roll something, citing this course.
Cause
Confusing a pedagogical constraint with an engineering one. Our list optimises for you learning. A product team optimises for shipping, and those give different answers.
Fix
The transferable claim is not "hand-roll physics" — it is "having hand-rolled physics, I can evaluate Jolt, read its docs with comprehension, and debug it when it misbehaves." That is worth a great deal, and it is an argument for your judgement rather than against the library.

8Exercises

0.2.1Commit to a datewarm-up

Use the planner. Pick the hours/week you believe about your median week, then halve it and look at the result. Write down two dates: when Module 2's spinning wireframe lands, and when you finish. Put them somewhere you will see them.

Why halve it?

Because your estimate is of a good week, and there are not many of those. If the halved number still finishes — and it does; even three hours a week gets there, in about thirty-three months — then you have a plan robust to real life, rather than one that requires you to be your best self every week for a year and a half.

0.2.2Predict the bucketguided

For each item, predict its Figure 1 bucket — kept literally, becomes understanding, or discarded — and say why in one sentence:

  1. Your look_at() function from 2.9.
  2. Your bilinear texture filter from 3.9.
  3. Your Sutherland–Hodgman near-plane clipper from 3.3.
  4. Your fixed-timestep accumulator from 1.4.
Solution

1. look_at() — kept literally. It produces a mat4. The GPU does not care who built the matrix; it is uploaded and used. This code is in the capstone.

2. Bilinear filter — becomes understanding. The GPU does it in hardware, for free, via SDL_GPUSampler. But you will know why nearest-neighbour sparkles, and — the real payoff — you will know what a sampler is instead of copying its create-info.

3. Clipper — becomes understanding, mostly. The GPU clips for you. But this is the subtlest one: knowing why clipping cannot be skipped (what happens to a vertex behind the eye) is what lets you diagnose the deranged geometry you get when a projection matrix is subtly wrong. The code goes; the diagnostic power stays.

4. Accumulator — kept literally, and it is the odd one out: it never cared about the renderer at all. The loop from 1.4 is untouched by Module 4 and still running the physics in Module 7. Renderer-agnostic code is the most durable code in an engine — which is itself the boundary rule from 0.1 §3.4 paying a dividend.

0.2.3Find the failure modesguided

Promise 5 says you see the artifact before the fix. Open the curriculum and find three lessons whose description promises a specific broken picture. For each, predict what the artifact looks like before you read the lesson.

Hint

Look at 3.1, 3.2, and 7.1 — and 6.1, which is subtler because the artifact has been quietly wrong since Module 3 without you noticing.

Solution sketch

3.1 — without a z-buffer, triangles draw in submission order: far walls punch through near ones, and the picture reshuffles as the camera moves. 3.2 — affine texturing makes textures visibly swim and warp across triangles, most obviously on floors (the classic PlayStation 1 look, which is exactly this artifact). 7.1 — gimbal lock: at 90° pitch, two of your three rotation axes collapse onto each other and a whole degree of freedom vanishes while you watch.

6.1 is the interesting one. There is no dramatic artifact — just lighting that has been subtly, pervasively wrong since Module 3, with midtones too dark and blends muddy. You will not have noticed, because you have nothing to compare against. That is the most instructive kind of bug: the one whose absence you cannot perceive.

0.2.4Argue against meopen

Build the strongest case that the software rasterizer is a mistake — that the course should go straight to SDL_GPU in Module 1 and spend the recovered 132 hours on advanced rendering instead. Steelman it; do not build a strawman to knock over. Then decide, and name the evidence that would change your mind.

Solution sketch

The strong case against — and it is genuinely strong. (a) Nobody ships software rasterizers, so the skill is not directly marketable. (b) 132 hours buys a lot of PBR. (c) Plenty of excellent graphics programmers never wrote one and are fine. (d) You can learn the depth test from the GPU by toggling compare_op and observing — learning-by-experiment on the real thing. (e) Motivation is a real resource, and a GPU triangle in week two is more motivating than a CPU triangle in month two.

The case for rests on (d) being weaker than it sounds. You can only learn by experiment if you can generate hypotheses and observe outcomes. The GPU gives you a black screen and no error — the observation channel is nearly closed until you can already read a RenderDoc capture, which itself needs the model. Point (c) is true, but those programmers built the model some other way, usually painfully over years. We are buying it in 132 concentrated hours.

My verdict, and I hold it loosely: for someone with no graphics background, the CPU stage is the cheapest path to the GPU, not a detour. But (e) is a real cost and I will not pretend otherwise — it is why Module 1 gets you pixels on screen in the first few hours and Module 2 ends with spinning 3D, rather than saving everything for a big reveal.

What would change my mind: a student who already has a solid mental model of the pipeline — say, from a graphics course or shipping shaders — should absolutely skim Modules 2–3 for our conventions and go to Module 4. The argument is for people without the model, not for the technique.

9Recap & Next

What you can do now that you could not an hour ago

  • Judge the plan instead of trusting it. The CPU rasterizer is not a detour: ~60% of it survives as literal source, ~25% becomes GPU state you can configure knowingly, and only ~400 lines are discarded — the ones that teach the most.
  • Explain NDC parity — the decision that makes Module 4 an API change rather than a maths change — and name its cost: a projection matrix that will not match the tutorials.
  • Hold me to six promises, and recognise the symptom when one breaks.
  • Apply the build-vs-buy rule — build what is the lesson, import what is a different course — and know it is pedagogical, not professional advice.
  • Name the date you finish.

The sentence worth carrying: you throw away the implementation and keep the engineer. Everything in Modules 1–3 is aimed at the moment in Module 4 when a struct field is a sentence rather than a spell.

10Further Reading

  • Series Handmade Hero — Casey Muratori building a game and engine from scratch, on stream, for years. The most complete existing argument for the from-scratch method. He goes further than we do (no libraries at all); the disagreement is instructive.
  • Site Scratchapixel — the closest thing to this course's approach to maths: derive everything, geometrically, from zero. Our main reference for Modules 2–3. Note their z range differs from ours (§3.2).
  • Site LearnOpenGL — the best free graphics tutorial on the internet, and worth reading even though we do not use OpenGL. Its structure and clarity are a benchmark. Same caveat about the projection matrix.
  • Series Fabian Giesen, "A Trip Through the Graphics Pipeline" — what the hardware actually does with your triangles. Dense. Best read after Module 3, when you have your own pipeline to compare against — which is precisely the transfer this lesson is arguing for.
  • Book Jason Gregory, Game Engine Architecture — the reference for the whole course. Chapter 1 pairs with 0.1.
