2D Vectors, Geometrically

Five lessons ago, Exercise 1.2.3 asked you to notice that moving diagonally was 41% too fast, and promised the fix would need a tool we did not have: a way to say this direction, one unit long. That tool is the vector, and it is worth far more than the bug it fixes. Today it arrives as an arrow — not as a pair of numbers — and the dot product arrives as a shadow, long before any formula. By the end you will have the first piece of the maths library that everything from Module 2 onwards stands on.

1The Problem

Here is the movement code, essentially unchanged since Lesson 1.2:

Press D and the player moves at speed. Press W and the same. Press both and the player moves at… not speed.

Worked example — the diagonal, measured

With W and D both held, dx = 1 and dy = −1 (up is negative y on screen). At a speed of 90 pixels per second the per-second movement is:

  • Horizontally: 1 × 90 = 90 px
  • Vertically: −1 × 90 = −90 px
  • Actual distance covered, by Pythagoras: √(90² + 90²) = √16200 = 127.28 px

127.28 / 90 = 1.4142 — the player travels 41.4% further per second diagonally than along an axis. That number is √2, and it is not a rounding error or a tuning problem: it is the length of the arrow (1, −1), which we scaled by speed as though its length were 1.

The bug is small. What it reveals is not. We have been writing x and y as two independent numbers that happen to be near each other, and doing arithmetic on each separately — and the moment a question involves both at once, such as "how long is this step?", the code has no way to ask it. There is no name for the thing whose length we needed.

Every geometric idea in the next eight modules has this shape. "How far apart are these?" "Which way is the light coming from?" "Is this triangle facing me?" "Is this point inside?" All of them are questions about a quantity with direction and size, and none of them can be asked cleanly of two loose floats.

So we introduce that quantity. Its name is a vector, and the reason to introduce it as geometry rather than as a pair of numbers is that the geometry is what makes it useful. A student who thinks of a vector as "a struct with x and y" will implement the dot product from a formula and never know what it is for. A student who thinks of it as an arrow will reinvent the formula when they need it.

2Building Intuition

2.1 An arrow with nowhere to be

A vector is an arrow. It has two properties and only two:

  • a direction — which way it points;
  • a length — how long it is, also called its magnitude.

And now the part that is easy to read past and worth stopping on: a vector has no position. It is not at anywhere. "Three metres east" is a complete vector; it does not become a different vector if you start from your kitchen instead of from mine. Slide an arrow around the page without turning or stretching it and it is the same arrow throughout.

Figure 1 makes the point by drawing the same vector four times in four different places. A fair objection: we do want to talk about positions, and we will use the same type for them. The reconciliation is that a position is a vector from an agreed origin — the arrow that takes you from (0, 0) to the place you mean. That works, and it is what every engine does, but it means the type is being used for two different ideas. Keeping them straight in your head matters: adding two positions is meaningless, while adding a position and a displacement is exactly how things move.

2.2 Adding and stretching

Two operations, both defined by drawing rather than by arithmetic.

Adding is following one arrow and then the other. Put the tail of the second at the tip of the first; the sum is the arrow from the original tail to the final tip. Walk three metres east then four metres north, and the sum is the single arrow describing where you ended up relative to where you started — which is emphatically not seven metres.

Scaling is stretching. Multiplying by 2 gives an arrow twice as long in the same direction; by ½, half as long; by −1, the same length pointing the opposite way. Scaling changes length and possibly reverses direction, but never turns an arrow to a new angle. Figure 2 shows both operations.

Notice we have defined two useful operations without writing down a single number. That is not a stunt: it is the reason vectors survive the jump to three dimensions unchanged. The pictures are identical in 3-D, so the rules are too, and Module 2 will get vec3 almost for free.

3The Theory

3.1 Components, at last

To compute with arrows we have to describe them numerically, and the way to do that is to pick two reference directions — x and y — and ask: how far along each does this arrow reach?

Those two answers are the arrow's components. An arrow reaching 3 units along x and 4 along y is written (3, 4). Nothing has changed about the arrow; we have only chosen a way to write it down.

The components depend on the axes, and the vector does not

Turn the axes and the same arrow gets different numbers. That is worth holding onto, because it is exactly what the whole of Module 2 is about: a "coordinate space" is a choice of axes, and moving a vector between spaces means recomputing its components while the arrow itself sits still. The arrow is the reality; the components are a description relative to a frame.

With components, §2.2's two pictures become arithmetic. Adding tip to tail means the x-reaches add and the y-reaches add. Scaling stretches both reaches equally:

These rules are derived from the geometry, not decreed alongside it. That distinction matters when you meet an operation whose component form looks arbitrary — the dot product is about to be exactly that — because the honest response is to go back to the picture.

3.2 Length, and the unit vector

An arrow reaching 3 along x and 4 along y forms a right-angled triangle with those two reaches as its sides. Pythagoras gives the arrow itself:

So (3, 4) has length √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5. And (1, −1), our diagonal input, has length √(1 + 1) = √2 ≈ 1.41421 — there is the 41.4%, and it was never mysterious. We scaled an arrow of length 1.414 by speed and expected to move at speed.

The fix follows immediately. If the problem is that the arrow's length is not 1, make it 1. Divide an arrow by its own length and the direction is untouched while the length becomes exactly 1 — a unit vector. The operation is called normalisation, and Figure 3 shows it applied to the diagonal that has been troubling us.

Worked example — the five-lesson-old bug, closed

Holding W and D at a speed of 90 px/s:

Direction usedStep per secondDistance covered
Before(1, −1), length 1.41421(90, −90)127.28 px — 41.4% too far
After(0.70711, −0.70711), length 1(63.64, −63.64)90.00 px ✓

And single-axis movement is unaffected, because (1, 0) already has length 1 and normalising it changes nothing. That is the sign of a fix rather than a fudge: it corrects the broken case and provably leaves the working case alone.

The one input that breaks it

No keys held means (0, 0), whose length is zero — and dividing by it is 0/0, which is NaN. From Lesson 1.3 §3.5 you already know what happens next: NaN spreads through every arithmetic operation it touches, silently, until the whole scene disappears several frames later. There is no mathematically correct answer to "which way does a zero-length arrow point", so the code must decide. Ours returns (0, 0) — a frame with no input means no movement — with a normalised_or() variant for cases that genuinely need some direction.

3.3 The dot product, as a shadow

Now the operation this lesson is really for. Forget any formula you have seen; we are going to build it from a picture.

Take two arrows, a and b, starting from the same point. Shine a light straight down onto b — rays perpendicular to it — so that a casts a shadow on the line along b.

That shadow has a length, and it answers a genuinely useful question: how much of a points along b? If the two arrows point the same way the shadow is as long as a itself. As a swings away, the shadow shortens. When a is exactly perpendicular the shadow has no length at all — none of a goes in b's direction. Swing further and the shadow falls on the far side, pointing backwards.

The shadow's length is |a| cos θ, where θ is the angle between the arrows — that is just the definition of cosine applied to the right-angled triangle in the figure. And the dot product is that shadow multiplied by b's length:

Why multiply by |b|? That step looks arbitrary.

It does, and it deserves an answer rather than a shrug. The shadow alone — |a| cos θ — is a perfectly good quantity, called the scalar projection, and we use it directly in §4. But it is lopsided: it treats a and b differently, so projecting a onto b gives a different number from projecting b onto a.

Multiplying by |b| makes it symmetric — dot(a,b) = dot(b,a) — and that symmetry is what makes the operation behave algebraically, which is what makes §3.4's derivation possible at all. It also matches physics: the work done by a force is |F| |d| cos θ, force times distance-moved-along-the-force. The symmetric version is the one that turned out to be worth naming.

Drag the arrow below and watch the shadow, the angle and the number move together. It is worth a minute: the sign flipping as the arrow crosses perpendicular is the single most useful fact in this lesson.

Drag the blue tip. a · b = 0 θ = 0° side-on

Amber is b, fixed. Blue is a, which you drag. Green is a's shadow on b. Watch the shadow vanish, and the number pass through zero, exactly when the arrows are perpendicular.

3.4 Deriving the formula

We have a geometric definition involving an angle and two lengths. The form you will actually write in code has neither:

Multiply the x's, multiply the y's, add. Where did the angle go? Most texts assert these two are the same and move on. Let us actually connect them, using only things we have already established.

Step one: shadows add. If you lay one arrow after another, tip to tail, the shadow of the combined arrow is the shadow of the first plus the shadow of the second. Figure 5 shows why: the shadows fall on the same line, end to end, exactly as the arrows do. In symbols: dot(u + v, b) = dot(u, b) + dot(v, b).

Step two: every arrow is a sum of its components. By definition, a = ax·x̂ + ay·ŷ, where x̂ and ŷ are unit arrows along the two axes. That is literally what "reaches 3 along x and 4 along y" says.

Put those together and the dot product splits apart:

The derivation, in three lines

Start from the geometric definition and use step one to break a apart:

StepReason
a · b = (ax·x̂ + ay·ŷ) · bStep two: write a as its components.
     = ax (x̂ · b) + ay (ŷ · b)Step one: shadows add, and scaling an arrow scales its shadow.
     = ax · bx + ay · byBecause x̂ · b is bx — see below.

The last line is the one worth pausing on. What is x̂ · b? By the geometric definition it is |x̂| |b| cos α, where α is the angle between the x axis and b. But |x̂| = 1, so it is |b| cos α — which is the shadow of b on the x axis. And the shadow of an arrow on the x axis is precisely what we called its x component in §3.1.

Components are projections onto the axes. That is why the formula looks the way it does: ax·bx + ay·by is not a coincidence of algebra, it is "project onto each axis and add up", which is the shadow picture applied twice.

Worked example — both routes to the same number

Take a = (3, 4) and b = (4, 3), the arrows in Figure 4.

Via componentsVia geometry
3 × 4 + 4 × 3 |a| = √(9+16) = 5, |b| = √(16+9) = 5
= 12 + 12 cos θ = 24 / 25 = 0.96 → θ = 16.26°

Same answer, arrived at from opposite ends. The component route needs two multiplications and an addition; the geometric route needs two square roots and an inverse cosine. In code we always use the first — but we think in the second, which is why the derivation was worth doing.

4Implementation: vec2

The engine gains src/math/, and the file is a header with no .cpp beside it. That is deliberate.

C++ in place — why this one is header-only

Every other subsystem so far has been a .hpp declaring and a .cpp defining, so that changing an implementation recompiles one translation unit instead of all of them. vec2 breaks that pattern for a specific reason.

These functions are two or three lines each and are called thousands of times per frame. A function defined in another translation unit generally cannot be inlined — the compiler cannot see its body — so every a + b would be a real function call: arguments pushed, a jump, a return, for an operation that should be two machine instructions. Putting the bodies in the header lets the compiler inline them everywhere, and a vector add becomes what it should be.

The trade is real: change one line and everything that includes it recompiles. That is acceptable for code which is small, hot, and stable — a vector type is all three. It is not acceptable for a renderer, and this is not a licence to make everything header-only. When Module 5 draws the public API boundary, this distinction becomes a design rule rather than a judgement call.

4.1 A struct, passed by value

Public members and no constructor. There is nothing to encapsulate — a vector is its two components, there is no invariant to protect, and hiding them behind get_x() would add ceremony and subtract clarity. The default member initialisers mean vec2 v; is the origin rather than two floats of whatever was in that memory.

Everything takes and returns vec2 by value, not by const&. The type is eight bytes — measured, not assumed — which fits in a register. Passing by reference would mean handing over an address the callee must then dereference: more work, not less. The rule this sets for the course: pass small value types by value; reach for a reference when the object is large or must be mutated in place.

4.2 length_squared, and the square root you did not need

Two things here. First, length_squared is dot(v, v) — the dot product of an arrow with itself is |v||v|cos 0 = |v|², since a vector is perfectly aligned with itself. A pleasing consistency check on §3.4's formula, and a genuine reuse.

Second, and more practically: prefer length_squared whenever you are only comparing. Square root is monotonic, so |a| < |b| exactly when |a|² < |b|². Every "which is nearest", "is this within range", "sort by distance" gives the identical answer without it.

One square root is not expensive. A square root per enemy per frame, or per pixel, is a measurable cost buying nothing — and Lesson 1.5 showed how quickly per-element work adds up when the element count is large.

4.3 The zero-vector guard

The guard tests length_squared rather than length, which avoids a square root on the path that is about to take one anyway — and, more importantly, tests the quantity we are about to divide by.

Returning (0, 0) is a choice, and it is worth being clear that it is one. A zero-length arrow genuinely has no direction; there is no correct answer to return. What we can choose is a safe answer, and "no input means no movement" is the behaviour that keeps the demo sane. Where some direction must exist — a facing that has to point somewhere even when the player stands still — normalised_or() takes an explicit fallback, which forces the caller to decide rather than inheriting ours by accident.

6Build & Run

What you should see

  1. Move the mouse across the top panel. The blue arrow follows it; the green shadow slides along the amber arrow. Watch a · b in the readout as you swing the cursor around — largest when the arrows agree, and it passes through exactly zero as you cross perpendicular.
  2. Move the cursor to the far side. The shadow now falls behind the origin and the dot product is negative, with the readout saying "behind". Nothing computed an angle.
  3. Hold D. Both squares move right at the same speed, and the ratio readout stays at 1.000.
  4. Now hold W and D together. The coral square pulls ahead of the green one, visibly, and the ratio climbs towards 1.414. That gap is the bug Exercise 1.2.3 identified, drawn to scale.
  5. Release everything. Nothing moves, nothing disappears, and no NaN appears — the zero-vector guard doing its silent job.
Checkpoint — verified before publishing

  • On the 3-4-5 triangle: length_squared = 25, length = 5.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Everything vanishes when the player stands still — NaN from a zero vector
Symptom
The scene disappears, or a position reads nan in the debugger, the instant no movement keys are held. Often it survives a while and then breaks, because the value spreads before anything draws it.
Cause
Normalising (0, 0): dividing zero by a zero length is 0/0, which is NaN (§3.2).
Fix
Guard on length_squared(v) <= 0 before dividing. When hunting one of these, remember from Lesson 1.3 that the crash site is never where the NaN was born — test std::isnan at a few checkpoints and bisect.
Diagonal movement is still too fast — normalisation applied to the wrong thing
Symptom
You added normalised() and the diagonal is still faster, or is now the wrong speed entirely.
Cause
Normalising after scaling — normalised(input * speed * dt) — which throws the speed away and yields a step of length 1. Or normalising the position rather than the direction, which teleports the object to the unit circle around the origin.
Fix
Normalise the direction, then scale: normalised(input) * speed * dt. The order is the whole point — direction first, magnitude second, which is what separating them buys you.
A square root in a hot loop — a profiler points at length()
Symptom
Distance checks show up in a profile, in a loop over many objects or pixels.
Cause
length() used where only a comparison was needed. The square root is computed and then immediately discarded by a <.
Fix
length_squared(delta) < range * range. Square the constant once, outside the loop, rather than rooting the variable every iteration (§4.2).
The dot product's sign is backwards — "in front" reports enemies behind you
Symptom
Facing checks are inverted, or a lighting term is brightest where it should be dark.
Cause
One of the two arrows points the other way from what you assumed. The classic pair: enemy − me versus me − enemy, and a light direction versus the direction to the light.
Fix
Name the variables for what they are — to_enemy, to_light — rather than delta or dir. Most sign bugs in vector code are naming failures, and they survive review precisely because dir looks fine.
Comparing vectors with == never matches — a position that should be exact isn't
Symptom
A check like if (pos == target) never fires, even though the values look identical when printed.
Cause
Floating-point equality. Two routes to the "same" position — accumulated addition versus a single computation — rarely produce bit-identical floats, and printing rounds the difference away.
Fix
Compare a distance against a tolerance: length_squared(pos - target) < epsilon * epsilon. operator== exists on our vec2 because it is genuinely useful in tests and for detecting an unchanged value, not because it is the right way to ask "are these two points in the same place".

8Exercises

1.7.1Watch the ratio convergewarm-up

Run the demo, press R, then hold W+D without releasing. Watch the ratio readout. What does it approach, and why does it start at 1.000 rather than jumping straight there?

Solution

It approaches √2 = 1.41421. It starts at 1.000 because the ratio is over total distance travelled, so any straight-line movement you did earlier is still in the numerator and denominator equally. Hold a pure diagonal from a reset and it climbs steadily; mix in some horizontal movement and it settles somewhere between 1 and 1.414.

That is worth noticing in its own right: the error is not a constant, it depends on the path. A player who moves only in straight lines never sees the bug, while one who strafes diagonally gets a permanent 41% advantage — which is exactly why this shipped in real games and became a speedrunning technique rather than a bug report.

1.7.2Find perpendicular by handwarm-up

In the demo, move the mouse until a · b reads as close to zero as you can get it. Now look at the shadow. Then check the claim in code: pick any vector, call perpendicular() on it, and print the dot product of the two.

Solution

The shadow shrinks to nothing — a perpendicular arrow contributes nothing along b. And dot(v, perpendicular(v)) is exactly 0.0f, not approximately: perpendicular({x,y}) is {−y, x}, so the dot product is x(−y) + yx, which cancels identically before any rounding can occur.

Exact zeros are rare in floating-point work and worth recognising when they appear. This one holds because the two products are the same magnitude with opposite signs, so the subtraction is exact regardless of the values.

1.7.3Make the squares chase the mouseguided

Replace the keyboard input with "move towards the cursor": compute the vector from the square to the mouse, normalise it, and step along it at k_speed. What happens when the square arrives, and how would you stop it?

Hint

The direction is mouse_fb - position. Think about what that vector's length does as the square gets close, and what normalised() does to it.

Solution

The square jitters around the cursor. Each step is a full speed × h regardless of how close it is, so it overshoots, turns around, overshoots again — the normalisation that made movement consistent is now preventing it from ever slowing down.

Two standard fixes, and both are worth implementing. Stop when length_squared(to_target) < arrival_radius² — note the squared comparison from §4.2 doing real work. Or clamp the step to the remaining distance: step = min(speed * h, length(to_target)), so the last step lands exactly on the target. The second is what most engines do, and it is the seed of the "arrive" steering behaviour.

1.7.4Reflect a bouncing ball off any wallguided

Our bounces have always been axis-aligned: flip vy and carry on. Using the dot product, work out how to bounce a velocity v off a surface with unit normal n at any angle. Implement reflect(v, n) and test it against the axis-aligned case.

Hint

Split v into the part along n and the part along the surface. Which one should a bounce reverse, and which should it leave alone? §3.3's projection gives you the first part.

Solution

The component along the normal is dot(v, n) * n (with n a unit vector). A bounce reverses that and keeps the rest, so you subtract it twice:

Check it against what we had: for a floor, n = (0, −1), so dot(v, n) = −vy and the result is (vx, −vy) — exactly the flip we have been hard-coding since Lesson 1.3, now as a special case of the general rule. That is the payoff of the geometric route: the axis-aligned version was never a different idea, only a simpler instance. 1.7.5Design vec3 before you write itopen

Module 2 needs a three-dimensional version. Go through vec2.hpp function by function and decide, for each: does it carry over unchanged, does it need a new definition, or does it not exist in 3-D at all? Then find the one operation that exists in 3-D and has no 2-D counterpart.

Solution sketch

Almost everything carries over with one more component: addition, scaling, dot product (ax·bx + ay·by + az·bz), length, normalisation, projection, lerp. That is the dividend from defining them geometrically — none of the pictures changed, so none of the definitions did.

perpendicular() is the interesting failure. In 2-D there are exactly two perpendicular directions and picking one is a convention. In 3-D there is a whole plane of them, so "the perpendicular" is meaningless and the function cannot survive.

And the operation with no 2-D counterpart is the cross product: given two arrows in 3-D it produces one perpendicular to both, which is only a well-posed request because 3-D has a third direction to escape into. It is how Module 3 computes surface normals and does back-face culling. Worth predicting now, so it arrives as an answer to a question you already asked.

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • It has a name for "which way and how much". src/math/ exists, and vec2 is the first piece of a maths library that will carry the rest of the course.
  • Movement is honest in every direction. Direction and speed are chosen separately, so a diagonal travels exactly as fast as an axis — the bug Exercise 1.2.3 spotted five lessons ago is closed, and you can watch the gap.
  • It can ask geometric questions. "How much of this points along that?" is one multiply-add, and its sign answers "in front or behind?" without a single trigonometric function.
  • You derived the dot product rather than memorising it. ax·bx + ay·by came out of projecting onto the axes and adding — so when it turns up in Lambert shading, in back-face culling and in the Separating Axis Theorem, it will be the same idea rather than a new one.

The idea to carry forward: a vector is an arrow, and its components are its shadows on the axes. Everything else in this lesson — the length formula, the dot product, even the reason normalisation works — follows from taking that picture seriously.

Next: 1.8 — Checkpoint: Pong. Module 1 has given us an event loop, an input system, an honest clock, a fixed timestep with interpolation, a framebuffer we own, a colour module and now vectors. Time to prove it holds together by building something small and complete: a game with a ball, two paddles, a score and a way to lose — the first thing in this course that another person can play.
