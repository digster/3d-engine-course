Module 2 — Software Rasterizer I · Lesson 2.5

Matrices as Basis Transforms

Four lessons have gone into the inside of a triangle. This one turns to where triangles come from — and it is the lesson that decides whether the rest of the course feels inevitable or arbitrary. A matrix will not be handed to you as a grid of numbers with a multiplication rule to memorise. It arrives as the answer to a single question: where do the basis vectors land? Ask that, write the two answers down side by side, and you have built a matrix. Everything else — the multiplication rule, the rotation formula, why order matters, what the determinant is — falls out, and none of it needs remembering separately.

1The Problem

Here is how the demo has been rotating its triangle since Lesson 2.2:

Look at what that actually does. It never rotates anything. It generates three points from three angles, and to make them appear to spin it adds phase to each angle before generating them again. The triangle has no existence between frames; it is recomputed from scratch, from parameters that happen to include an angle.

That works beautifully for a shape defined by angles, and it is completely useless for anything else. Lesson 3.5 loads a mesh from an OBJ file: a few thousand vertices, arriving as plain coordinates. There is no angle to add to. There is no phase. There is a list of points and a wish to turn all of them, together, by the same amount.

Four questions the current approach cannot answer
  1. How do I rotate a point I did not generate? Given (37, −12) from a file, turn it 40° about the origin.
  2. How do I do two things at once? Squash by 1.6 horizontally, then rotate. Now do it the other way round — is the result the same? (It is not, and you should not have to run it to know.)
  3. How much bigger did the shape get? If a transformation stretches one axis and squashes another, by what factor did the area change?
  4. How do I undo it? Given a transformed point, recover the original.

Every one of those has the same answer, and it is a single object — a small block of numbers that is the transformation. You apply it to any point you like, you combine two of them into one, you read the area factor straight off it, and you invert it. That object is a matrix, and by the end of this lesson you will have derived every part of it rather than been told any of it.

One thing this lesson deliberately does not do: translation. Moving everything two units to the right is the most obvious transformation there is, and a 2×2 matrix cannot express it. That is not an oversight in our design — it is a genuine limitation of the tool, §3.8 proves it in one line, and Lesson 2.7 fixes it in a way that will feel inevitable only if you have first felt the gap. So we are going to leave it open on purpose.

2Building Intuition

What a transformation does to a grid

Stop thinking about individual points for a moment and think about the whole plane at once. A transformation takes every point somewhere. A good way to see what it does is to draw a grid on the plane first and then look at what happens to the grid.

The transformations we are after — the ones a matrix can express — are exactly those that leave the grid looking like a grid. Figure 1 shows what that means and what it rules out.

That middle picture is worth staring at, because it is more permissive than people expect. The shape has changed completely: right angles are gone, the squares have become parallelograms, nothing is where it started. And yet the grid is still a grid. That is the constraint, and it is the reason the whole subject is as simple as it turns out to be.

Every vector is a recipe

Now the observation everything else rests on, and it is almost too simple to notice.

Write î for the arrow one unit along x, and ĵ for the arrow one unit along y — the two basis vectors. The vector (3, 2) is not merely described by the numbers 3 and 2. It is three of î plus two of ĵ:

\[ \mathbf{v} = (3, 2) = 3\,\hat{\imath} + 2\,\hat{\jmath} \] v = (3, 2) = 3 * i-hat + 2 * j-hat

Coordinates are a recipe: how much of each basis vector to take. That is what writing a vector as a pair of numbers has always meant, and it is easy to stop noticing because the recipe and the ingredients are usually never mentioned separately.

Here is why it matters. Suppose some transformation moves the plane around, and suppose it is one of the well-behaved ones from Figure 1. Ask what happens to v. If the transformation preserves the recipe — if "three of this plus two of that" still holds after the move — then you do not need to know anything about v at all. You need to know where î went and where ĵ went. Then you take three of the first and two of the second.

So you only have to answer twice

Put those two ideas together and the conclusion is a little startling the first time:

The entire lesson, in one sentence

A linear transformation of the plane is completely determined by where it sends î and ĵ. Two arrows. Four numbers. There is nothing else to know about it.

Not "you can approximate it with two arrows", not "two arrows are a convenient summary" — determined, exactly, with nothing left over. Every one of the infinitely many points in the plane has its destination fixed by those four numbers, because every point is a recipe in terms of the two arrows, and the recipe survives.

A matrix is those two answers written down next to each other. That is all a matrix has ever been. The grid of numbers people find intimidating is two vectors in a trench coat, and once you have seen that, the notation stops being something to decode.

3The Theory

Linear, precisely

§2.1 gave the visual version: straight lines stay straight, parallel lines stay parallel and evenly spaced, and the origin stays put. That is a description of the result. The definition is two rules about the transformation itself, and they are the two things "the recipe survives" actually requires:

\[ T(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}) = T(\mathbf{a}) + T(\mathbf{b}) \qquad\text{and}\qquad T(c\,\mathbf{a}) = c\,T(\mathbf{a}) \] T(a + b) = T(a) + T(b), and T(c*a) = c*T(a)

In words: it does not matter whether you add first or transform first, and it does not matter whether you scale first or transform first. A transformation obeying both is called linear.

Now watch those two rules do all the work. Take any vector and split it into its recipe, then apply the rules one at a time:

\[ T(\mathbf{v}) = T(x\,\hat{\imath} + y\,\hat{\jmath}) = T(x\,\hat{\imath}) + T(y\,\hat{\jmath}) = x\,T(\hat{\imath}) + y\,T(\hat{\jmath}) \] T(v) = T(x*i + y*j) = T(x*i) + T(y*j) = x*T(i) + y*T(j)

The first step is the definition of coordinates. The second uses the addition rule. The third uses the scaling rule. And the result contains no T applied to anything except the two basis vectors — which is §2.3's claim, now proved rather than asserted.

Why "the origin stays put" is not a third rule

It follows from the second one. Put c = 0: T(0·v) = 0·T(v), and 0·v is the origin while 0·T(v) is the origin, so T(origin) = origin. Always. No linear transformation moves the origin, and that one line is the whole of §3.8 — it is why translation is out of reach.

The columns are the answers

So: to record a linear transformation, record two vectors. Write them as the columns of a block of numbers and you have written a matrix.

\[ M = \begin{pmatrix} \big| & \big| \\ T(\hat{\imath}) & T(\hat{\jmath}) \\ \big| & \big| \end{pmatrix} \] M = [ T(i-hat) T(j-hat) ], written as two columns side by side

And applying it is the recipe, which we already derived:

\[ M\mathbf{v} = x \cdot \mathbf{c_0} + y \cdot \mathbf{c_1} \] M*v = v.x * (first column) + v.y * (second column)

That is the entire definition of matrix–vector multiplication. Write it out component by component and you get the familiar rows-times-column arithmetic — but as a consequence, not a rule to memorise, which is why you never again have to pause and wonder which index goes where.

Worked example — the matrix from Figure 2

Suppose î lands at (2, 0) and ĵ lands at (1, 3). Those are the columns, so

\[ M = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \] M = | 2 1 | | 0 3 | -- first column (2,0), second column (1,3)

Now transform v = (3, 2). Three of the first column plus two of the second:

\[ M\mathbf{v} = 3\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} + 2\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix} \] M*v = 3*(2,0) + 2*(1,3) = (6,0) + (2,6) = (8,6)

Cross-check it the way you were probably taught — each row dotted with the vector: (2, 1)·(3, 2) = 6 + 2 = 8 and (0, 3)·(3, 2) = 0 + 6 = 6. Same answer, because it is the same arithmetic rearranged. The column view tells you why; the row view is a way to compute it.

Asserted in the harness, along with the two checks that keep the claim honest: M·(1,0) returns the first column exactly, and M·(0,1) returns the second.

Written in rows, stored in columns

Now a piece of pure bookkeeping that causes more real bugs than any concept in this lesson.

On paper we write a matrix in rows — that is just how a 2-D block of text is read. In memory we are going to store it in columns, because the columns are the meaningful objects. So the first two floats in memory are the left column read downwards, not the top row read across.

Two reasons we take the column-major side of this, and neither is arbitrary.

It matches the meaning. The columns are the images of the basis vectors, so they are the objects you actually want to name, pass around and look at in a debugger. Our mat2 literally stores two vec2s called c0 and c1, which makes the storage order a consequence of the design rather than a convention bolted on afterwards.

It matches what we will hand to a GPU. SDL_GPU, and the shading languages behind it, expect column-major matrix data. Choosing anything else now would mean a transpose at the boundary in Module 4, forever, and transposes at boundaries are exactly the kind of thing that gets applied twice or not at all.

How this bites

A transposed matrix is not obviously wrong. A transposed rotation is a rotation the other way — perfectly plausible, just backwards. A transposed shear shears the other axis. Nothing crashes, nothing looks like garbage, and you can lose an afternoon to it.

The tell: if your rotation goes the wrong way and your shear tilts the wrong axis, you have a transpose, not two separate bugs. The fix is one place, not two.

Rotation, derived

Time to build a real matrix, and to do it without looking anything up. The recipe is fixed: ask where the two basis vectors go, write the answers as columns, stop.

Where does î go under a rotation by θ? It starts at (1, 0), one unit from the origin, pointing along x. Turning it by θ keeps it one unit from the origin and swings it to angle θ. A point one unit from the origin at angle θ has coordinates (cos θ, sin θ) — that is not a formula to recall, it is what sine and cosine are. So the first column is (cos θ, sin θ).

Where does ĵ go? It starts at (0, 1), which is a quarter turn ahead of î. Rotation turns everything by the same amount, so it stays a quarter turn ahead: it lands at angle θ + 90°. A quarter turn takes (x, y) to (−y, x), so (cos θ, sin θ) becomes (−sin θ, cos θ). That is the second column, and Figure 3 shows both.

Which way does it turn?

Counter-clockwise — in a space where y points up, which is the mathematical convention and the one mat2 uses. Our framebuffer has +y pointing down, so the identical numbers appear to turn clockwise on screen. Nothing about the matrix changes; the picture is flipped.

This is the same situation as edge_function's sign in Lesson 2.2, and the demo handles it in exactly one place — a single negation of y at the moment maths coordinates become pixel coordinates. Lesson 2.11 makes that negation official and calls it the viewport transform.

Worked example — a quarter turn

Put θ = 90°. Then cos θ = 0 and sin θ = 1, so the columns are (0, 1) and (−1, 0):

\[ R(90°) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \] R(90 degrees) = | 0 -1 | | 1 0 |

Sanity-check it against the picture rather than the algebra: a quarter turn should send the arrow pointing right to the arrow pointing up, and the arrow pointing up to the arrow pointing left. First column (0,1) — right becomes up ✓. Second column (−1,0) — up becomes left ✓.

And a bonus that is worth the price of the whole section. Rotating by a and then by b must be the same as rotating by a + b. So R(a)·R(b) and R(a+b) have to be the same matrix — and multiplying the two out, element by element, gives you the angle-addition formulas:

\[ \cos(a+b) = \cos a \cos b - \sin a \sin b, \qquad \sin(a+b) = \sin a \cos b + \cos a \sin b \] cos(a+b) = cos a cos b - sin a sin b; sin(a+b) = sin a cos b + cos a sin b

Two identities most people memorise for a decade, falling out of a picture of two arrows. Verified numerically over 1,716 pairs of angles: worst element disagreement 2.98 × 10⁻⁷. And spot-checked directly — the top-left element of R(0.6)·R(0.9) comes out as 0.070737, which is cos(1.5) to every digit printed.

Scale and shear

The same question, twice more. Neither needs a paragraph.

Scale. Stretching by sx horizontally and sy vertically sends î = (1,0) to (sx, 0) and ĵ = (0,1) to (0, sy). Columns, done:

\[ S = \begin{pmatrix} s_x & 0 \\ 0 & s_y \end{pmatrix} \] S = | sx 0 | | 0 sy |

Notice what happens with a negative factor. scale(−1, 1) sends î to (−1, 0) — the arrow now points the other way, and the whole plane has been mirrored. The machinery does not treat that as a special case; it just sends a basis vector backwards. §3.7 gives it a name.

Shear. A shear slides points sideways in proportion to how far up they are — a deck of cards pushed over. Points on the x-axis do not move, so î stays at (1, 0). A point one unit up slides across by k, so ĵ lands at (k, 1):

\[ H = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & k \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \] H = | 1 k | | 0 1 |

Shear is the transformation worth keeping in mind whenever "linear" starts to sound like "barely changes anything". A shear can turn a square into a parallelogram of any slant you like — the shape is unrecognisable — and yet every grid line is still straight, still parallel to its neighbours, still evenly spaced. That is what the constraint from Figure 1 actually costs, and it is much less than it sounds.

Composition, and why order matters

Now the question the trigonometry approach could not answer at all: do two things in sequence.

Apply B and then apply A. That is a transformation in its own right, and it is linear (both rules survive being done twice), so it has a matrix. Which one? Ask the only question we ever ask: where do the basis vectors land? Send î through B — that is B's first column — and then send the result through A. So:

\[ (AB)\text{'s first column} = A\,\mathbf{b_0}, \qquad (AB)\text{'s second column} = A\,\mathbf{b_1} \] first column of A*B = A applied to B's first column; likewise for the second

That is matrix multiplication, complete, derived in two lines from the same question as everything else. In code it is literally those two lines:

Read it right to left

A * B means B happens first. That is not a convention somebody chose to be annoying — it is forced by the notation. (A*B)*v has to mean the same thing as A*(B*v), and in A*(B*v) it is obviously B that touches v first.

It follows from writing vectors as columns and applying matrices on the left. A codebase that uses row vectors and writes v' = v*M reads left-to-right instead. Both are self-consistent; mixing them gives you code that is transposed and backwards, which is why our conventions page pins this down and never revisits it: column vectors, v' = M*v, column-major storage.

And now the thing everyone is told and few are shown: order matters. Matrix multiplication is not commutative. Figure 5 does it with one scale and one rotation.

Worked example — and the numbers are cleaner than the picture

Take R = a quarter turn and S = scale(2, 1), a pure horizontal stretch. Compose both ways by sending each basis vector through in turn.

Scale first, then rotate (R·S). î stretches to (2, 0), then turns to (0, 2). ĵ is untouched by the stretch, then turns to (−1, 0):

\[ RS = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 2 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \] R*S = | 0 -1 | | 2 0 |

Rotate first, then scale (S·R). î turns to (0, 1) — which is vertical, so the horizontal stretch does nothing to it. ĵ turns to (−1, 0) — now horizontal, so the stretch doubles it to (−2, 0):

\[ SR = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -2 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \] S*R = | 0 -2 | | 1 0 |

Different matrices, and the difference is easy to feel: the stretch always acts along the x-axis, so what gets stretched depends on what happens to be lying along x when the stretch arrives. Apply both to (1, 0) and you get (0, 2) against (0, 1) — a factor of two apart.

But both have determinant 2. Same area factor, different shape. That is a useful thing to know about the determinant before §3.7 defines it: it is a summary, and summaries lose information.

Two exceptions worth having: a uniform scale commutes with rotation (there is no preferred axis left for the order to matter to — verified), and composition is always associative even though it is not commutative, so (A·B)·C = A·(B·C) and you may bracket a chain however you like. Also verified.

The determinant is an area factor

Here is a question the matrix can answer that nothing in Modules 1 or 2 could: by how much did the transformation change areas?

Start with the unit square — the square whose sides are î and ĵ, area 1. Where does it go? Its sides become c₀ and c₁, so it becomes the parallelogram spanned by the two columns. Its area is therefore the number to compute, and because grid squares everywhere transform the same way, every region in the plane scales by that same factor.

That last point is worth dwelling on, because it is the kind of connection that makes a subject stop feeling like a pile of separate facts. Lesson 2.2 defined

\[ E(A, B, P) = (B_x - A_x)(P_y - A_y) - (B_y - A_y)(P_x - A_x) \] E(A,B,P) = (Bx-Ax)(Py-Ay) - (By-Ay)(Px-Ax)

Put A at the origin, B = c₀ and P = c₁, and every subtraction of A disappears:

\[ E(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{c_0}, \mathbf{c_1}) = c_{0x}c_{1y} - c_{0y}c_{1x} = \det M \] E(origin, c0, c1) = c0.x*c1.y - c0.y*c1.x = det M

Verified exhaustively over 28,561 integer matrices: zero mismatches. The rasterizer's inside test, the sign that Lesson 3.4 will use for back-face culling, and the determinant are one number wearing three hats.

The sign carries the other half of the meaning, and it is exactly the sign Lesson 2.2 was already reading:

det MWhat happenedExample
> 1areas grewscale(3, 2) → 6
= 1areas unchangedany rotation; any shear
between 0 and 1areas shrankscale(0.5) → 0.25
= 0the plane collapsed onto a linecolumns parallel
< 0areas scaled by |det| and the plane flipped overscale(−1, 1) → −1

"Shear preserves area" is the row worth checking against your intuition, because a shear visibly mangles a shape. Push a deck of cards over: each card keeps its width, the stack keeps its height, and no card has changed size. Nothing was gained or lost, only slanted — and the determinant of shear(k, 0) is exactly 1, for every k.

Checked against the rasterizer, not asserted

A claim about area ought to be testable by measuring an area, and we happen to own a rasterizer. So the harness transforms a 140-pixel square, fills it with fill_triangle, counts the lit pixels, and compares against side² × |det|:

The residual is the fill rule counting pixel centres rather than measuring area exactly, so it scales with the perimeter and vanishes as the square grows. The demo runs the same measurement live, at a smaller size, where the error is around 1%.

One more property, free and useful: determinants multiply. Doing two things in a row scales area by one factor and then the other, so det(A·B) = det(A)·det(B) — verified. Which also settles the sign rule: two reflections give a positive determinant, because flipping twice puts you back.

Worked example — the signed area of a familiar triangle

Take the triangle Lessons 2.2 to 2.4 have used throughout — (2,2), (10,4), (4,10) — whose signed area, doubled, is +60.

Transform all three corners by scale(−1, 1), a mirror, and recompute: the answer is −60. Same size, opposite sign — the winding reversed, which is precisely what det = −1 promised.

Now transform by rotation(1.1) · scale(1.7, 0.6), whose determinant is 1.0200. The doubled signed area goes from +60 to +61.2 — a ratio of exactly 1.0200. The determinant does not approximately scale areas; it scales them.

This is also a preview of Lesson 3.4. Back-face culling decides visibility from the sign of a triangle's signed area — so a transformation with a negative determinant turns every front face into a back face. A mesh scaled by (−1, 1, 1) renders inside-out, and now you know why before you meet it.

What a 2×2 cannot do

We should end the theory by being honest about the hole in it, because the hole is the subject of Lesson 2.7 and it will make much more sense if you have already noticed it.

A 2×2 matrix cannot translate. It cannot move everything two units to the right. This is not a limitation of our implementation, and it is not something a cleverer choice of numbers would fix — it is impossible, and §3.1 already proved it in one line: put c = 0 into the scaling rule and you get T(origin) = origin. Every linear transformation pins the origin. A translation does not. Therefore no translation is linear, and no matrix of any size that multiplies a 2-vector can express one.

Why this is annoying in practice

Rotation is about the origin. Scaling is about the origin. But you almost never want to rotate a model about the world origin — you want to rotate it about its own centre, which means: move it to the origin, rotate, move it back. Two translations, sandwiching the one thing we can express.

You can of course write M*v + t and be done with it. Try composing three of those by hand and you will discover why nobody does: the translations do not merge into the matrices, so a chain of ten transformations becomes ten matrices and ten separate vectors, applied in the right order, by you, every time.

Hold that thought. Lesson 2.6 builds the 4×4 machinery for 3-D, and Lesson 2.7 shows that adding a single extra coordinate to a vector makes translation fall inside the matrix after all — one object, one multiplication, chains that compose. The trick is genuinely lovely, and it is much lovelier if you have spent a lesson wanting it.

4Implementation

One new file, and it is short — which is the point. Every function in it is a sentence from §3 written in C++, and none of them is longer than the sentence.

4.1  The type is the lesson

Two vec2s, not a float[4], and that is a deliberate choice rather than a stylistic one. The columns are the images of the basis vectors — that is the entire content of this lesson — so a type whose members are those two vectors makes the idea structural. Print a mat2 in a debugger and you see two arrows with names, not four anonymous floats you have to mentally re-shape.

It also makes the storage convention free rather than something to enforce. Two adjacent vec2s are four adjacent floats in the order c0.x, c0.y, c1.x, c1.y — which is column-major. We did not have to choose it; it fell out of naming the right things. (Verified: the raw floats really do come out as 2, 0, 1, 3 for the worked example, and sizeof(mat2) is exactly four floats with no padding.)

The default is the identity. A default-constructed transformation ought to be the one that does nothing; a zero matrix would collapse the plane to a point, which is a surprising thing to get from writing mat2 m;.

C++ in place — default member initialisers and aggregate init

vec2 c0{1.0f, 0.0f}; gives the member a default and leaves mat2 an aggregate, so mat2 m{{2,0},{1,3}} still works with no constructor written. You get both: a safe default when you say nothing, and brace initialisation when you say everything.

This is why the maths types in this engine have no constructors at all. A constructor would buy nothing here and would cost aggregate initialisation, constexpr-friendliness and the guarantee that the layout is exactly what it looks like — which matters the moment Module 4 uploads one of these to a GPU as raw bytes.

4.2  Applying and composing

That is the whole of matrix arithmetic. The first is §3.2 — x of the first column plus y of the second. The second is §3.6 — each column of the result is where that basis vector ends up after B and then A. Neither mentions an index or a loop, and neither can be written down backwards, because there is nothing in them to get backwards.

4.3  Bridging the two views

§3.3 established that we write in rows and store in columns, so the type offers a way to index the way you write:

Note the argument order: at(row, col), matching how the element is named on paper, even though the lookup goes to the column first. That is the point of the function — it exists so that reading code against a written derivation does not require transposing in your head.

There is no bounds check. That is a different trade from framebuffer::put_pixel, which is bounds-checked because its indices come from arithmetic that can genuinely go out of range — the rule is not "always check" or "never check", it is check where the input can actually be wrong.

4.4  The three constructors, and one thing to notice

Each is its derivation, transcribed. rotation reads as "the first column is where î went, the second is where ĵ went" — and the minus sign is on c1.x, which is the top-right element when written out, exactly as Figure 3 shows. If you have ever put that minus sign in the wrong place, it was almost certainly because you were transcribing a written matrix into row-major storage.

scale and shear are constexpr; rotation is not, because std::cos is not constexpr before C++26. Worth knowing rather than worth fighting — a rotation by a compile-time constant angle is rare, and the two that can be folded at compile time are the ones most likely to appear as constants.

4.5  Determinant and inverse

One line, and its doc comment spends more space on the connection to edge_function than on the arithmetic — because the arithmetic is obvious and the connection is the part that will save you time later.

The inverse is where the design has to make a decision about failure:

A zero determinant means the transformation squashed the plane onto a line, and many different inputs now share one output — so there is nothing to recover and no inverse exists. That is a statement about information, not about arithmetic, and it is worth checking rather than taking on faith. With the columns (2,1) and (4,2), which are parallel: (1,0) and (−1,1) both land on (2,1), and all 1,681 sampled inputs land somewhere on the single line x = 2y. Verified.

Returning zeros rather than dividing anyway keeps a degenerate matrix from becoming a silent NaN that spreads through everything downstream — the same reasoning as normalised() in Lesson 1.7 and barycentric_at in Lesson 2.3. The engine core has no exceptions (a Module 5 decision, taught properly there), so "cannot" is expressed as a defined, checkable value.

The trap in transpose

transpose is included because it is two lines and because it is what people reach for when a transformation comes out backwards. Reach carefully: transposing equals inverting only for a rotation (more generally, only for an orthonormal matrix). Verified both ways — transpose(rotation(0.83)) is its inverse; transpose(scale(2, 0.5)) is not, and is not even close.

Using a transpose as a cheap inverse on something that also scales gives a result that looks almost right, which is the worst kind of wrong. Lesson 3.6 meets this properly, when normals turn out to need a different transform from positions.

4.6  The demo, and one bug worth reporting

The new view draws four things: the image of the integer grid, the transformed unit square, the two basis vectors in the course's axis colours, and an asymmetric glyph.

The glyph is an F and that is not decoration. A blob or a circle looks identical when mirrored, so it cannot show you that a reflection happened; an F cannot hide it. Every one of its ten vertices goes through the same matrix, which is the claim from §1 made visible — one transformation, applied uniformly to a list of points that were not generated from an angle.

Compare that with the trigonometry in §1. There is no angle here, no phase, and no per-vertex special knowledge — just m * applied to whatever the points happen to be. That is the whole upgrade.

The y-flip lives in exactly one function:

mat2 works in the mathematical convention with +y up; the framebuffer has +y down. Rather than sprinkling negations through the drawing code — or, worse, building the flip into the matrices and then being confused about which way rotation() turns — the whole discrepancy is one minus sign, applied at the boundary between the two spaces. Lesson 2.11 gives that boundary a name.

A defect in the first version of this demo, and how it was found

The grid drawing loop originally skipped i == 0, on the reasoning that the axes were being drawn separately. That left the images of the lines x = 0 and y = 0 missing — so the grid cell containing the origin had no left or bottom edge, appeared twice its true size, and the unit square drawn inside it looked as though it did not line up with the grid at all.

Nothing reported an error; the picture was simply subtly wrong, and it was wrong in a way that undermined the exact claim the figure exists to make (the unit square is one cell of the transformed grid). It was caught by rendering the view offscreen and looking at it. The fix is to draw i == 0 as well, slightly brighter — and the separate, fainter pair of straight lines is now unambiguously the original axes, there for reference.

Finally, the determinant readout is measured rather than asserted. The demo fills the transformed unit square into a scratch framebuffer with fill_triangle, counts the lit pixels, and prints that beside unit² × |det|. It is the same check as §3.7's table, running live, using the rasterizer built in Lesson 2.2 to audit the maths written in Lesson 2.5.

What you should see

The engine now opens on the basis-transform view — the new demo is first in the Tab cycle. On screen: a faint grid, a filled blue parallelogram, a red arrow, a green arrow, and a white F.

Read those in order, because each one is a different way of looking at the same four numbers.

  • The red arrow is where (1,0) landed. The green arrow is where (0,1) landed. Those two arrows are the matrix — the readout on the right prints the same four numbers.
  • The blue parallelogram is the transformed unit square, and it is exactly one cell of the transformed grid. Check that: the grid's two brightest lines run along the two arrows.
  • The faint straight grey cross is the original axes, left in place so the transformed frame has something to be measured against.
  • The F is a list of ten points put through the same matrix — the thing the trigonometry of Lessons 2.2–2.4 could not do.

Z cycles the transformation and , / . adjust its one parameter; 0 resets it and Space animates. Work through them:

Checkpoint — six things to do with the demo
  1. Identity. The arrows are one unit along each axis, the square is a square, the grid is square, det = +1.0000. Everything starts here.
  2. Rotation. Hold . and watch the grid turn. The determinant does not move off 1.0000: turning a shape does not change its area.
  3. Scale. Watch the determinant track the product of the two factors, and watch the measured pixel count follow it within about 1%.
  4. Shear. The square becomes a dramatically slanted parallelogram — and the determinant stays at 1.0000 the whole way. This is the row of §3.7's table that looks wrong until you see it.
  5. Push the scale until det goes negative. The readout turns red and says (flipped!), and the F is mirrored. That is what a negative determinant means, and it is why Lesson 3.4's back-face culling will care.
  6. The two composition modes. Each draws the other order as a gold outline. Turn the angle and watch the filled shape and the outline separate — that separation is non-commutativity, live.

In the two composition modes, notice what the readout says: the determinants of both orders are identical while the shapes plainly are not. §3.6's worked example predicted exactly that.

Why the measured area is not exact

The counted pixel total sits within roughly 1–2% of unit² × |det| and wanders as you turn the angle. That is not an error in the determinant — it is the fill rule counting pixel centres, so the discrepancy scales with the perimeter rather than the area and shrinks as the shape grows. At the 140-pixel size the harness uses it is under 0.3%, and for an axis-aligned square it is exact.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Everything rotates the wrong way, and the shear tilts the wrong axis — a transposed matrix
Symptom
Rotations go clockwise when you asked for counter-clockwise. Shears slant vertically when you asked for horizontally. Nothing crashes, nothing looks like garbage — it all just behaves like a mirror image of what you wrote.
Cause
A matrix written down in rows and copied into memory as if it were rows, when the storage is column-major (§3.3). The four numbers are right; two of them have swapped places.
Fix
Check one column against a known answer instead of reading all four numbers. Multiply by (1,0): the result must be the first column, and for a rotation it must be (cos θ, sin θ). The diagnostic worth remembering is that a transpose breaks rotation and shear together — if both are wrong, that is one bug in the layout, not two bugs in the constructors.
Transformations apply in the opposite order to the one you wrote — reading the product left to right
Symptom
You write rotation(a) * scale(s) expecting "rotate, then scale", and get an object that was scaled and then rotated. Objects orbit the origin when they should spin in place; a non-uniform scale stretches along the wrong direction.
Cause
With column vectors and v' = M*v, the rightmost factor is applied first (§3.6). The written order is the reverse of the temporal order.
Fix
Read the product right to left, as a sentence: A * B * C * v is "do C, then B, then A, to v". If you find that permanently uncomfortable, the honest cure is to build chains through a named helper whose parameter order is the order things happen — but never by silently reversing the multiplication, which will collide with every reference you read.
Objects swing around the world origin instead of spinning in place — forgetting that linear means origin-fixed
Symptom
You rotate a sprite that lives at (400, 300) and it flies off in a huge arc instead of turning where it stands.
Cause
Nothing is broken. Every linear transformation fixes the origin (§3.1), so rotation is always rotation about the origin. Your object is 500 units away from it, so it travels 500 units.
Fix
Translate to the origin, transform, translate back — M*(v − centre) + centre. Note that the two translations cannot be folded into M, which is precisely the limitation §3.8 leaves open and Lesson 2.7 removes. Feeling the awkwardness here is the point; do not paper over it yet.
A shape collapses to a line and never comes back — a zero determinant
Symptom
Geometry vanishes, or flattens into a streak. Applying the inverse does not restore it. Downstream code starts producing NaN or zeros.
Cause
The two columns became parallel — often from a scale factor reaching zero, or from a matrix built out of a normalised vector that was zero-length. The transformation folded the plane onto a line, and that is genuinely irreversible: distinct inputs now share an output.
Fix
Test determinant(m) != 0 before inverting, and treat a determinant near zero as a red flag in its own right — an almost singular matrix has an inverse with enormous entries, which is numerically explosive rather than merely wrong. Our inverse returns zeros rather than dividing by zero so the failure is a defined value you can test, not a NaN that spreads silently.
Using a transpose as a cheap inverse — true for rotations only
Symptom
Undoing a transformation almost works. Positions come back a little stretched; with lighting (Module 3) normals end up slightly off, and surfaces are subtly too bright or too dark on the scaled axes.
Cause
transpose(M) == inverse(M) holds for rotations, and for nothing else. It is an easy habit to pick up because rotations are what you test with, and it fails the moment a non-uniform scale enters the chain.
Fix
Use inverse unless you can state why the matrix is orthonormal. The check is one line: transpose(M) * M must be the identity. Verified both ways in the harness — it is for rotation(0.83) and is not for scale(2, 0.5).

8Exercises

2.5.1Build a matrix from the picturewarm-up

Without writing any code, give the 2×2 matrix for each of these. Then add a mode to the demo for one of them and check.

  1. Reflection in the x-axis.
  2. Reflection in the line y = x.
  3. A quarter turn clockwise.
  4. Squash everything onto the x-axis.
Hint

Every one of them is the same two questions. Draw î, ask where it goes, write that down as the first column. Repeat for ĵ. Do not try to recall a formula for any of these — there is nothing to recall.

Solution
  1. î stays at (1,0); ĵ flips to (0,−1). So scale(1, −1), determinant −1 — a flip, as expected.
  2. Reflecting in y = x swaps the axes: î → (0,1), ĵ → (1,0). Columns (0,1) and (1,0); determinant 0·0 − 1·1 = −1. Also a flip, which is right — a reflection always is.
  3. î → (0,−1), ĵ → (1,0). This is rotation(−90°), and it is the transpose of the quarter turn from §3.4 — which is also its inverse, exactly as §4.5 said it would be for a rotation.
  4. î → (1,0), ĵ → (0,0). Determinant 0: the plane has been folded onto a line, and inverse will return zeros.

2.5.2Predict the compositionwarm-up

Take H = shear(1, 0) and R = rotation(90°). Work out H·R and R·H by hand — send each basis vector through in turn — then check both against the code. Which one turns the unit square into a shape with a vertical edge?

Hint

Do it as sentences, not arithmetic. For H·R: "î turns to (0,1), and then the shear slides things right in proportion to height, so (0,1) slides to (1,1)." That is the first column, and you never multiplied anything.

Solution

H·R (rotate first): î → (0,1) → (1,1); ĵ → (−1,0) → (−1,0) (no height, so no slide). Columns (1,1), (−1,0).

R·H (shear first): î → (1,0) → (0,1); ĵ → (1,1) → (−1,1). Columns (0,1), (−1,1).

Different, as expected. R·H has first column (0,1) — straight up — so that is the one with a vertical edge. Both determinants are 1, since a shear and a rotation each preserve area, and determinants multiply.

2.5.3Rotate about a point that is not the originmedium

Add a mode that spins the F about its own centre rather than about the origin. Implement it as translate-rotate-translate, then write down honestly what is unsatisfying about the result as a piece of design.

Hint

m * (v − c) + c, where c is the glyph's centre. It is three lines. The interesting part of this exercise is not getting it working.

Solution sketch

It works immediately. The unsatisfying part is that the transformation is no longer a matrix — it is a matrix and a vector, and a rule for combining them that lives in your drawing code rather than in the type.

Try composing two of them and the cost becomes concrete: to do "(M₂, t₂) after (M₁, t₁)" you have to work out that the combined pair is (M₂M₁, M₂t₁ + t₂) — a rule you have to derive, store and get right, where matrices alone just multiply. Ten transformations in a chain means carrying ten vectors alongside ten matrices.

That is precisely the pain Lesson 2.7 removes. Do this exercise before reading it; the homogeneous-coordinate trick is unremarkable if you have not first wanted it.

2.5.4Measure the determinant yourselfmedium

§3.7 checks the area claim by rasterising and counting pixels, and finds errors of a fraction of a percent. Reproduce that, then investigate the error: plot it against the size of the square. What does it scale with, and why?

Hint

Which pixels are at risk of being counted wrongly? Not the ones deep inside the shape — only the ones the boundary passes through. Now count how many pixels the boundary passes through as a function of side length, and compare with how many are inside.

Solution

The absolute error grows with the perimeter (proportional to the side) while the total grows with the area (side squared), so the relative error falls roughly as 1/side. Doubling the square should roughly halve the percentage error, and it does: at 140 px the harness sees 0.26% for a rotation, and at the demo's 44 px it is around 1%.

Two further things worth noticing. An axis-aligned square is exact at any size, because the fill rule's top-left convention makes shared boundaries come out exactly right — that is Lesson 2.2 §3.6 paying off in an unexpected place. And this is a general lesson about verifying continuous claims with discrete measurements: know which way your measurement is biased and how fast the bias vanishes, or you will mistake a sampling artifact for a bug in the maths.

2.5.5Find the transformationopen

The inverse problem. Given a shape and the same shape after an unknown linear transformation, recover the matrix. Then answer the harder question: when is the answer unique, and what exactly do you need to observe for it to be?

Hint

You know the matrix if you know where two vectors went — but not just any two. What is special about î and ĵ that made §2.3's argument work, and what goes wrong if you pick two vectors without that property?

Solution sketch

Observing where any two linearly independent vectors u and w land is enough. Their images are Mu and Mw; assemble U = [u w] and V = [Mu Mw] as matrices, and then MU = V, so M = V·U⁻¹ — which exists exactly when U is invertible, i.e. exactly when the two vectors are not parallel.

If they are parallel you have really only made one observation, and infinitely many matrices agree with it. The special thing about î and ĵ was never that they are axis-aligned — it is that they are independent, and that U is the identity so the inversion is free.

Worth noticing where this shows up for real: it is how you fit a transformation to observed data, and the same structure — more observations than unknowns, solved by least squares — is how camera calibration and animation retargeting work. It is also why a degenerate (zero-determinant) observation set cannot be rescued by measuring more carefully; the information is not there to be measured.

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • It can transform points it did not generate. One matrix applies to a list of vertices from anywhere — which is the thing the per-vertex trigonometry of Lessons 2.2–2.4 fundamentally could not do, and the reason Lesson 3.5 can load a mesh.
  • A matrix is two answers, not a grid of numbers. Where does î go, where does ĵ go — write them as columns and every rule falls out: multiplication, rotation, composition, all of it derived rather than memorised.
  • Composition is one multiply, and the order is forced. A·B means B first, because (A·B)v must equal A(Bv). Not a convention to look up — a consequence.
  • The determinant is an area factor with a sign, it multiplies along a chain, and it is the same expression as edge_function — verified over 28,561 matrices, and checked against the rasterizer by counting pixels.
  • Written in rows, stored in columns, with the storage falling out of naming the columns properly rather than being imposed.
  • And it knows what it cannot do: no linear transformation moves the origin, so no 2×2 translates. Proved in one line, left open on purpose.

The habit worth carrying forward: when a piece of mathematics feels like a list of rules to memorise, look for the one question it is all answering. Here it was "where do the basis vectors land?" — and once you have it, the rotation matrix, the multiplication rule and the determinant stop being three facts and become three consequences.

Next: 2.6 — Building mat4 by Hand. The same question in three dimensions, and then one more column than you might expect. Everything derived here survives the move: the columns are still where the basis vectors land, the product still means "do the right one first", the determinant is still a scale factor — for volume now instead of area.

And then Lesson 2.7 spends the fourth component, and translation walks in through a door we spent this lesson establishing was locked.
