Module 2 — Software Rasterizer I · Lesson 2.10

Perspective from Similar Triangles

This is the keystone of Module 2. Everything so far has moved geometry around rigidly; nothing has made a distant thing smaller. Perspective is that, and it is astonishingly simple once you see it: a single divide by depth, which falls straight out of a diagram of similar triangles. We derive it from that diagram before any matrix appears — and packaging it as a matrix is where w finally stops being 1 and the perspective divide, deferred since Lesson 2.7, gets its name.

1The Problem

Run the demo from Lesson 2.9 and orbit the camera. It works — you can look at the scene from anywhere. But look harder, and something is wrong in a way that is easy to miss because you have been staring at it since Lesson 2.6:

The failure mode — a world with no distance

The three cubes are all the same size on screen, no matter how far each one is from the camera. The floor grid's rails run dead parallel to the horizon instead of converging. And when you dolly the camera in with [-] / [=] — moving it physically closer to the scene — nothing changes size at all. The whole thing looks like a technical drawing, an architect's elevation: correct, measured, and completely flat. It is a picture of a world in which distance does not exist.

This is not a bug. It is exactly what our projection does, honestly: to_screen3 takes a view-space point, keeps x and y, and throws z — the depth — away. That is an orthographic projection, and orthographic projection is genuinely useful (CAD, isometric games, shadow maps later in the course). It is just not how eyes or cameras or lenses see. Your eye makes the far cube smaller. A photograph makes the rails converge. We threw away the one number — depth — that carries all of that.

In this lesson's demo you can toggle between the two with [P], and it is worth doing before reading on. Under orthographic, the scene is flat and dollying is inert. Press [P] and the far cubes shrink, the floor rails bend toward a vanishing point, and dollying finally zooms. The difference between those two pictures is one matrix, and this lesson is that matrix. The remarkable part is how little there is to it.

2Building Intuition

The pinhole and the projection plane

Forget matrices entirely. Think about the oldest camera there is: a pinhole. Light from the world passes through a single point — the eye, the pinhole, the lens's centre — and lands on a surface behind it, the film. Every point in the world is connected to its image on the film by a straight line through that one point.

We will put the "film" in front of the eye rather than behind it (it flips the image, and putting it in front is the standard convention that avoids the flip). Call it the projection plane, or the near plane: a flat screen sitting at some small distance n directly in front of the camera. To find where a world point appears, draw the straight line from the eye to that point, and see where it crosses the plane. Figure 1 is the whole idea.

Twice as far, half as tall

Now the key observation, and it needs no algebra — just the picture. Take two points at the same height y, one twice as far away as the other. The near ray and the far ray both start at the eye. The near point's ray is steeper; the far point's ray is shallower, because it has to travel twice the depth to reach the same height. So the far ray crosses the near plane lower — at half the height, in fact, because it is exactly half as steep.

Double the depth, halve the image. Triple the depth, third the image. The projected size is inversely proportional to depth, and that single inverse relationship is perspective in its entirety: the vanishing point (parallel rails meet at infinite depth, where the image shrinks to zero), the way a hallway tapers, the reason a coin can eclipse the moon. All of it is "divide by depth".

You have already seen these exact numbers

In Lesson 2.7 §3.6 we broke a matrix's bottom row and watched x/w come out as 1.0, 0.5, 0.2, 0.1 for a point at depths z = −1, −2, −5, −10. That was not a curiosity — it was this, arriving early. The w we made equal to −z back then is the depth we are about to divide by. This lesson is where that loose thread gets tied off: the perspective divide is the division by w, and w is the depth.

Drag the point's depth below and watch its projected height on the near plane. It is a live version of Figure 1, and the numbers it prints are the ones we are about to derive.

3The Theory

Similar triangles give the divide

Now put coordinates on Figure 1 and read off the algebra. The camera is at the origin, looking down −z. A world point sits at view-space coordinates (x, y, z), with z negative because it is in front of the camera, so its distance from the eye is −z. The near plane is at distance n.

The eye, the point, and the axis form one triangle; the eye, the projected image, and the axis form a smaller one inside it. They share the angle at the eye and both have a right angle at the axis, so they are similar — same shape, scaled. Corresponding sides are therefore in the same ratio:

\[ \frac{y'}{n} = \frac{y}{-z} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad y' = \frac{n\,y}{-z} \] y' / n = y / (-z) => y' = n * y / (-z)

The projected height y′ is to the near distance n as the true height y is to the depth −z. Solve for y′ and you get y′ = n·y/(−z), and the same argument runs identically for x: x′ = n·x/(−z). In prose, because the equation must never be the only carrier of the fact: the image coordinate is the world coordinate scaled by the near distance and divided by the depth. Divide by depth. That is the whole of it.

Worked example — the numbers from Lesson 2.7, now geometry

Put the near plane at n = 1 and take a point with x = 1, swept back in depth. Then x′ = 1·1/(−z) = 1/(−z):

  • z = −1: x′ = 1/1 = 1.0
  • z = −2: x′ = 1/2 = 0.5
  • z = −5: x′ = 1/5 = 0.2
  • z = −10: x′ = 1/10 = 0.1

These are exactly the numbers Lesson 2.7 §3.6 produced by dividing x by a w we had set to −z. Back then it was a hint that w could be more than a padding byte; now it is the projected position of a point, derived from a picture. The thread is tied.

A matrix can't divide — so it defers

Here is the obstacle, and the elegant way around it. We want to fold x′ = n·x/(−z) into our matrix pipeline, so a projection is just one more matrix in the chain clip = P · view. But a matrix is a linear map — every output component is a weighted sum of the input components. Dividing one coordinate x by another coordinate z is not a weighted sum; it is not something any matrix can do. A matrix can scale x, it cannot divide x by z.

So the matrix does not do the divide. It does the one thing it can do that sets the divide up: it copies the depth −z into the fourth component, w. Then a separate step afterwards divides all the components by w — and dividing x by w = −z is exactly x/(−z), the perspective shrink we wanted. This is why the homogeneous w has existed since Lesson 2.7, and this is the third case it flagged: a projection matrix produces a w that is neither 0 nor 1, and the division by it is the perspective divide.

Two spaces, because of one deferred divide

This deferral is exactly why the pipeline has both a "clip space" and a "normalized device coordinates" (NDC) stage. Clip space is the output of the projection matrix, before the divide — where w still holds the depth. NDC is what you get after dividing by w. The names sound like jargon until you see that they are simply the two sides of the one divide the matrix could not do. (Clip space is also where clipping happens, Lesson 3.3 — hence the name — because it is the last place a straight edge is still straight before the non-linear divide bends things.)

Concretely, the bottom row of the projection matrix is (0, 0, −1, 0). Applied to a view-space point (x, y, z, 1), that row computes w = 0·x + 0·y + (−1)·z + 0·1 = −z. The matrix has copied the depth into w. Everything else in the matrix is bookkeeping to make x, y and depth land in the right ranges after the divide — and that bookkeeping is the next three sections.

Field of view and aspect: filling [-1,1]

After the divide, the GPU wants x and y in the range [−1, 1] — the edges of the screen (Conventions §4). But x′ = n·x/(−z) lands in whatever range the near plane's physical size dictates, which depends on how wide a view we want. That "how wide" is the field of view.

Picture the frustum side-on (Figure 2). The vertical field of view fovy is the total angle from the bottom of the view to the top. At depth −z, the top edge of the view is at height (−z)·tan(fovy/2). We want a point at that top edge to land at y_ndc = +1. So we need to scale y′ so that the edge maps to 1:

\[ y_{ndc} = \frac{f\,y}{-z}, \qquad f = \cot\!\left(\tfrac{fovy}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{\tan(fovy/2)} \] y_ndc = f * y / (-z), with f = cot(fovy/2) = 1 / tan(fovy/2)

Check it: at the top edge, y/(−z) = tan(fovy/2), so y_ndc = f·tan(fovy/2) = cot(fovy/2)·tan(fovy/2) = 1. The edge lands exactly on 1, as intended. The quantity f = cot(fovy/2) is sometimes called the focal length; a narrow field of view (small angle) gives a large f — a telephoto zoom — and a wide field of view gives a small f.

One more adjustment for x. Screens are wider than they are tall, so a square in the world must not become a square that fills the screen — it should be as wide as it is tall in pixels, which on a 16:9 screen means narrower in NDC. We divide the x scale by the aspect ratio (width ÷ height):

\[ x_{ndc} = \frac{f}{\text{aspect}}\cdot\frac{x}{-z}, \qquad y_{ndc} = f\cdot\frac{y}{-z} \] x_ndc = (f/aspect) * x / (-z); y_ndc = f * y / (-z)

Depth into [0,1], and why it's non-linear

We have x and y sorted. What about z? After the divide, the GPU wants depth in [0, 1] — 0 at the near plane, 1 at the far plane (this is SDL_GPU's range, and D3D12's and Metal's and Vulkan's; OpenGL uses [−1, 1], and getting this wrong is a classic silent bug). But there is a catch that makes depth subtler than x and y: whatever the matrix writes into clip.z, it is going to be divided by w = −z along with everything else. So we cannot just write a linear function of z and be done; we have to write a clip.z such that clip.z / (−z) comes out in [0,1].

Write clip.z = A·z + B (the most a matrix can do — linear in z). After the divide, z_ndc = (A·z + B)/(−z). We need two things: z_ndc = 0 at z = −near, and z_ndc = 1 at z = −far. Two equations, two unknowns:

\[ \frac{-A\,\text{near} + B}{\text{near}} = 0, \quad \frac{-A\,\text{far} + B}{\text{far}} = 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; A = \frac{-\text{far}}{\text{far}-\text{near}}, \quad B = \frac{-\text{far}\cdot\text{near}}{\text{far}-\text{near}} \] solving: A = -far/(far-near), B = -far*near/(far-near)

That is the whole depth story, except for the part that matters most in practice — the mapping is not linear in depth. Because we divide by −z, points near the camera get a huge share of the [0,1] range and points far away get almost none. Push real numbers through it (near = 1, far = 100):

Worked example — depth is lopsided
  • z = −1 (near plane): z_ndc = 0.000
  • z = −2: z_ndc = 0.505  — half the entire range, one unit in
  • z = −5: z_ndc = 0.808
  • z = −10: z_ndc = 0.909
  • z = −100 (far plane): z_ndc = 1.000

By z = −2 — one unit past a near plane at 1 — we have already spent half of the depth buffer's range. The other 98 units of the frustum share the remaining half. This is why depth precision is lavish up close and starving in the distance, why z-fighting (two far surfaces flickering because their depths round to the same value) is a distant-geometry problem, and why the single most effective fix is to push the near plane out: moving near from 0.01 to 1 buys back an enormous amount of far precision. Lesson 3.1's z-buffer is where this stops being trivia and starts being a bug you have to manage.

The handedness flip lives here

One convention to make explicit, because it is invisible in the algebra and ruinous when wrong. Our view space is right-handed: +x right, +y up, −z forward (Conventions §2). SDL_GPU's clip space is left-handed: +x right, +y up, and +z going into the screen toward the far plane (Conventions §4–5). Something has to flip the handedness between them, and that something is the projection matrix — it is the one border crossing in the whole pipeline.

You can see the flip in the entries we just derived. View-space forward is −z (negative, in front), but depth comes out 0 at near and 1 at far — increasing into the screen. The bottom row's −1 (turning −z into a positive w) and the signs of A and B together perform the reversal. We do not add a separate "flip matrix"; the projection is built to land in SDL_GPU's clip space directly, which is what makes the Module 4 port a change of API and not a change of maths. (One thing we do not flip here: +y stays up. The +y-up-to-+y-down flip for the framebuffer is the viewport's job, Lesson 2.11.)

⚠ Verify

SDL_GPU's NDC depth range is [0, 1] and its NDC +y is up — verified on the Conventions page against the SDL wiki and confirmed in §6 below, where the demo's own projection lands the near plane at z_ndc = 0 and the far at 1. If you are adapting this matrix for OpenGL, its NDC depth is [−1, 1] and the A, B entries differ; do not copy this one blind.

The matrix, assembled and walked

Collect the four results — x scaled by f/aspect, y by f, depth by (A, B), and −z into w — and the projection matrix writes itself:

\[ P = \begin{bmatrix} f/\text{aspect} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & f & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & A & B \\ 0 & 0 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \] P rows: [f/aspect,0,0,0], [0,f,0,0], [0,0,A,B], [0,0,-1,0]

With fovy = 60°, aspect = 16/9, near = 1, far = 100, that is f = cot(30°) = 1.732, f/aspect = 0.974, and A = B = −1.010. Now walk one point all the way through, so the pipeline is concrete rather than a diagram:

Worked example — a view-space point to NDC

Take p_view = (2, 1, −10) — 2 right, 1 up, 10 units in front.

Project (multiply by P). Each clip component is a row dotted with (2, 1, −10, 1):

  • clip.x = 0.974·2 = 1.949
  • clip.y = 1.732·1 = 1.732
  • clip.z = A·(−10) + B = (−1.010)(−10) + (−1.010) = 9.091
  • clip.w = −1·(−10) = 10.0  ← the depth, copied into w

So clip = (1.949, 1.732, 9.091, 10.0). This is where w stops being 1. Divide every component by w = 10 — the perspective divide:

  • ndc.x = 1.949/10 = 0.195
  • ndc.y = 1.732/10 = 0.173
  • ndc.z = 9.091/10 = 0.909

ndc = (0.195, 0.173, 0.909). The point is slightly right of centre, slightly up, and deep (0.909, most of the way to the far plane in the buffer even though it is only a tenth of the way there in metres — the non-linearity again). All three are in range: x, y ∈ [−1, 1], z ∈ [0, 1]. The harness confirms every digit.

That is the keystone laid. From a matrix's inability to divide, we got a two-step projection — a linear matrix that stashes depth in w, then a divide that finishes the job — and the whole of perspective is that divide.

4Implementation

Two engine functions and a demo rewiring. First, the perspective divide gets the named home Lesson 2.7 promised it. It is deliberately a separate function from xyz(), so "drop w" and "divide by w" can never be confused at a call site:

No guard on w. A zero or negative w means a point on or behind the plane through the eye, and the correct fix is to clip that geometry away before it reaches here — which is Lesson 3.3. Branching inside an accessor would paper over the real problem and slow the common case; the demo instead keeps its vertices in front of the camera so w stays safely positive, and §6 verifies it.

Then the projection matrix itself, in mat4.hpp beside the other factories. It is the derivation transcribed — f, then A and B, then the four columns:

Remember the return value is stored column-major, so the four brace groups are the columns. Read the third column, (0, 0, A, −1): its A is clip.z's dependence on z, and its −1 is the entry that copies −z into w. Written out as rows it is exactly the matrix from §3.6.

The demo change is where it becomes a picture. The rendering path now carries every point through the projection and the divide before it reaches a pixel. All three steps live in one small helper:

Three lines, three pipeline stages: proj * point(v_view) is the projection into clip space; perspective_divide is the divide into NDC; and the last expression is the viewport transform — mapping NDC's [−1,1] onto the framebuffer rectangle, with the −ndc.y doing the +y-up to +y-down flip. That viewport step is doing real work and deserves its own treatment, which it gets next lesson; here it is three constants.

C++ in place — passing the projection by const&

project takes the matrix as const engine::mat4&, and so do line3, draw_cube and the rest. A mat4 is 64 bytes; copying it into every one of the thousands of per-frame calls would be pure waste, and none of them mutate it. A const reference hands over the address, promises not to change it, and the compiler enforces the promise. This is the reference-versus-value judgement from Lesson 1.7 at a larger size: small types (vec3, 12 bytes) go by value because a reference would cost more than the copy; big ones (mat4) go by const&.

Because the whole difference between perspective and orthographic is which matrix you pass to project, the [P] toggle is nothing but a choice of matrix — the same project, line3 and draw code run either way:

The orthographic matrix is built by the demo (a general orthographic() belongs in the engine eventually — 2-D overlays, shadow maps — but nothing yet needs it beyond this comparison, so it stays local, and Lesson 2.11 owns the viewport/ortho machinery). It keeps w = 1, so the very same perspective_divide runs and simply divides by one — which is why the comparison is honest: the only thing that differs between the two pictures is whether the matrix put depth into w.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Everything collapses to a dot — the whole scene at the centre
Symptom
The projected scene is a single point or a tiny smear in the middle of the screen.
Cause
The perspective divide was skipped. You multiplied by the projection matrix but never divided by w, so you are looking at clip space directly — where every point's coordinates are scaled by wildly different w values and nothing lines up.
Fix
Call perspective_divide (or divide by w) after the projection matrix and before the viewport. The pipeline is project then divide then viewport; missing the middle step is the single most common perspective bug.
The image is upside-down or inside-out — depth or y inverted
Symptom
Near and far are swapped (distant things are bigger), or the whole scene is mirrored, or depth testing (from Lesson 3.1 on) rejects exactly the surfaces it should keep.
Cause
A sign error in the projection: the bottom row is (0,0,+1,0) instead of (0,0,−1,0), so w = +z (negative, for a point in front) and the divide flips everything; or near and far were passed swapped so A and B invert the depth.
Fix
Check the two anchors by hand: a point at z = −near must give z_ndc = 0 and a point at z = −far must give z_ndc = 1, with a positive w. If w is negative for a point you can see, the bottom row's sign is wrong.
Depth precision is terrible far away — flickering distant surfaces
Symptom
Distant coplanar surfaces (a decal on a wall, two overlapping far walls) flicker between each other as the camera moves — z-fighting.
Cause
Not a bug in the matrix — it is the non-linear depth mapping of §3.4 working as designed. Depth resolution is concentrated near the camera, so far surfaces whose true depths differ slightly round to the same buffer value. A near plane set very small (e.g. 0.001) makes it dramatically worse, because it hands even more of the range to the first few centimetres.
Fix
Push the near plane out as far as the scene allows — going from 0.01 to 0.5 buys back huge amounts of far precision. (Advanced options, past this lesson: a floating-point depth buffer with a reversed [1,0] mapping, which pairs beautifully with the non-linearity — Exercise 2.10.5 and Lesson 3.1.)
The scene is stretched or squashed — circles become ellipses
Symptom
Everything looks horizontally stretched or vertically squashed; a world sphere renders as an ellipse.
Cause
The aspect passed to perspective does not match the actual width-to-height ratio of the viewport rectangle you draw into. The matrix pre-corrects x by aspect; if the viewport's real shape disagrees, the correction is wrong.
Fix
Make the two agree: aspect = viewport_width / viewport_height. In the demo the viewport rectangle is deliberately 16:9 (k_vp_half_w / k_vp_half_h = 16/9) to match the aspect handed to the matrix. Resize one, resize the other.
Geometry vanishes or smears when the camera gets close — edges disappear near the camera
Symptom
As the camera approaches a surface, parts of it wink out or stretch to infinity instead of filling the screen.
Cause
Geometry crossing the near plane. A vertex with −z < near has w approaching zero (dividing by nearly nothing) or going negative (behind the eye, mirrored). The demo's project guard drops any line with such an endpoint, which is why an edge disappears rather than smearing — a crude stand-in for real clipping.
Fix
Proper near-plane clipping — trimming each edge at the near plane and emitting a new vertex — is Lesson 3.3, and it is not optional in a real renderer. Until then, keep geometry in front of the camera (the demo clamps the dolly so it never gets close enough to matter) and know that the vanishing edges are the symptom clipping exists to cure.
It works in this course but not in OpenGL — depth washed out or clipped
Symptom
You reuse this matrix in an OpenGL project and half the scene is clipped away, or depth testing behaves strangely.
Cause
NDC depth range. This matrix targets SDL_GPU / D3D12 / Vulkan / Metal, whose NDC depth is [0, 1]. OpenGL's default NDC depth is [−1, 1], so a matrix built for one is wrong for the other — the A and B entries differ.
Fix
Use the projection matched to your API's clip space. For [−1, 1] depth, A = −(far+near)/(far−near) and B = −2·far·near/(far−near). The course is [0,1] throughout because SDL_GPU is (Conventions §4).

8Exercises

2.10.1Project a point by handwarm-up

Using fovy = 90°, aspect = 1, near = 1, far = 9, project the view-space point (3, 0, −3) to NDC by hand. Is it on screen?

Hint

f = cot(45°) = 1, so f/aspect = 1. Compute A = −far/(far−near) and B = −far·near/(far−near), form clip, then divide by w = −z. "On screen" means x, y ∈ [−1, 1] and z ∈ [0, 1].

Solution

A = −9/8 = −1.125, B = −9/8 = −1.125. Then clip = (1·3, 0, (−1.125)(−3) + (−1.125), −(−3)) = (3, 0, 2.25, 3). Divide by w = 3: ndc = (1.0, 0.0, 0.75). It sits exactly on the right edge (x = 1) — which makes sense: with a 90° horizontal field of view and aspect 1, a point 3 across at 3 deep is at 45°, precisely the edge of the view. z = 0.75 is in range, so yes, on screen (just).

2.10.2Dolly zoom vs FOV zoomguided

There are two ways to "zoom": move the camera closer (dolly), or narrow the field of view. They look different, and the difference is a famous cinematic effect. Make fovy adjustable at runtime (say [ and ]), then try to keep the selected object the same on-screen size while changing fovy — by dollying to compensate. What happens to the background?

Hint

Rebuild scene_perspective each frame from a mutable fovy instead of the const at namespace scope. Narrowing fovy magnifies everything uniformly; dollying magnifies near things more than far ones. Compensating one with the other holds your subject fixed while the background rushes in or falls away.

Solution sketch

This is the dolly zoom (the "Vertigo effect"): hold the subject constant while the background's apparent depth changes dramatically. It works because dolly and FOV act on the 1/z falloff differently — dolly changes every object's z, FOV scales all projected sizes equally. The exercise is really about internalising that "zoom" is two unrelated operations that happen to both make a subject bigger. A camera system (Module 5) exposes both because they are not interchangeable.

2.10.3Read a frustum out of a projection matrixmedium

Given only a projection matrix (its 16 numbers), recover the fovy, aspect, near and far it was built from. Write a function that does it and check it round-trips against perspective for several inputs.

Hint

Invert the construction. f is entry (1,1), so fovy = 2·atan(1/f). aspect = f / entry(0,0). For the planes, you have two equations — A = −far/(far−near) and B = −far·near/(far−near) — in two unknowns; solve them. (A tidy route: near = B/A, then far = B/(A+1).)

Solution sketch

near = B/A and far = B/(A+1) fall straight out of the two definitions. This is genuinely useful — engines and debuggers extract frustum planes from the composed view-projection matrix all the time (for frustum culling in Module 6), and this is the one-object warm-up for that. It also cements that the matrix is not a black box: every number in it came from a parameter you can read back.

2.10.4Watch depth precision diemedium

Add a HUD readout of the selected corner's z_ndc, then quantise it as a 16-bit depth buffer would (round(z_ndc · 65535) / 65535) and show both. Move the near plane from 0.3 down to 0.01 and orbit out to the far cubes. How much world depth now maps to a single quantised step out there?

Hint

The gap between adjacent representable depths, converted back to world z, is what you are after. Two far points whose world z differs by less than that gap are indistinguishable to the buffer — they z-fight. Because the mapping is 1/z, that world gap grows quadratically with distance and shrinks linearly with the near plane.

Solution sketch

With near = 0.01, far-plane depth resolution collapses — a single 16-bit step can span metres of world depth at the far end, which is z-fighting made quantitative. Pull near back to 0.5 and the far resolution improves by orders of magnitude. This is the exercise that turns §3.4's "depth is non-linear" from a fact into an instinct: the near plane is the most important knob on depth precision, and it costs nothing to turn. Lesson 3.1 builds the actual z-buffer this protects.

2.10.5Reversed-Zopen

Modern engines often map near to 1 and far to 0 — "reversed-Z" — paired with a floating-point depth buffer. Derive the A and B for that mapping, explain why it dramatically improves far-plane precision, and describe what else has to change (the depth comparison direction, the clear value). Would it help our software rasterizer?

Solution sketch

Swap the two anchors: solve for z_ndc = 1 at near and 0 at far, giving A = near/(far−near), B = far·near/(far−near). The magic is in the interaction with floating point: 1/z crowds precision near z_ndc = 0 (the far plane, in reversed-Z), and floats also crowd precision near 0 — the two non-uniform distributions cancel, leaving depth resolution nearly uniform across the whole frustum. It is one of the highest-value, lowest-cost changes in a real renderer. The catches: the depth test flips to "greater", and the buffer clears to 0. For our integer-free CPU rasterizer it helps less (we control the depth format directly in Lesson 3.1), but knowing the trick is the point — it is exactly the kind of technique the course exists to make you able to read and adopt from a paper.

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • See in perspective. There is a perspective matrix and a perspective_divide, and the demo renders a genuinely 3-D scene — distant things smaller, rails converging, dolly zooming. The chain is now model → world → view → clip → NDC.
  • Understand why it works. Perspective is one divide by depth, from similar triangles. A matrix cannot divide, so it stashes −z in w and defers — which is why w stops being 1, why clip space and NDC are distinct, and what the perspective divide is.
  • Reason about depth. Depth is stored non-linearly, lavish up close and starved far away; the near plane is the master control on precision, and this is where z-fighting is born.
  • Match the GPU. The matrix lands in SDL_GPU's clip space exactly — z ∈ [0,1], the handedness flip absorbed — so Module 4 changes the API, not the maths.

Next: 2.11 — The Viewport Transform. We have been quietly mapping NDC to framebuffer pixels with three hand-picked constants and a −ndc.y flip, and calling it "the viewport" in passing. Next lesson gives that step its real derivation and its own home: the exact affine map from the [−1,1] cube to a rectangle of pixels, the +y-up-to-+y-down flip named properly at last, and the depth range carried through to the z-buffer. Then Lesson 2.12 lights the whole chain up end to end — a spinning wireframe mesh, every line of which you can explain.
