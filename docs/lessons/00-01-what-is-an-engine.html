1The Problem

Ask ten programmers what a game engine is and you will get ten answers, most of them lists. "It's got a renderer, physics, audio, a scene graph, an editor…" That is not a definition; it is an inventory. It tells you what is in the box without telling you what the box is for, and it will not help you make a single decision.

This matters more than it sounds, because you are about to make roughly four hundred hours of decisions. Here is the failure mode, and it is worth looking at directly, because it is the most common way ambitious engine projects die:

The failure mode: a pile of features

Someone starts with the inventory. They write a renderer. Then a physics library. Then an audio mixer. Each part is competent in isolation. Then they try to make a game with it, and discover the physics wants to run at a different rate than the renderer, the audio stutters whenever a texture loads, and adding a character to the scene means touching six files.

Nothing is broken. Every component works. But the parts were designed to be good, not to be good together, on time, sixty times a second — and that last clause is the entire discipline. They built the inventory. They did not build the engine.

So the inventory definition fails a basic test: it makes wrong predictions. It predicts that an engine is a library of game stuff you call when you need it. Let us check that prediction against reality, because the way it fails is the door into everything else.

2Building Intuition

2.1 An engine is not a library

Think about how you use std::sort. You have data, you decide when to sort it, you call the function, it returns, you carry on. You own the flow of control. The library is a tool on a shelf; you take it down when you want it and put it back.

Now think about how you use a UI toolkit. You do not call it in a loop asking "has anyone clicked yet?" You register a handler and hand over control, and it calls you when something happens. That inversion has a name — inversion of control — and it is what separates a framework from a library. The framework owns the flow; your code is a passenger.

An engine is a framework that went further, in three specific ways. Figure 1 lays the three side by side, because the differences are precisely where the interesting design pressure lives.

Figure 1's rightmost panel is the whole lesson in a picture. Two properties make an engine an engine, and neither appears in the inventory definition:

  • It has a heartbeat. A framework is idle until something happens. An engine is never idle — it is producing frames whether or not anything happened, because the display demands a new image sixty times a second regardless of whether the world changed.
  • It has a deadline. This is the one that matters, and everything else in this course descends from it.

2.2 The heartbeat

Here is the mental image worth keeping for the next four hundred hours. A library is a toolbox on the wall. A framework is a factory line, and you fill in the stations. An engine is a heart.

A heart does not beat when convenient. It beats on a schedule, it beats whether you are thinking about it or not, and the interesting failure mode is not "it stopped" — it is "it was late". Your game code is a passenger riding a rhythm it does not control.

You might be wondering whether this is just a poetic way of saying "there is a while loop in main()". It is not, and the difference shows up the moment you ask what happens when the loop takes too long. Hold that thought for about four paragraphs, because it is the crux.

2.3 "Real-time" does not mean "fast"

Engines are real-time systems, and that word does not mean what most people assume. A real-time system is not one that is fast. It is one that is on time.

Those come apart immediately. Consider two engines rendering the same scene:

EngineAverage frameWorst frameFeels like
A10 ms40 msA stutter you can see. Unshippable.
B15 ms16 msGlass smooth.

Engine A is 50% faster on average and it is the worse engine — not marginally, but categorically. Nobody perceives an average. People perceive the worst frame, because the worst frame is the one that visibly hitches, and one hitch per second is what the player remembers.

A train that arrives early is not on time. A frame that renders in 2 ms and then 40 ms has not "averaged 21 ms"; it has missed a deadline and dropped a frame, and no amount of subsequent speed repays that. Late is wrong. This is why engine programmers talk in frame times and percentiles, and are suspicious of anyone quoting average FPS.

The ninety-percent picture

Formally, this is a soft real-time system: missing a deadline degrades quality rather than causing catastrophe. A pacemaker or an anti-lock brake is hard real-time — a missed deadline is a failure, full stop. We get to drop a frame and survive. That distinction lets us make tradeoffs a hard real-time engineer cannot, and we will use that freedom deliberately. The missing ten percent — scheduling theory, deadline analysis — we never need; if you are curious, the term to search is "rate-monotonic scheduling".

3The Theory

3.1 The frame budget, derived

So there is a deadline. What is it? This is the one piece of arithmetic in the lesson, and it is worth doing slowly, because the number you get is the number the rest of the course is organised around.

Your display refreshes at some rate — call it f, measured in hertz, meaning "times per second". A 60 Hz monitor puts up a new image sixty times every second. If sixty images take one second, then the time available for one image is one sixtieth of a second. That is all the derivation is: a rate inverted is a period.

\[ T_{\text{frame}} = \frac{1}{f} \text{ seconds} = \frac{1000}{f} \text{ milliseconds} \] frame_budget_ms = 1000 / refresh_rate_hz

In words, so this survives a dead CDN and a bad memory: the frame budget in milliseconds is one thousand divided by the refresh rate in hertz. The factor of 1000 is only unit conversion — seconds to milliseconds — because milliseconds are the unit an engine programmer thinks in.

Worked example — the number that runs your life

For a 60 Hz display:

  • T = 1000 / 60 = 16.666… ms

So 16.67 milliseconds. Not 16 — and that rounding is not a nitpick. The difference between 16.67 and 16 is 0.67 ms, which is 0.67 / 16.67 = 4.0% of your entire budget. Four percent is an enormous amount of engine performance to throw away by being sloppy with a division.

Push a few more rates through the same formula:

Refresh rateArithmeticBudgetWhere you meet it
30 Hz1000 / 3033.33 msConsole cinematics; a fallback target
60 Hz1000 / 6016.67 msThe default assumption. Our target.
90 Hz1000 / 9011.11 msVR's floor — below it, people feel ill
120 Hz1000 / 1208.33 msModern phones; high-refresh displays
144 Hz1000 / 1446.94 msPC gaming monitors

Look at the right-hand column as a whole. Moving from 60 Hz to 144 Hz does not ask you to be "a bit faster" — it cuts your entire budget to 6.94 / 16.67 = 42% of what it was. Everything you do must fit in under half the time.

Now here is the part that turns a number into an architecture. That 16.67 ms is not 16.67 ms for your game code. It is 16.67 ms for everything: reading input, stepping physics, animating skeletons, culling the scene, recording draw calls, mixing audio, running your gameplay logic, and handing a finished image to the display — plus whatever the operating system decides to do with the CPU while you are not looking.

The thesis of this entire course

Nearly every strange thing you will ever see in an engine's architecture is a consequence of 16.67 ms. Not tradition, not taste — that number.

Every one of those is an answer to "how do we finish in 16.67 ms, every single time?" You do not have to memorise this table. You have to believe the number is load-bearing — and by Module 5 you will have measured it yourself.

3.2 The vsync cliff

Here is the payoff to the thought I asked you to hold. What actually happens if a frame takes 17 ms instead of 16.67?

The intuitive answer — you get 1000/17 ≈ 58.8 FPS, a 2% loss, who cares — is wrong, and the way it is wrong is the single most important performance fact in this lesson.

The display does not accept an image whenever you happen to be ready. It refreshes on its own fixed schedule: at 0 ms, 16.67 ms, 33.33 ms, 50 ms, and so on forever. With vsync on (and it usually is, because without it you get tearing), your finished frame can only be shown at one of those instants. Miss one and you do not lose the overshoot — you lose the whole remaining period, waiting for the next one. Figure 2 draws the cliff.

Worked example — the arithmetic of the cliff

Vsync ticks at 60 Hz land every 16.67 ms: at 0, 16.67, 33.33, 50.00, …

Case A — a 16.0 ms frame.

  • Work finishes at 16.0 ms. The next tick is at 16.67 ms.
  • 16.0 ≤ 16.67, so we make it. We idle 0.67 ms and are displayed at 16.67.
  • One frame per period → 60 FPS.

Case B — a 17.0 ms frame.

  • Work finishes at 17.0 ms. The 16.67 tick has already gone — missed by 0.33 ms.
  • The next tick is 33.33 ms. We wait: 33.33 − 17.0 = 16.33 ms of pure stall.
  • One frame every two periods → 1000 / 33.33 = 30 FPS.

Being 0.33 ms — 2% — over budget cost us half the framerate. And note the cruelty: the 17 ms frame did 6% more work than the 16 ms one but delivered half as many frames. Work does not convert to framerate linearly. It falls off a cliff.

The general rule, which is what the widget below implements:

  • periods_needed = ceil(work / budget)
  • effective_fps = refresh_rate / periods_needed

Check it: ceil(17.0 / 16.67) = ceil(1.02) = 2, so 60 / 2 = 30 FPS ✓. And ceil(16.0 / 16.67) = ceil(0.96) = 1, so 60 / 1 = 60 FPS ✓. That ceil is the cliff.

This is why an engine programmer's instinct — "find the millisecond" — is not obsessive-compulsive. Near the budget edge, one millisecond is worth thirty frames per second. Away from the edge it is worth nothing at all. Performance work in a real-time system is not "make everything faster"; it is get under the line, then stop.

3.3 The anatomy, derived from the loop

Now we can do the anatomy tour properly — not as an inventory, but as a derivation. We are going to ask what the loop must do, and let the subsystems fall out. That way you are not memorising someone's folder structure; you are re-deriving why it has to look roughly like that.

Once per frame, the engine must answer six questions in order:

  1. "What happened?" The player pressed a key; the window resized; the OS wants attention. Something must talk to the operating system and turn its events into facts our code can use. → platform + input
  2. "What is true now?" The world moved on. Bodies fell, animations advanced, gameplay rules fired. → simulation
  3. "What can be seen?" Of the ten thousand things in the world, perhaps two hundred are in front of the camera. Finding them is cheaper than drawing them. → scene + culling
  4. "What does it look like?" Those two hundred things become triangles, become pixels. → renderer
  5. "Where did the data come from?" None of the above works without meshes, textures, and sounds in memory. Disk is a million times slower than RAM, so this cannot be done naively mid-frame. → assets
  6. "Why is it broken?" It will be broken. Constantly. You need to see inside. → tooling

That is the whole anatomy, and every subsystem in Figure 3 is an answer to one of those six questions. There is nothing else in an engine — only refinements of these.

3.4 Why the boundaries are where they are

Figure 3 draws lines between boxes. Where do those lines come from? This is the question that separates someone who can use an engine from someone who can design one, and the answer is pleasingly simple:

The rule

A boundary belongs wherever a rate changes or an owner changes. Not where the vocabulary changes, and not where a textbook's chapter heading changes.

Test the rule against every line in Figure 3:

BoundaryWhat changes across itConsequence
simulation ↔ renderer Rate. Simulation wants a fixed step for determinism; rendering wants to go as fast as the display allows. They cannot share a clock. This forces the fixed-timestep loop, and the interpolation that hides the mismatch. → 1.4
assets ↔ everything Rate, brutally. A frame is ~16 ms; a disk read can be tens of milliseconds — and a texture decode longer. Loading can never be a blocking call inside a frame. Hence handles, async loading, and placeholder assets. → 5.4, 5.5
platform ↔ core Owner. Below the line the OS decides; above it, we do. Everything OS-specific gets quarantined in one place, so porting touches one folder. This is why SDL exists.
tooling ↔ runtime Owner and lifetime. Tools exist for the developer, at development time. The editor may depend on the engine; the engine may never depend on the editor — or you ship your tools to players. → 5.10, 8.7
scene ↔ renderer Owner. The scene owns what exists; the renderer owns how to draw. The most contested line in engine design — see below. Ours is drawn in 5.9 and 6.13.

Notice what is not a reason for a boundary: "these are different topics." Physics and animation are different topics, yet they sit in the same box in Figure 3 — because they run at the same rate and both mutate the same world state. A line between them would buy nothing and cost a synchronisation point.

Honest: the boundaries are argued about, constantly

I have presented Figure 3 with a confidence it does not fully deserve. Real engine teams fight over these lines, and the fights are not stupid — they are genuine tradeoffs:

  • Is animation simulation or rendering? It looks like rendering (it is visual, and skinning happens on the GPU). It behaves like simulation (it is stateful and time-stepped). We put it in simulation, and we will find the seam in Module 7.6.
  • Is culling scene or renderer? Both claim it. It needs the scene's spatial data and the renderer's camera. Many engines end up with a "render scene" layer that exists only to settle this argument.
  • Is audio simulation? It is time-stepped and spatial, so yes — but it runs on its own thread at its own rate, so also no. Rate change ⇒ boundary; our own rule pulls it out. → 7.13

When you meet an engine that draws these differently, it is usually not wrong. It has different pressures. What you should demand — of them, and of yourself — is a reason.

3.5 How real engines draw these lines

The anatomy is universal; the packaging is not. It is worth knowing the landscape, because you will read these codebases — and by the end of this course you will read them fluently.

EngineOrganising ideaWhat it optimises forThe tradeoff it accepts

Do not read that table as a ranking. Read the last column. Every engine is a set of tradeoffs made by people who knew things you do not yet know about pressures you have not yet felt. Unity's object model was not a mistake — it was the right call for 2005's problem, and it stopped being right when scale changed. Architecture is a function of its constraints, which is precisely why we started with the constraint.

Doom 3 is worth your time, genuinely

Fabien Sanglard's code reviews of the id Tech engines are the closest thing our field has to literary criticism, and Doom 3's source is often called the most readable AAA engine code ever released. You do not have to understand it yet. Skim it in Module 5 after the refactor, and again at the end. The distance you have travelled will be legible in how much of it makes sense. Links in §10.

Nothing happens. 7.3, 7.8, 8.2 — still 60 FPS, still green. Then you hit 8.4, the total reaches 16.70 ms, and the readout does not sag to 59 FPS. It falls to 30. One notch. Half your framerate. That is the cliff, and it is worth feeling once with your hands rather than only reading about.

Three things are worth noticing while you play, and each one is a lesson you would otherwise have to learn the hard way:

  • The output is quantized. Effective FPS jumps 60 → 30 → 20 → 15. There is no 58, no 47. You are always at refresh / n for a whole number n.
  • Headroom is worth nothing until it is worth everything. Shaving 2 ms off a frame that already fits buys you exactly zero frames per second. Shaving 0.4 ms off a frame that just misses doubles your framerate. This is why we measure before optimizing (3.10) — untargeted optimization is usually wasted effort.
  • Switch to 144 Hz without touching the sliders. The same engine that was comfortable is now missing by a mile. Your architecture is not "fast" or "slow" in the abstract — it is fast enough for a target, and the target is a design input.

6Build & Run

Nothing to build. But there is something to run, and it is more useful than it looks: find out what your own display actually does. Every budget number in this course assumes 60 Hz, and if your monitor is 120 Hz or your laptop throttles to 48 Hz on battery, your real budget is not the one in the table.

Windows: Settings → System → Display → Advanced display. Read "Refresh rate".

macOS: System Settings → Displays. The refresh rate sits under the resolution list; ProMotion displays report a range (e.g. 47–120 Hz), which is its own fascinating problem.

Expected result

You should finish this lesson able to state, out loud, without looking anything up:

Checkpoint — say these four things
  1. My display runs at ___ Hz, so my frame budget is ___ ms. (1000 ÷ refresh rate)
  2. If I exceed that budget by 0.1 ms with vsync on, my framerate halves — because a missed vsync costs the whole remaining period, not the overshoot.
  3. The six subsystems are platform/input, simulation, scene/culling, renderer, assets, tooling — and I can say which question each answers.
  4. A boundary belongs where a rate or an owner changes.

If any of those four is shaky, the section to reread is linked from the contents above. Number 4 is the one that will still be paying you back in Module 8.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

No code yet, so these are pitfalls of thinking — which are the expensive kind, because they take months to show up as symptoms.

Quoting average FPS — "we're getting 90 FPS!" but it feels awful
Symptom
The counter says 90 FPS. The game visibly hitches. Both are true.
Cause
FPS is a rate, and averaging a rate hides exactly what you need to see. Nine frames at 8 ms and one at 88 ms average to 16 ms — a reassuring 62 FPS — while the player watched an 88 ms freeze, over five frames long. Worse, FPS is reciprocal, so equal FPS gaps are wildly unequal amounts of work: dropping 60 → 30 FPS costs 33.33 − 16.67 = 16.67 ms, while dropping 300 → 270 FPS — also a 30 FPS loss — costs only 3.70 − 3.33 = 0.37 ms. Same headline number, 45× less work.
Fix
Think in frame times, in milliseconds, and quote the 99th percentile — the worst 1 in 100 frames. That is the number the player feels. Our profiling in 3.10 and 8.3 measures times, never averages of rates.
"I'll optimize later" — the rewrite that eats a year
Symptom
The engine works beautifully with 100 objects. At 10,000 it dies, and no profiler hotspot explains it — the time is smeared evenly across everything.
Cause
Smeared cost is the signature of an architectural problem, not a hot loop. Usually it is memory layout: objects scattered across the heap, every access a cache miss. You cannot optimize your way out of that, because there is nothing local to fix — the layout is the problem. This is the wall Unity hit, and DOTS is what it cost them.
Fix
Distinguish the two: micro-optimization genuinely can wait — Knuth was right about that. Data layout cannot, because it is load-bearing for everything above it. That is why Module 5 does data-oriented design before Module 6 builds the fancy renderer on top. The order is not an accident.
"It's only one millisecond" — a 2% regression halves the framerate
Symptom
A small, obviously harmless feature lands. Framerate drops from 60 to 30. The feature costs 0.4 ms.
Cause
You were at 16.4 ms — under budget, but with only 0.27 ms of headroom. The vsync cliff of §3.2 did the rest. The feature is not the bug; the missing headroom is.
Fix
Budget for headroom deliberately — many teams target ~80% of the frame (≈13 ms at 60 Hz) precisely so a 0.4 ms feature is survivable. And track frame time on every build: a regression is cheap to find the day it lands and brutal to find a month later.
Treating the subsystem list as canonical — cargo-culted architecture
Symptom
Six folders matching Figure 3, created on day one, most of them empty for months — and a SceneManager that manages nothing.
Cause
Mistaking a map for a plan. Figure 3 describes forces that exist in every engine; it does not prescribe your directory tree on day one. Structure created before the pressure that justifies it is structure you will have to unlearn.
Fix
Let the boundaries emerge where the rule says they must — at rate and owner changes — and not before. This is exactly why this course keeps a single executable through Module 4 and only splits into engine/ + demos/ in 5.1, once the seam is real and you can feel it.

8Exercises

0.1.1Your actual budgetwarm-up

Find your display's refresh rate (§6) and compute your real frame budget. Then compute the budget for a 165 Hz monitor and for a 47 Hz ProMotion low-power mode. Which is the harsher constraint, and by how much?

Solution

1000 / 165 = 6.06 ms and 1000 / 47 = 21.28 ms. The 165 Hz display is harsher by a factor of 21.28 / 6.06 ≈ 3.5× — the same engine has three and a half times less time per frame. Worth internalising: a "high refresh rate" feature is not a rendering setting, it is a wholly different performance target that your architecture either meets or does not.

0.1.2Find the cliff by handguided

An engine spends 7.2 ms rendering, 3.1 ms on physics, 2.4 ms on animation, 1.0 ms on audio, and 1.8 ms on gameplay, targeting 60 Hz. Using periods = ceil(work / budget) and effective_fps = refresh / periods:

  1. What is the total, and does it fit?
  2. What effective FPS does the player get?
  3. A new feature adds 1.5 ms. Now what?
  4. Starting from (3), what is the smallest saving that restores 60 FPS?
Hint

Do part 4 by asking what total the ceil needs in order to come back down to 1. Then subtract. The answer is smaller than the feature you added — which is the whole point of the exercise.

Solution

1. 7.2 + 3.1 + 2.4 + 1.0 + 1.8 = 15.5 ms. Budget is 16.67 ms, so it fits — with 1.17 ms of headroom.

2. ceil(15.5 / 16.67) = ceil(0.93) = 1 → 60 / 1 = 60 FPS. Comfortable.

3. 15.5 + 1.5 = 17.0 ms. Now ceil(17.0 / 16.67) = ceil(1.02) = 2 → 60 / 2 = 30 FPS. A 1.5 ms feature — 9% more work — cost half the framerate.

4. We need total ≤ 16.67, so we must save 17.0 − 16.67 = 0.33 ms. One third of a millisecond — 2% of the frame — takes you from 30 FPS back to 60.

Sit with the asymmetry in (3) and (4). Adding 1.5 ms cost 30 FPS; removing 0.33 ms buys all 30 back. Near the cliff edge, the exchange rate between milliseconds and frames is insane. Far from it, milliseconds are worthless. Knowing which regime you are in is most of performance work — and it is why 3.10 teaches measurement before technique.

0.1.3Read a real engine's treeopen

Open the Godot or Doom 3 source tree in your browser (links in §10) and look only at the top-level directory names — do not open a single file yet. For each directory, predict which of Figure 3's six questions it answers. Then open two of them and check.

Solution sketch

Godot's servers/ is the interesting one, because it will not map cleanly — it holds the rendering and physics servers, an architecture where subsystems are accessed through a command interface rather than called directly. Ask yourself which boundary rule that reflects. (It is a rate/owner split: the servers can run on their own threads, so a hard interface is required. Our rule predicted it.)

In Doom 3, idlib/ is the "core" row of Figure 3, renderer/ is unmistakable, and game/ is the passenger from Figure 1's right-hand panel — it even compiles to a separate DLL, which is the boundary made physical. That is the same move we make in 5.1.

If a directory does not map onto Figure 3, that is the interesting case, not a failure of the map. Ask what pressure produced it.

0.1.4Defend a boundaryopen

Someone proposes merging the renderer and the scene into one World class: "they always change together anyway, and passing data between them is pure overhead." Write the strongest possible case for the merge, then the strongest case against. Decide — and name the specific evidence that would change your mind.

Solution sketch

For: it is genuinely not a strawman. Fewer indirections, no synchronisation, no duplicated transform data, and culling stops being a political argument. Small engines do this and ship. "They change together" is a real signal — it is the classic argument that a boundary is imaginary.

Against: apply the rule. Does a rate change? Yes — the scene is mutated by the fixed-timestep simulation while the renderer runs at display rate. That alone forces a boundary, because they cannot share a clock without one corrupting the other's view of the world. Does an owner change? Yes — a headless server needs the scene and no renderer at all, which is impossible if they are one class.

Verdict: keep them separate — but for the rate reason, not the aesthetic one. The evidence that would change my mind: if the engine were single-threaded, render-only, and locked to the simulation rate, the rate difference evaporates and the merge becomes defensible. Constraints decide, not taste.

This is what a design interview actually sounds like. "It's best practice" is not an answer; "here is the constraint, and here is what would change my mind" is.

9Recap & Next

What you can do now that you could not an hour ago

  • Define an engine without an inventory. It is a system that turns a fixed budget of time into frames, forever, on a deadline. The parts list is a consequence, not the definition.
  • Compute a frame budget — 1000 / refresh — and explain the vsync cliff: 2% over costs 50% of the framerate, because performance is quantized.
  • Derive the anatomy from the six questions a frame must answer, instead of memorising six folder names.
  • Place a boundary with a reason: where a rate changes, or an owner changes. Nowhere else.

The one sentence worth carrying into every remaining lesson: everything strange about engine architecture is an answer to "how do we finish in 16.67 ms, every single time?" When Module 5 asks you to give up pointers for handles, or Module 8 asks you to write an allocator, that is the question being answered. You will not have to take it on faith — you will have measured it.

10Further Reading

None of this is required. All of it is good.

  • Book Jason Gregory, Game Engine Architecture — chapters 1 and 14. The definitive survey, written from inside Naughty Dog. Chapter 1 is the long version of this lesson; if you buy one book for this course, buy this one.
  • Source id-Software/DOOM-3-BFG — for exercise 0.1.3. Look at the top-level directories only, for now.
  • Essay Fabien Sanglard, "Doom 3 Source Code Review" — the closest our field has to literary criticism. Also his Doom, Quake, and Wolfenstein "black books". Read for pleasure; understanding will come.
  • Source godotengine/godot — a modern, readable, complete engine you can actually browse. Note servers/.
  • Talk Mike Acton, "Data-Oriented Design and C++" (CppCon 2014) — abrasive, correct, and the clearest statement of why the frame budget dictates architecture. We earn the right to disagree with parts of it in Module 5.6.
  • Post Glenn Fiedler, "Fix Your Timestep!" — the canonical treatment of the loop. Do not read it yet; we derive it from scratch in 1.4 and it will mean more afterwards.
  • Reference SDL3 Wiki — bookmark it now. From 0.5 onward it is the most-visited tab in your browser, and 0.6 teaches you to read it properly.
