Module 2 — Software Rasterizer I · Lesson 2.4

Interpolating Attributes Across a Triangle

Lesson 2.3 built three weights and hung nothing on them. Today we hang everything on them. The formula is one line and you could guess it — which is exactly the danger, because three separate things have to go right and two of them are silent when they go wrong. One is the fill rule's -1, still sitting in the accumulators where it does not belong. One is that a colour is a quantity of light rather than a number, so averaging the stored values averages the wrong thing. The third is that reorienting a triangle moves its corners, and whatever those corners were carrying has to move with them.

1The Problem

We have three weights per pixel and we are throwing them away. fill_triangle computes them — that is what the three edge-function accumulators are, up to a division — checks their signs, writes one flat colour, and moves on. Lesson 2.3 added barycentric_at so we could look at the weights; nothing in the engine acts on them.

That is the gap, and it is a wide one. Every surface worth rendering varies across itself. A lit sphere is bright on one side and dark on the other. A brick wall shows a different texel at every pixel. A depth-tested scene needs each pixel to know how far away it is. Not one of those is possible while a triangle can only be one colour, and all of them are the same operation: something is known at the corners, and every interior pixel needs its share.

So the formula is obvious, and you could write it right now:

\[ a(P) = w_0 a_0 + w_1 a_1 + w_2 a_2 \] a(P) = w0*a0 + w1*a1 + w2*a2

Take the value at each corner, weight it by that corner's barycentric coordinate, add them up. That is the whole of attribute interpolation and it will not get more complicated for the rest of Module 2.

Which is precisely why this lesson is not short. The formula is easy and the context is where the bodies are buried. Three things have to be right, and only the first of them announces itself when it is wrong:

  1. The weights must belong to the right corners. Lesson 2.3 §3.5 dealt with this — but 2.3 only ever looked at weights, and a rotated pairing looked plausible. Now the pairing decides which corner is red, and a new way to break it appears: reorienting a backwards triangle moves the vertices, and the colours have to come along.
  2. The weights must be unbiased. Our accumulators carry the top-left rule's -1. That -1 is a statement about who owns a boundary pixel. It is not a statement about where the pixel is, and interpolating with it displaces the entire attribute field by a fraction of a pixel — a fraction you will not see, until you do.
  3. Colour is not a number. Averaging two stored channel values does not average the light they stand for, and Lesson 1.6 measured exactly how badly. That lesson's argument was made with a pair of ramps drawn side by side; it has been waiting for a real use ever since, and this is it.

Here is the third one, since we are supposed to show the artifact before explaining it. Take a triangle with a pure red corner, a pure green corner and a pure blue corner — the most ordinary test triangle in graphics — and interpolate the stored channel values the way the formula above literally says. The centre pixel comes out at (85, 85, 85). Do the same interpolation on the light those colours stand for and the centre comes out at (156, 156, 156).

The artifact, in one number

Both of those claim to be "one third red, one third green, one third blue". The first emits 27% as much light as the second — measured, in §3.4. On screen it is the difference between a clean neutral grey and a dark, muddy band through the middle of every gradient in your renderer.

You have seen this artifact. It is in a great deal of shipped software, and it is one line of code away in ours.

By the end of the lesson the demo will draw that triangle both ways with M switching between them, and print the centre pixel so the two numbers are on screen rather than in a paragraph.

2Building Intuition

Three surveyors and a field

Forget pixels for a moment. Three surveyors stand at the corners of a triangular field. Each reports the altitude where they are standing: 10 metres, 40 metres, 70 metres. Somebody asks what the altitude is at a particular spot in the middle of the field.

You cannot answer that from three numbers alone — the real ground could do anything between the corners. But you can answer the question if the field is flat, meaning flat in the sense of a tilted plane rather than a horizontal one. Three points determine a plane, three altitudes determine that plane's tilt, and then the altitude everywhere is fixed. There is nothing left to choose.

That is the picture to keep, and Figure 1 draws it. The triangle is the field seen from above; the surface floating over it is the plane through the three reported altitudes; the height of that surface over any interior point is the interpolated value. What we are going to build is a machine for evaluating that plane's height, and the barycentric weights are how it does it.

Two things to notice before we go near algebra. First, the surface has no bumps: it is a plane, so it has one slope and that slope is the same everywhere. That is the meaning of the word affine, and it will matter in §3.1 because it is what makes the answer unique. Second, the plane extends past the triangle's edges. Ask for the altitude somewhere outside the field and the machine will happily answer, using weights that have gone negative — which is exactly what Lesson 2.3 §3.6 found, arriving from the other direction.

The weights do not know what they are mixing

Now the second intuition, and it is the one that makes this lesson's machinery worth building once rather than six times.

The weights sum to 1, and inside the triangle none of them is negative. A set of non-negative numbers summing to 1 is a recipe: 40% of this, 30% of that, 30% of the other. A recipe does not need to know what is in the jars. Feed it three altitudes and it produces an altitude. Feed it three colours and it produces a colour. Feed it three depths and it produces a depth. The arithmetic is identical; only the labels change.

Figure 2 makes the point by running one fixed recipe over three different payloads. The left column — the weights — never changes. Everything to the right of it does, and the machine in the middle cannot tell.

Where the intuition lies to you

Here is where I want to slow you down, because the recipe analogy is about to mislead you and it is going to feel completely reasonable while it does.

Mixing paint and mixing light are different operations, and the number stored in a pixel is neither. Suppose you have a red vertex and a green vertex, and you want the colour halfway along the edge between them. "Half of each" seems unambiguous. Red is stored as 255 in the red channel; green is stored as 255 in the green channel. Average the stored numbers and you get 128 in each — a dark olive.

But look at what you asked for. You asked for a point emitting half of red's light plus half of green's light. Red's channel at full strength emits some amount of light; half of it is half that amount. And Lesson 1.6 measured what stored value emits half of full strength: it is 188, not 128. The honest midpoint is (188, 188, 0) — a bright yellow-olive, distinctly lighter than the one the naive average gives.

The reason is the transfer curve from Lesson 1.6, and Figure 3 in the next section draws exactly this. But the intuition to fix now, before any curve, is the category error:

The one-sentence version

The number in a pixel is not how much light; it is a code for how much light, chosen so the 256 available codes land where the eye can tell them apart. Averaging codes is not averaging light, any more than averaging two postcodes gives you a place halfway between two towns.

Which raises the obvious objection, and it is a good one: who says I want to average light? If you are an artist building a UI gradient, "halfway between these two swatches" may well mean halfway in the stored values, because that is what your eye and your colour picker were doing. That is a legitimate thing to want. It is just not what a renderer wants, because a renderer is modelling photons arriving at a camera, and photons add. We will make the rule explicit in §3.3 and then never revisit it.

3The Theory

The formula, and why it is forced

The formula was in §1 and you had probably already guessed it. What is worth having is the argument that it is not a guess — that this is the only answer, so there is nothing to tune and no better scheme to go looking for later.

Start with what we are asking for. We want a function a(x, y) defined over the triangle that (i) takes the value a₀ at v₀, a₁ at v₁ and a₂ at v₂, and (ii) is affine — a tilted plane, no bumps, constant slope. An affine function of two variables has exactly three coefficients:

\[ a(x, y) = \alpha x + \beta y + \gamma \] a(x, y) = alpha*x + beta*y + gamma

Three unknowns. Three conditions, one per corner. Provided the three corners are not collinear — which is exactly the condition that the triangle has non-zero area, the same condition fill_triangle already checks — that system has exactly one solution. So there is one affine function through three corner values, and any correct method must find it.

Now the trick, which is that we already have it. Lesson 2.3 established two facts about the weight functions w₀(x, y), w₁, w₂:

  • each one is affine in the pixel position — it is an edge function, which is affine, divided by a constant (2.3 §3.1, and 2.2 §3.5 is where we exploited it to make the fill incremental);
  • each one is 1 at its own vertex and 0 at the other two (2.3 §3.6).

Put those together. The combination w₀a₀ + w₁a₁ + w₂a₂ is a sum of affine functions scaled by constants, so it is affine. And at v₀ the weights are (1, 0, 0), so it evaluates to a₀; likewise at the other two corners. It satisfies both requirements, and we just argued only one function does.

What that argument bought

Barycentric interpolation is not "a reasonable way to blend three corner values". It is the affine function through them, and any other scheme claiming to interpolate a triangle affinely is either this one in disguise or wrong. If you ever see a rasterizer doing something that looks different, it is either not affine — perspective correction, §3.2's caveat, is exactly that — or it is this with the algebra rearranged.

Worked example — the surveyors, with the same triangle as Lessons 2.2 and 2.3

v₀ = (2, 2), v₁ = (10, 4), v₂ = (4, 10), and the point P = (5, 5). Lesson 2.3 §3.4 computed the weights there: w = (0.4, 0.3, 0.3).

Attach the surveyors' altitudes — 10, 40 and 70 — and turn the handle:

\[ a(P) = 0.4(10) + 0.3(40) + 0.3(70) = 4 + 12 + 21 = 37 \] a(P) = 0.4*10 + 0.3*40 + 0.3*70 = 4 + 12 + 21 = 37

That is the number in Figure 1, and it is the number the harness asserts. Notice it lands between 10 and 70 but nowhere near the plain average of 40 — the point is closer to v₀, so v₀'s low altitude pulls harder.

The check that proves the machinery, not just the arithmetic. Pick an attribute that is itself a known affine function of position — say f(x, y) = 3x − 2y + 7 — evaluate it at the three corners, feed those three numbers through the interpolator, and compare against evaluating f directly at the interior point. If interpolation really is exact for affine functions, these must agree everywhere. Measured over 3,721 points spread well beyond the triangle: worst disagreement 3.05 × 10⁻⁵, on values ranging up to about 120 — that is float rounding and nothing else.

Anything you can average

Because the derivation never mentioned what a was, it holds for anything you can multiply by a scalar and add. In practice, in this course:

AttributeInterpolated asArrives inWatch out for
Colour three scalars, in linear light this lesson averaging stored values instead of light (§3.3)
Depth one scalar Lesson 3.1 (the z-buffer) precision — comparisons are against tiny differences (§3.8)
Texture coordinate two scalars, u and v Lesson 3.7 screen-space affine is wrong under perspective (below)
Surface normal three scalars Lesson 3.6 the result is not unit length; renormalise per pixel

The normal case is worth a sentence now, because it is the clearest example of the machinery handing you something plausible and wrong. Interpolate two unit vectors and the result is generally shorter than 1 — the straight line between two points on a circle passes inside the circle. Nothing in the rasterizer notices, because the rasterizer does not know it is carrying a direction. Lighting computed with a normal of length 0.94 is simply 6% too dark, uniformly, in the middle of every triangle. Lesson 3.6 renormalises; today it is just a warning that "correct interpolation" and "correct result" are different claims.

Where this all stops being true — and it is not far off

Everything in this lesson interpolates affinely in screen space: equal steps across the screen produce equal steps in the attribute. For the flat 2-D triangles we are drawing today that is exactly right.

The moment a triangle is tilted away from the camera in 3-D, it stops being right. Equal steps across the screen no longer correspond to equal steps across the surface: the far end of a receding triangle is compressed, so more surface is packed into each pixel there. Interpolate a texture coordinate affinely on screen and the texture will visibly swim and bend along the triangle's diagonal — the artifact that gives first-generation PlayStation games their distinctive wobbling floors.

This is the ninety-percent picture; the missing ten percent is the perspective divide, and Lesson 3.2 shows the artifact first and then fixes it with the 1/w trick. It cannot bite us before then, because nothing we draw has depth yet.

A colour is a quantity of light

Now the part that turns Lesson 1.6 from a curiosity into a rule.

Recall the shape of the sRGB transfer function. A stored channel value c in [0, 1] corresponds to a quantity of light L given by a curve that is steep near black and shallow near white — most of the 256 codes are spent on dark values, because that is where the eye can distinguish them. The curve is not a decoration; it is what makes 8 bits per channel sufficient at all.

The consequence for interpolation is entirely mechanical. Interpolating on the horizontal axis of that curve and interpolating on the vertical axis are different operations, and Figure 3 shows just how different.

So the rule, stated once and then obeyed everywhere:

The rule

Decode to linear light at the edges of a calculation, do all arithmetic there, encode once on the way out. Interpolating, adding several lights together, multiplying by a material's reflectance — all of it happens in linear light. Storage and display happen in sRGB. The conversion belongs at the boundary, never in the middle.

That rule is why this lesson adds a linear_rgb type rather than doing the conversions ad hoc. A type whose name says what its numbers mean is the cheapest possible defence against mixing units, and mixing these units is the single most common rendering bug there is. Module 6 takes the same idea much further, when the values stop being confined to [0, 1] and the whole pipeline goes HDR.

And the honest exception

If your three "vertex colours" are not lights but an artist's chosen swatches for a UI gradient, the artist may genuinely have meant the encoded blend — they picked those colours in a tool that showed them stored values. That is a real and defensible use.

Our rule stands because our vertex colours will very shortly become lighting results (Lesson 3.6 computes them from a light and a normal). A quantity of light gets averaged as light. When in doubt, ask what the number is a measurement of.

The numbers, worked

Time to push real values through. Take the standard test triangle: v₀ pure red (255, 0, 0), v₁ pure green (0, 255, 0), v₂ pure blue (0, 0, 255). All three primaries are at either full or zero, which is what makes this triangle such a sharp test — it exercises the extreme ends of the transfer curve in every channel at once.

Worked example — the centre of the triangle

At the centroid the weights are (⅓, ⅓, ⅓).

The naive path — average the stored values. Each channel gets one third of 255 and two thirds of 0:

\[ r = \tfrac{1}{3}(255) + \tfrac{1}{3}(0) + \tfrac{1}{3}(0) = 85 \] r = (1/3)*255 + (1/3)*0 + (1/3)*0 = 85

and the same for green and blue, giving (85, 85, 85).

The correct path — average the light. Decode first. Stored 255 is light 1.0; stored 0 is light 0.0. So each channel's light is

\[ L = \tfrac{1}{3}(1.0) + \tfrac{1}{3}(0) + \tfrac{1}{3}(0) = 0.3333 \] L = (1/3)*1.0 + (1/3)*0 + (1/3)*0 = 0.3333

and now encode 0.3333 back to a stored value using the transfer function from Lesson 1.6:

\[ 1.055 \times 0.3333^{1/2.4} - 0.055 = 0.6125 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 0.6125 \times 255 = 156.2 \] 1.055 * 0.3333^(1/2.4) - 0.055 = 0.6125, and 0.6125 * 255 = 156.2

giving (156, 156, 156).

85 against 156. Both are legitimate greys; only one of them is a third of white's light. Decode them both and the naive answer carries 0.0908 of white's light where the correct one carries 0.3325 — a factor of 3.66. The naive blend delivers about 27% of the light it claims to.

Worked example — the same point as Lessons 2.2 and 2.3

At w = (0.4, 0.3, 0.3), with the same red/green/blue corners, the light in each channel is just the weight of the corner that owns it: (0.4, 0.3, 0.3). Encoding each:

  • 0.4 → 1.055 × 0.41/2.4 − 0.055 = 0.6652 → 170
  • 0.3 → 1.055 × 0.31/2.4 − 0.055 = 0.5838 → 149

so the correct pixel is (170, 149, 149). The naive path gives 0.4 × 255 = 102 and 0.3 × 255 = 77, i.e. (102, 77, 77) — the same hue, drastically darker.

And on the red-to-green edge, at the midpoint, the two paths give (188, 188, 0) and (128, 128, 0). Those are precisely the numbers Lesson 1.6 §3.4 produced with its two ramps. Same arithmetic, arriving through a triangle instead of a gradient bar — which is how you know 1.6 was not a special case.

All four of those figures are computed by the verification harness rather than by me with a calculator, and the demo reads its centre pixel back out of the framebuffer and prints it, so the number on screen is what was actually drawn.

The bias has to come out

Now the silent one. This is the part of the lesson you would not have thought to check.

Lesson 2.2 §3.6 folded the top-left rule into the edge accumulators as an integer bias: 0 for a top-or-left edge, -1 for the others, added once into the starting value so that the per-pixel test stays a single comparison against zero. That was a good trick and it is still a good trick. But the values in those accumulators are now doing a second job — they are about to become weights — and the bias has no business being in them.

Precisely: the accumulator holds E(x, y) + bias, where E is the true edge function. Divide that by the area and you get wᵢ + biasᵢ / 2A. Two things break.

First, the weights stop summing to 1. Lesson 2.3 §3.2 relied on e₀ + e₁ + e₂ = 2A exactly, in integers, everywhere in the plane. With biases in the way:

\[ w_0 + w_1 + w_2 = 1 + \frac{b_0 + b_1 + b_2}{2A} \] w0 + w1 + w2 = 1 + (b0 + b1 + b2) / (2A)

So how wrong is that? It depends on how many of the three biases are -1, and there is a tidy argument that pins it down. Walk the triangle's three directed edges and add up their dy: you return to where you started, so the total is zero. The three dy values cannot all be zero (that would be a degenerate triangle), so at least one is negative and at least one is positive. A negative dy is a left edge — bias 0. A positive dy is neither top nor left — bias -1.

Therefore

Exactly one or two of the three biases is -1, never zero of them and never all three. Checked by brute force over every non-degenerate triangle with vertices in a 9×9 grid — 495,648 of them: 247,824 had bias sum -1, 247,824 had -2, and none had anything else.

Second, and worse, the whole attribute field slides. Adding a constant to an affine function does not tilt it; it translates it. So every weight is displaced by the same amount, which means the interpolated attribute at a pixel is the value that should belong to a slightly different point.

How far? A weight changes by 1/2A. The rate at which w₀ changes with position is the magnitude of its gradient, and from 2.2 §3.5's per-pixel steps we know the gradient exactly — the steps are the gradient, scaled:

\[ \left\| \nabla w_0 \right\| = \frac{\sqrt{(y_1-y_2)^2 + (x_2-x_1)^2}}{2A} = \frac{\left\| \mathbf{e} \right\|}{2A} \] |grad w0| = sqrt((y1-y2)^2 + (x2-x1)^2) / (2A) = |e| / (2A)

where ‖e‖ is the length of the edge opposite v₀. Divide the change by the rate of change and the 2A cancels:

\[ \text{displacement} = \frac{1/2A}{\left\| \mathbf{e} \right\| / 2A} = \frac{1}{\left\| \mathbf{e} \right\|} \ \text{pixels} \] displacement = (1/2A) / (|e|/2A) = 1 / |e| pixels
A result worth remembering

The attribute field is displaced by one over the opposite edge's length, in pixels, perpendicular to that edge. The area cancels out entirely. A triangle with a 100-pixel edge is off by 1/100 of a pixel; a triangle with a 4-pixel edge is off by a quarter of a pixel.

Which tells you where this bug lives: small triangles. Dense meshes. Exactly the geometry where you are least likely to be staring at any individual triangle.

A tenth of a pixel. On a smooth colour ramp that changes a channel by a fraction of one level out of 256 and you will never find it. So why care?

Because attributes are not always smooth. The moment an attribute is quantised — a texel index, a stripe, a checker cell, anything with a threshold in it — a sub-pixel shift at a threshold flips whole pixels to the wrong side. And here the honest measurement is more useful than a dramatic one. The demo draws a small triangle striped by an interpolated scalar, twice, and counts how many pixels the bias changes. The transferable lesson

A sub-pixel error in a quantised attribute cannot be tested away by trying one configuration and seeing nothing. "It looked fine when I tried it" is not evidence here — it is a sample of one from a distribution that contains both 0 and 15.

This is the general shape of numerical bugs in a renderer, and it is why the discipline is to reason about the magnitude of an error rather than to look for it. You can compute 1/‖e‖ in your head. You cannot eyeball it.

And the fix costs nothing, which is the happiest part. The bias is a known constant, chosen by us, sitting in an accumulator whose value is an exact integer. Undoing it is one subtraction:

\[ E_i(x,y) = \big(E_i(x,y) + b_i\big) - b_i \] true edge value = accumulator - bias

No second set of accumulators, no re-evaluation, no accumulated error to unwind — the stepped integers are exact (§3.8), so subtracting a constant from them is exact too. One integer subtraction per edge per pixel buys the correct answer, and the coverage test upstream keeps using the biased values, because for that job the bias is right.

One loop, any payload

Look at what the inner loop actually does with an attribute, once the weights are in hand: three multiplies and two adds, per component. Colour needs three of those (one per channel). A texture coordinate needs two. Depth needs one. A normal needs three.

Nothing about that arithmetic mentions what the numbers mean. Which is the promise of Figure 2 delivered — and it is also, honestly, where a rasterizer starts to accumulate a design problem. Our engine's fill_triangle interpolates a colour. The demo needs to interpolate a texture coordinate, and it does so by writing the loop out again with two floats where the three were. That is fine once. It is not fine four times.

Where this goes, so you can see it coming

The pressure resolves in three stages, and each is a lesson rather than a refactor done in passing. Module 3 grows struct vertex as each attribute earns its place — z for the depth buffer, u and v for textures, a normal for lighting — so there is one loop carrying a fixed, known set. Module 4 hands the whole problem to the GPU, where these are called varyings, you declare them in a shader signature, and dedicated hardware interpolates them for free. Module 5 is where we would build a general mechanism if we still needed one — and part of the lesson is that by then we do not.

Resisting the urge to write the general version today is deliberate. A templated N-attribute interpolator written before we have three real attributes would be designed against guesses.

What correctness costs, measured

Lesson 2.1 established the house rule: measure, do not guess. Doing colour properly means a pow in the encode direction, per pixel, per channel — and pow is expensive. So let us find out how expensive rather than worrying about it.

A triangle covering 20,760 pixels of a 320×180 framebuffer, filled 400 times, release build:

FillPer trianglePer pixelRelative
flat fill_triangle (Lesson 2.2)20.2 µs0.97 ns1.0×
shaded, encoded blend (wrong, but cheap)57.9 µs2.8 ns2.9×
shaded, linear blend (correct)232.0 µs11.2 ns11.5×

That is not a rounding error. Correct colour costs 11.5 times the flat fill, and four times the naive blend. At 232 µs a triangle you fit about 70 of them in a 60 Hz frame, which for a software rasterizer drawing one big triangle is fine and for a mesh is not.

Where does it go? Almost entirely into linear_to_srgb, which calls std::pow three times per pixel. Note the asymmetry with decoding: decode has only 256 possible inputs, so Lesson 1.6 put it in a lookup table and it costs a load. Encode takes a continuous float, so there is no table — at least, not an obvious one.

Two things are worth saying about that number, and they pull in opposite directions.

First, some of it we already avoided. The three corner colours are decoded once per triangle, not once per pixel. Had we decoded inside the loop the cost would have been higher again, for no benefit whatsoever — the corners do not change. Hoisting loop-invariant work out is the least glamorous optimisation there is and it is almost always the biggest one available.

Second, the remaining cost is removable, and we are not going to remove it today. A 4,096-entry table indexed by the linear value would replace the pow with a load, and the error is boundable rather than hoped-for: near black the encode curve has slope 12.92 × 255 ≈ 3295 stored levels per unit of light, so a table step of 1/4095 moves the output by about 0.8 of a level, and rounding to the nearest entry is under half a level of error everywhere. That is Exercise 2.4.3, with the measurement to confirm it.

Why not just do it now

Because the demo draws one triangle per frame and 232 µs of a 16,600 µs budget is not a problem we have. Optimising it now would be optimising by reflex, and Module 3's profiling lesson exists precisely to teach the alternative: find the cost, write down the number, and spend effort where the number is large. The number is written down. That is the discipline.

It is also worth knowing that this entire cost vanishes in Module 4. A GPU writes sRGB textures through dedicated hardware that does the encode as part of the write, for free. Paying for it in software is a consequence of doing everything ourselves, which is the point of Modules 2 and 3 rather than a flaw in them.

Stepping: less frightening than folklore

One more measurement, and this one corrected something I expected to find.

Lesson 2.1 §3.7 made a strong argument against running float sums: DDA's position is accumulated, so its error grows with the length of the line, while Bresenham's integer error term is exact at every step. The obvious extension is that stepping the weights as floats would drift too, and that this would be a visible problem. Our implementation steps integers and divides per pixel, so it sidesteps the question — but is the question real?

float weight, never reset across 900 rows4.26 × 10⁻⁵0.0110.17

The integer accumulator agrees with a direct evaluation of the edge function exactly, which is not a surprise — adding integers is adding integers. But the float column is the interesting one, and it is much better than the folklore suggests. After four thousand accumulated additions the error is about one eight-hundredth of a colour level. You could not see that if you tried.

So state the real reason

We step integers not because floats produce a visible artifact here — they do not — but because integers are exact, reproducible on every machine, and cost the same. When two options are equally cheap and one is exact, take the exact one; you no longer have to reason about its error at all, in this lesson or any later one that builds on it.

That is a weaker claim than "floats will ruin your triangle", and it has the advantage of being true. Overselling a real principle with a fake symptom is how students learn to distrust the principle.

Where float accumulation does bite is where the comparisons are against tiny differences. A depth buffer decides visibility by comparing two interpolated depths that may differ in the sixth decimal place, and there a 4 × 10⁻⁵ drift is not noise — it is the wrong surface in front. Lesson 3.1 meets that properly, and it will refer back to this table.

4.2  A vertex that keeps hold of its attributes

Here is the bug this section exists to make unwritable. fill_triangle reorients a backwards triangle by swapping two vertices:

That was harmless in Lesson 2.2 because a triangle was six loose integers and one colour for all of it. It is not harmless now. If the corners carry colours and you swap the coordinates without swapping the colours, you get a triangle of exactly the right shape, in exactly the right place, covering exactly the right pixels — shaded one corner out of step.

Why that bug is nasty

It fires only for triangles of one winding. A spinning triangle passes through both, so half the rotation looks right and half looks wrong, and the transition happens at the instant the triangle is edge-on and there is nothing to see anyway. A static test scene might contain no backwards triangles at all, and then the bug ships.

The fix is structural rather than careful. Put the position and its attributes in one object and the swap moves everything at once:

One std::swap, and there is no longer a version of this code that swaps some of a vertex. Add z to the struct in Module 3 and it comes along for free; add u and v and they do too. That is the argument for the struct — not tidiness, and not object-orientation. It removes a category of mistake rather than warning about it.

C++ in place — std::swap on an aggregate

std::swap on a plain struct of trivially-copyable members compiles to the same three moves you would write by hand — there is no penalty for the convenience, and with -O2 the temporary disappears entirely.

It also gets more valuable as the struct grows: the hand-written version has to be revisited every time a member is added, and a member added but not swapped is a silent bug of precisely the kind described above. Prefer the operation that cannot fall out of date.

We take the three vertices by const& and copy them into locals, rather than taking them by value. Both would work; by-reference-then-copy says plainly that the caller's vertices are not modified, which matters because a caller will very reasonably want to hand the same vertex to two adjacent triangles.

The rule this follows

Not "never repeat yourself" — that rule, applied literally, produces abstractions nobody wanted. The useful version is never repeat something subtle. A duplicated x + 1 costs nothing if it drifts. A duplicated top-left rule costs an afternoon with a magnifying glass.

4.4  Making the fill rule inspectable

is_top_left has lived in raster.cpp's anonymous namespace since Lesson 2.2, which was right when the bias was purely an internal detail of coverage. It is not internal any more: §3.5 requires anything that interpolates to undo the bias, so the rule that produces it is now part of how the rasterizer's numbers are to be interpreted. It moves to the header.

The immediate reason is the demo. To show what the bias does, the demo has to reproduce the fill loop with and without it — and it must ask the same question the engine asks. A re-typed copy of the rule in main.cpp could drift from the engine's, and then the magnifier would be comparing the demo against itself. Same discipline as Lesson 2.1's pixel inspector reading back the real routine's output: a rule you cannot inspect is a rule you cannot check.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Gradients have a dark muddy band through them — blending in encoded space
Symptom
Colour transitions look dirty rather than smooth. A red-to-green edge passes through a dark olive instead of a bright yellow-olive; the middle of a three-colour triangle is noticeably darker than any of its corners suggests it should be. Everything is plausible — there are no obvious artifacts, it just looks a bit grim.
Cause
The weighted average was taken on stored channel values rather than on the light they encode. Averaging codes is not averaging light (§3.3).
Fix
Decode the corner colours to linear once per triangle, interpolate there, encode once per pixel. Check the arithmetic against a known point: the centre of a red/green/blue triangle is (156,156,156), not (85,85,85). If you are seeing 85 you are in encoded space regardless of what the code appears to say.
The shading is rotated by one corner, but only sometimes — attributes left behind during reorientation
Symptom
A spinning triangle looks right for half its rotation and wrong for the other half: the red corner is suddenly green. Geometry is perfect throughout — the right pixels are covered, the edges are in the right places. Only the shading is out of step, and the switch happens as the triangle passes edge-on, where there is nothing to see.
Cause
fill_triangle swaps two vertices to force positive signed area. The coordinates were swapped and the attributes were not, so from that point on w₁ is being paired with v₂'s colour.
Fix
Swap whole vertices, not coordinates: std::swap(v1, v2) on a struct that holds the position and everything the corner carries (§4.2). To catch it in a test rather than by eye, draw the same triangle with both windings and compare the two framebuffers — they must be identical pixel for pixel. Our harness does exactly that over 200 triangles.
A faint seam along shared edges, or textures a hair out of place — interpolating with biased weights
Symptom
Usually nothing. Occasionally a one-pixel line of the wrong colour where two triangles meet, or a texture that lands a fraction of a texel off, or — with any attribute that gets quantised — a scatter of individually wrong pixels that look like noise. The weights printed at a pixel sum to something like 0.9667 instead of 1.
Cause
The top-left rule's -1 is still in the accumulators when they are divided by the area. That translates the whole attribute field by 1/‖e‖ pixels perpendicular to the opposite edge (§3.5) and breaks the sum-to-one identity by 1/2A or 2/2A.
Fix
Subtract each bias back out before dividing. To catch it: assert that the three interpolated weights sum to 1 within a small tolerance — that check is useless for the pairing bug (Lesson 2.3 §3.5) but it catches this one immediately, because a bias sum of -1 or -2 over the area is far larger than float noise.
The fill got dramatically slower — converting colours inside the loop
Symptom
Frame time jumps by an order of magnitude when Gouraud shading is switched on, far more than the extra multiply-adds could account for. A profiler points at pow or at linear_to_srgb.
Cause
The corner colours are being decoded per pixel instead of per triangle. The corners do not vary across a triangle, so that is twenty thousand identical conversions where three would do.
Fix
Hoist the three to_linear calls and the 1/area reciprocal above the row loop. Note that the encode at the end genuinely is per pixel and cannot be hoisted — §3.7 measures what it costs (11.2 ns/px) and Exercise 2.4.3 replaces it with a table.
Interpolated normals make surfaces uniformly too dark — forgetting that interpolation does not preserve length
Symptom
Not yet — this one is waiting for Lesson 3.6, and it is here so you recognise it when it arrives. Smoothly shaded surfaces come out slightly dimmer in the middle of each triangle than at the corners, by a few percent, with no visible seam.
Cause
Two unit vectors interpolated linearly produce a vector shorter than 1 — the chord between two points on a circle passes inside the circle. A Lambert term computed with a normal of length 0.94 is 6% too small.
Fix
Renormalise the interpolated normal per pixel. The deeper point is the one from §3.2: the rasterizer does not know what it is carrying, so "the interpolation is correct" and "the result is correct" are separate claims and you have to make the second one yourself.

8Exercises

2.4.1Carry something of your ownwarm-up

Add a third view that interpolates a single scalar of your choosing — distance from one corner, say, or a "temperature" of 0, 1 and 0.5 at the three vertices — and render it as a greyscale ramp. Before you run it, write down what you expect at each vertex, at the midpoint of each edge, and at the centroid.

Hint

You only need one weighted sum instead of three. If you render the ramp by writing pack_argb(v, v, v) directly from the scalar, ask yourself whether that value is a code or a quantity of light — and therefore whether it should go through to_encoded first. Both answers are defensible; the point is to notice the question.

Solution

Vertices give the corner values exactly. Edge midpoints give the average of the two corner values — the third weight is 0 there. The centroid gives the plain average of all three, since all weights are ⅓; for 0, 1 and 0.5 that is 0.5.

On the code-versus-light question: a "temperature" is an abstract scalar, not a quantity of light, so mapping it to a colour is a visualisation choice and either mapping is legitimate. The moment the scalar is a light — a Lambert term, say — the answer stops being a choice.

2.4.2Predict the pixelwarm-up

Without running anything, compute what the demo will print for the centre pixel if you change the corner colours to (255,255,255), (0,0,0) and (0,0,0) — in both blend spaces. Then change them and check.

Hint

In encoded space it is one third of 255. In linear space, decode (white is 1.0, black is 0.0), average to get ⅓ of a unit of light, then encode that. The encode is 1.055 × L^(1/2.4) − 0.055.

Solution

Encoded: (85, 85, 85). Linear: (156, 156, 156) — the same two numbers as the red/green/blue triangle, which is the interesting part. The primaries were never relevant; what mattered was that each channel was averaging one full value against two zeroes.

That generalises usefully: the linear-versus-encoded gap is largest when a channel spans its whole range across a triangle, and vanishes when the three corners have similar values. Which is why the error hides in low-contrast content and screams in high-contrast content.

2.4.3Kill the powmedium

§3.7 measured the correct blend at 11.2 ns/pixel, nearly all of it in three std::pow calls. Replace linear_to_srgb_u8 with a lookup table indexed by the linear value, measure the speedup, and — this is the actual exercise — bound the error before you measure it, then check whether your bound held.

Hint

The worst case is where the encode curve is steepest, which is at the black end where it is linear with slope 12.92. In stored levels that is 12.92 × 255 ≈ 3295 levels per unit of light. If your table has N entries spanning [0,1], one table step moves the output by 3295/(N−1) levels; rounding to the nearest entry halves that.

Compare every one of your table's answers against the real pow over a few million random inputs rather than trusting the bound.

Solution

With N = 4096: one step is 3295/4095 ≈ 0.80 levels, so nearest-entry rounding is under 0.4 levels everywhere — below one least-significant bit, so the table is visually exact. A 2048-entry table gives 0.8 levels of error, which is borderline; 1024 gives 1.6 and will band visibly in dark gradients.

Expect the shaded fill to drop from roughly 11 ns/pixel to 3–4, i.e. close to the encoded path's cost — which is the real conclusion: correct colour is not inherently expensive, a transcendental function per pixel is. Those are very different problems and only one of them was ever worth trading correctness for.

Worth noticing what made the bound possible: we know the function, its slope, and its worst case analytically. That is why the estimate came before the measurement rather than after.

2.4.4Map the biasmedium

Extend the magnifier to sweep the band count automatically — say 1.0 to 12.0 in steps of 0.25 — and print a table of differing-pixel counts. Then change the small triangle's size and do it again. Explain the shape of what you get.

Hint

Two quantities are in play and they scale differently. The displacement is 1/‖e‖ pixels and depends only on the triangle. The band spacing depends only on the frequency. What matters is the ratio, plus the phase — where the thresholds happen to sit relative to the pixel grid.

Solution

You will find no clean trend, and that is the answer. The count is essentially a sampling artifact: it counts how many pixel centres happen to lie within 0.088 px of a threshold, and that depends on an interaction between three lattices — pixels, bands, and the triangle's own geometry. Shrinking the triangle raises the displacement and pushes the counts up on average, but any individual setting can still read zero.

The transferable conclusion is the one in §3.5, now with your own data behind it: a sub-pixel error in a quantised attribute has a damage profile you cannot sample. You have to bound it analytically — and once you have, the fix costs one subtraction, so there is no decision left to make.

2.4.5Design the general version — then argue against itopen

We now have two fill loops that differ only in their payload, and Module 3 will want at least two more. Design a mechanism that lets the rasterizer carry an arbitrary set of attributes without a new loop per set — then make the strongest case you can that we should not adopt it yet.

Hint

The obvious candidates: a fixed-size float[N] of varyings; a template parameter for the attribute type with a required lerp; a callback invoked per pixel with the three weights. Cost each one honestly — in code size, in inlining, in what happens to the inner loop's register pressure, and in how legible the result is to somebody reading it to learn.

Solution sketch

A template on a small attribute struct with a static interpolate is probably the best of them: it inlines completely, keeps the loop as tight as the hand-written one, and costs nothing at runtime. A per-pixel callback is the worst — an indirect call in the innermost loop of a rasterizer, and it destroys the compiler's ability to keep the accumulators in registers. A float[N] array is a reasonable middle and is close to what real GPUs do.

The case against adopting any of them today is that we have exactly one attribute in the engine and one in a demo. A generalisation designed against two examples — one of which we wrote specifically to be an example — is designed against guesses. Module 3 adds depth, texture coordinates and normals for real reasons, and then the shape of the problem is visible. Waiting also has a specific payoff here: Module 4 moves this to the GPU, where the answer is a shader signature and dedicated hardware, and a general software mechanism built in Module 2 would be deleted unread.

The judgement worth internalising: the cost of duplicating something small and obvious is low and paid immediately; the cost of the wrong abstraction is high and paid for years. Two copies of a loop is not yet evidence. Four is.

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • It can shade a surface. A triangle is no longer one colour — every interior pixel gets its own, computed from the corners. That is the first thing in this engine that varies across a surface, and every lighting model in the course is built on it.
  • The formula is forced, not chosen. Three coefficients, three conditions, one affine solution — so there is no better scheme waiting to be discovered, only the perspective correction that admits screen-space affine was the wrong question.
  • Colour is arithmetic on light. linear_rgb exists so the distinction lives in a type rather than in a comment, and the difference is 156 against 85 — a factor of 3.66 in emitted light.
  • The fill rule's bias is confined to coverage. One integer subtraction separates "who owns this boundary pixel" from "where is this pixel", and we can say exactly what skipping it costs: 1/‖e‖ pixels of displacement.
  • Attributes travel with their vertices. A struct and one std::swap made a whole category of shading bug unwritable rather than merely documented.
  • The costs are written down. 11.5× the flat fill for correct colour, 11.2 ns per pixel, almost all of it in one pow — measured, recorded, and deliberately not optimised yet.

And two habits worth more than any of it. First: when an error is smaller than a pixel, derive its magnitude instead of looking for it — §3.5's table showed the same 0.088-pixel displacement doing nothing and ruining a third of a triangle, with only the sampling changed. Second: do not oversell a real principle with a fake symptom. Float-stepped weights drift by a thousandth of a colour level, not enough to see, and we step integers anyway because exactness is free. Saying so keeps the principle trustworthy.

Next: 2.5 — Matrices as Basis Transforms. Module 2 has spent four lessons on the inside of a triangle; now it turns to where triangles come from. A matrix will not be introduced as a grid of numbers with a multiplication rule — it will be introduced as the answer to one question: where do the basis vectors land? Everything else, including the multiplication rule, falls out of that.

It is also where the course stops being two-dimensional. The rasterizer we have just finished is complete enough to draw anything, given screen coordinates; the next seven lessons are about producing those coordinates from a scene.
