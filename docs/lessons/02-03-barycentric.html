Module 2 — Software Rasterizer I · Lesson 2.3

Barycentric Coordinates from Signed Areas

Your rasterizer already computes three numbers per pixel and throws two thirds of the information away. It uses their signs to decide inside-or-outside and discards their magnitudes — which were areas, and which between them say exactly where in the triangle the pixel is. One division recovers that. Everything the rest of this course draws on a surface — colour, depth, texture, lighting — rides on the three fractions we build today.

1The Problem

Lesson 2.2's fill_triangle paints every interior pixel the same colour. That is a silhouette — a flat paper cutout. Every surface you actually want to render varies across itself: a lit sphere is bright at one edge and dark at the other, a textured wall shows a different texel at every pixel, and a depth-tested scene needs each pixel to know how far away it is.

All of those are the same request. You know something at the three corners, and you need it at every pixel in between. So you need to be able to say, for a given pixel: where in this triangle am I?

And screen coordinates cannot answer that. A pixel at (147, 92) tells you where it is on the screen, which is a fact about the framebuffer, not about the triangle. Move the triangle and the answer should not change; move the pixel to the corresponding place in a different triangle and it should be the same answer. What is needed is a coordinate system intrinsic to the triangle — one that says "40% of the way toward this corner" rather than "147 pixels from the left".

The good news, before we start

You are already computing it. Lesson 2.2 §3.3 noticed that each edge function's magnitude is twice the area of a sub-triangle, and §3.4 noticed that the three of them sum to the area of the whole. The rasterizer looks at their signs, decides inside-or-outside, and throws the magnitudes away.

This lesson does not add a computation. It stops discarding one.

2Building Intuition

Where a triangle balances

The name comes from barycentre — centre of mass — and the original idea is worth meeting first, because it makes the whole thing obvious.

Take a weightless triangular plate and put a lump of mass at each corner. The assembly balances at one particular point, and where that point sits depends only on the ratio of the three masses. Equal masses balance at the middle. Pile mass onto one corner and the balance point slides toward it, reaching the corner itself in the limit where the other two are zero.

Now run that backwards, which is the move that turns an observation into a coordinate system. Given a point inside the triangle, what three masses would balance there? There is essentially one answer — one set of ratios — so the three masses are a way of naming the point (Figure 1). Scale them so they sum to 1 and the naming becomes unique.

Three sub-triangles that tile

Masses are the origin story, but they are not how we will compute anything. For that, join your point P to all three corners (Figure 2). The triangle falls apart into three smaller triangles, and — this is the whole lesson — they exactly tile the original. No gaps, no overlap. So their three areas add up to the total.

Divide each by the total and you have three fractions that sum to 1. Slide P toward a corner and the sub-triangle opposite that corner grows to fill everything, while the other two shrink to nothing. Slide it onto an edge and the sub-triangle on the far side of that edge collapses to zero area. The fractions behave exactly like the masses did — because they are the masses.

3The Theory

The definition, from areas

Write the triangle's vertices v0, v1, v2, and let P be the point. Define three weights:

\[ w_0 = \frac{\operatorname{area}(P v_1 v_2)}{\operatorname{area}(v_0 v_1 v_2)}, \qquad w_1 = \frac{\operatorname{area}(P v_2 v_0)}{\operatorname{area}(v_0 v_1 v_2)}, \qquad w_2 = \frac{\operatorname{area}(P v_0 v_1)}{\operatorname{area}(v_0 v_1 v_2)} \] w0 = area(P v1 v2) / area(v0 v1 v2), and likewise w1, w2 — each using the sub-triangle OPPOSITE its vertex

In prose: each weight is the area of the sub-triangle opposite its vertex, divided by the area of the whole.

And we can already compute all four of those areas, because Lesson 2.2's edge function is twice a signed area. The bottom of each fraction is edge_function(v0, v1, v2); the top of w0 is edge_function(v1, v2, P). The factors of two cancel, and the signs cancel too — which is why this works for either winding without a special case:

\[ w_0 = \frac{E_{12}(P)}{E_{01}(v_2)}, \qquad w_1 = \frac{E_{20}(P)}{E_{01}(v_2)}, \qquad w_2 = \frac{E_{01}(P)}{E_{01}(v_2)} \] w0 = edge_function(v1,v2,P) / area2, w1 = edge_function(v2,v0,P) / area2, w2 = edge_function(v0,v1,P) / area2

Three integers you already have, one integer you compute once per triangle, and one division. That is the entire implementation.

Why they sum to one

Because the three sub-triangles tile the original, so their areas add to its area, so the three fractions add to one. That is the geometric argument and it is complete for points inside.

But it is worth knowing something stronger, because it is what makes the weights useful outside the triangle too. In terms of the edge functions the identity is:

\[ E_{12}(P) + E_{20}(P) + E_{01}(P) = E_{01}(v_2) \qquad \text{for every point } P \text{ in the plane} \] edge_function(v1,v2,P) + edge_function(v2,v0,P) + edge_function(v0,v1,P) = the total, for EVERY P

Expand the three edge functions symbolically and every term containing P appears once with each sign, so they all cancel; what survives does not mention P at all. The sum is therefore the total area no matter where P is — inside, outside, or a thousand pixels away, where one or two of the areas are negative and the cancellation still works out.

Leave this in as an assertion

In integers this identity is exact. If your three edge values ever fail to sum to the area, one of them has its vertices in the wrong order — and you will find out at the line that is wrong, rather than three lessons later when a texture looks sheared. It is the cheapest correctness check in the rasterizer.

The property that actually defines them

Here is the one to remember, and the reason "they sum to 1" is not a sufficient test:

\[ w_0\,v_0 + w_1\,v_1 + w_2\,v_2 = P \] w0*v0 + w1*v1 + w2*v2 = P

In prose: the weights, applied to the three vertices, reconstruct the original point. This is the centre-of-mass statement from §2.1 written as arithmetic, and it is what "barycentric coordinates" means. Everything else — summing to one, being 1 at a vertex, going negative outside — follows from it.

It is also the check that catches real bugs, which the sum does not. A set of weights can sum perfectly to 1 and still describe the wrong point (§3.5 shows exactly that). Reconstruction cannot be fooled: if the weights are wrong, the point comes back wrong.

A worked example

Worked example — the same triangle as Lesson 2.2

v0 = (2, 2), v1 = (10, 4), v2 = (4, 10), and the point P = (5, 5). Lesson 2.2 §3.4 already computed everything we need:

  • Total: E01(v2) = 60, so the triangle's area is 30.
  • E12(P) = 24 — the sub-triangle facing v0
  • E20(P) = 18 — facing v1
  • E01(P) = 18 — facing v2

Divide each by 60:

\[ w_0 = \tfrac{24}{60} = 0.4, \qquad w_1 = \tfrac{18}{60} = 0.3, \qquad w_2 = \tfrac{18}{60} = 0.3 \] w0 = 24/60 = 0.4, w1 = 18/60 = 0.3, w2 = 18/60 = 0.3

They sum to 1.0 ✓. Now the check that matters — reconstruct P:

  • x = 0.4(2) + 0.3(10) + 0.3(4) = 0.8 + 3.0 + 1.2 = 5.0 ✓
  • y = 0.4(2) + 0.3(4) + 0.3(10) = 0.8 + 1.2 + 3.0 = 5.0 ✓

(5, 5), which is where we started. These numbers are asserted in the verification harness, so the lesson and the code cannot drift apart.

The pairing trap

w0 uses the edge v1→v2 — the edge that does not touch v0. It is very natural to reach for v0→v1 instead, because that edge has v0's name on it. Doing so produces weights that are all still in [0,1], still sum to exactly 1, and are simply wrong.

Worked example — what a wrong pairing costs

Take the correct weights from above, (0.4, 0.3, 0.3), and apply them to the vertices rotated by one — the mistake you make by pairing each weight with the edge that shares its name:

  • x = 0.3(2) + 0.3(10) + 0.4(4) = 0.6 + 3.0 + 1.6 = 5.2
  • y = 0.3(2) + 0.3(4) + 0.4(10) = 0.6 + 1.2 + 4.0 = 5.8

(5.2, 5.8) instead of (5, 5). The sum is still 1. Every weight is still a plausible fraction. The point is just… somewhere else.

In a rendered image this shows up as texture that is subtly rotated within each triangle, or lighting that is brightest in the wrong corner — a defect that looks like a content problem rather than a code one, which is what makes it expensive.

Test reconstruction, not the sum

This is why §3.3 insists that reconstruction is the defining property. A sum check passes for all three rotations of a wrong pairing. A reconstruction check passes for exactly one.

Verified across 3,721 points spread well beyond the triangle: worst reconstruction error 0.000015 px.

Iso-lines, edges, and the outside

A few facts fall straight out of the definition, and knowing them makes debugging much faster because you can predict what a correct implementation should print.

At a vertex, that vertex's weight is 1 and the others are 0. Put P on v0 and the sub-triangle facing v0 is the whole triangle, while the other two are degenerate.

On an edge, the opposite vertex's weight is exactly 0. The sub-triangle facing it has collapsed to a line.

At the centroid, all three are 1/3 — the equal-masses case from Figure 1.

A constant weight traces a line parallel to the opposite edge, and the lines are evenly spaced (Figure 3). This one deserves a moment. The weight w0 is an area divided by a constant, and the sub-triangle facing v0 has a fixed base — the edge v1→v2. Its area therefore depends only on its height: how far P is from that edge. So "same w0" means "same distance from the opposite edge", which is precisely a parallel line.

Outside the triangle, at least one weight is negative — and the formula keeps working. This is the same statement as Lesson 2.2's seven regions, in different units: "inside" was "all three edge functions non-negative", and it is now "all three weights non-negative". Same test, divided by a positive constant.

Negative weights are a feature

Because the weights are defined over the whole plane, the same machinery extrapolates as well as interpolates. That is not idle: it is how a rasterizer evaluates a triangle's attributes at a pixel centre that lies slightly outside the covered area (needed for correct texture derivatives), and it is why conservative rasterization and multisampling can reason about partially covered pixels at all.

Verified over 5,041 sample points: "all three weights non-negative" agrees with fill_triangle's inside test at every single one.

Why this interpolation is the only one

Given some quantity known at the corners — call the values f0, f1, f2, and let them be a colour, a depth, a texture coordinate, anything — the barycentric weights blend them:

\[ f(P) = w_0 f_0 + w_1 f_1 + w_2 f_2 \] f(P) = w0*f0 + w1*f1 + w2*f2

It is tempting to file this under "a reasonable way to blend three things". It is considerably stronger than that: it is the only affine function of position that takes the right values at the three corners. Not the best one, not the natural one — the only one.

The argument is short. An affine function of two variables, f(x,y) = ax + by + c, has exactly three free coefficients. Requiring it to match a given value at each of three non-collinear points imposes exactly three independent conditions. Three unknowns, three independent equations, one solution. And the barycentric blend is affine in P — because each wi is an edge function over a constant, and Lesson 2.2 §3.5 established those are affine. So it is a solution, and therefore the solution.

"Affine in screen space" is not "affine on the surface"

That uniqueness is about the screen. Once a triangle is tilted away from a perspective camera, equal steps across the screen are not equal steps across the surface — so this blend, while still the unique affine screen-space interpolation, stops being the physically correct one.

The artifact is famous and horrible: textures that swim and bend as the camera moves. Lesson 3.2 shows it, explains it, and fixes it with the 1/w trick. Until then we have no perspective at all, so screen-space interpolation is exactly right — and it is worth knowing now that the correction is coming and why.

Precision, measured

The edge functions are integers and exact. The division is not. So how close to 1 does the sum actually get?

Measured over 32,761 points
  • Worst |w0 + w1 + w2 − 1|: 2.4 × 10⁻⁷ — about one float rounding, as expected.
  • Sum was bitwise exactly 1.0f at 27,876 of 32,761 points — roughly 85%.

Both halves matter. The error is tiny and bounded, because the integer identity is exact and only the final division rounds — there is no accumulation, unlike the running sums Lesson 2.1 §3.1 warned about. But it is not exactly 1, 15% of the time.

Never compare weights for equality

if (w0 == 0.0f) to test "on the edge" will miss most of the pixels that are on the edge. if (w0 + w1 + w2 == 1.0f) as an assertion will fire spuriously about one time in seven. Compare against a tolerance, or better, do the test on the integer edge values before dividing — where it is exact.

Use unbiased values for interpolation

One trap that only exists because of Lesson 2.2. The top-left fill rule subtracts 1 from some edge values to decide boundary ownership. That bias is correct for the inside test and wrong for interpolation — it shifts the weights by a fraction of a pixel.

Keep them separate: bias the copies used for coverage, and interpolate with the unbiased ones. Lesson 2.4 wires both into the same loop and has to be careful about exactly this.

4Implementation

The type is three floats, and giving it a name rather than returning a bare array is worth the six lines — b.w0 at the call site is self-documenting where b[0] is an invitation to the pairing bug:

And the computation is six lines, of which three are the edge functions we already had:

Two details are deliberate. The reciprocal is computed once and multiplied three times, because division is markedly slower than multiplication and this is the shape the per-pixel version in Lesson 2.4 will need. And the degenerate case returns all zeros rather than dividing by zero — the one input for which the weights do not sum to 1, documented in the header, because a silent NaN here would spread through every later calculation exactly as Lesson 1.3 §3.5 described.

C++ in place — returning an aggregate by value

return {a, b, c}; constructs the barycentric in the caller's storage directly — no temporary, no copy. That is guaranteed since C++17, not merely an optimisation compilers usually manage.

Which is why "return a struct" costs nothing here and you should not contort the interface into out-parameters to avoid it. Twelve bytes returned by value beats three float* arguments the caller has to allocate and you have to trust.

The demo's two new views are where the abstraction becomes something you can look at. The probe draws the three sub-triangles directly:

Those three lines are Figure 2, generated by the same pairing the maths uses. If someone later changes the pairing in one place and not the other, the picture stops matching the numbers — which is the kind of self-check a diagram drawn by hand can never give you.

The iso-line view needs one small idea. We want to draw a line where a weight crosses a multiple of 0.1, but §3.8 just established that floats will essentially never land on such a value exactly. So do not look for the value — look for the crossing:

If this pixel and its right-hand or lower neighbour fall in different "bands" of width 0.1, a contour passes between them. No equality test, no tolerance to tune, and it works for any step size. This is a generally useful trick for drawing level sets of anything.

6Build & Run

What you should see

The triangles demo, now with two more views on 4 and 5. Both follow the mouse.

Press 4 for the weight map. The triangle is filled with a ramp: bright where w0 is near 1 — at vertex 0 — fading to dark along the opposite edge where it reaches 0. Beyond that edge the ramp continues into red, which is w0 going negative. The weights do not stop at the triangle's boundary, and this view refuses to pretend they do.

Press 5 for the iso-lines: three families of evenly spaced parallel lines, one per vertex, each family parallel to that vertex's opposite edge. That lattice is the triangle's coordinate system, and seeing all three at once is the clearest picture of why three numbers are needed to name a point in two dimensions — they are not independent, which is exactly what "they sum to 1" means.

In both views, move the mouse. Three coloured outlines follow it: the three sub-triangles from Figure 2, tinted to match the vertex each one faces. Three bars show the weights, and the readout prints them with their sum.

Checkpoint — four things to verify by hand
  1. Put the cursor on a vertex: that vertex's weight reads 1.0000 and the other two 0.0000.
  2. Put it on an edge: the weight of the vertex opposite that edge reads 0.0000.
  3. Put it in the middle: all three read about 0.3333.
  4. Move it outside: at least one weight goes negative — and the sum stays 1.000000.

That last one is the surprising one, and it is the whole content of §3.2: the identity is not a property of being inside the triangle.

Watch the sum as you move around. It will read 1.000000 almost everywhere, and that is the measured 2.4 × 10⁻⁷ worst case being invisible at six decimal places — not the exactness of floating point. §3.8 has the numbers.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Interpolation is subtly rotated within each triangle — the pairing bug
Symptom
Colours or textures are present and smooth but land in the wrong corners — as if each triangle's content had been rotated by one vertex. Weights all look plausible and sum to 1.
Cause
Each weight paired with the edge sharing its name rather than the edge opposite it. w0 must use v1→v2.
Fix
Test reconstruction, not the sum: w0·v0 + w1·v1 + w2·v2 must return the point you asked about. A sum check passes for all three wrong rotations; reconstruction passes for exactly one. §3.5.
Everything is NaN after one bad triangle — division by zero
Symptom
A mesh renders fine until one particular triangle, after which colours go black or garbage — sometimes for the rest of the frame.
Cause
A degenerate triangle: three collinear vertices, zero area, dividing by zero. The result is NaN or ±inf, and NaN propagates through every arithmetic operation it touches while comparing false to everything.
Fix
Check the area before dividing, as barycentric_at does. Degenerate triangles are not exotic — they appear whenever geometry is scaled to zero, whenever two vertices are welded, and routinely in imported meshes.
An "on the edge" test almost never fires — comparing floats for equality
Symptom
if (w0 == 0.0f) to detect edge pixels catches almost none of them, or a sum == 1.0f assertion fires on perfectly good input.
Cause
The division rounds. Measured: the sum is bitwise 1.0f only about 85% of the time, and the worst deviation is 2.4 × 10⁻⁷.
Fix
Use a tolerance — or better, do the test on the integer edge values before dividing, where "exactly on the edge" really is exactly == 0. The integers are the exact object; the floats are a convenience.
Interpolated values are off by a fraction of a pixel — the fill-rule bias leaked in
Symptom
Everything looks nearly right, but attributes are consistently shifted a tiny amount toward one edge — visible as a seam when two triangles meet.
Cause
Interpolating with the edge values that Lesson 2.2's top-left rule biased by −1. That bias exists to decide coverage; it is not part of the geometry.
Fix
Keep two sets: biased for the inside test, unbiased for interpolation. Lesson 2.4 wires both into one loop and this is the detail it has to get right.
Textures swim as the camera moves — not a bug yet, but it will be
Symptom
Once perspective arrives, textures on triangles tilted away from the camera bend and slide, especially along the diagonal where two triangles meet.
Cause
Screen-space barycentric interpolation is affine on the screen, and under perspective that is not affine on the surface. §3.7.
Fix
Perspective-correct interpolation — interpolate attribute/w and 1/w, then divide. Lesson 3.2 shows the artifact first, then derives it. Nothing to do now: without perspective, screen-space is exactly right.

8Exercises

2.3.1Break the pairing on purposewarm-up

Rotate the pairing in barycentric_at — give w0 the edge v0→v1 — and run the demo. Confirm the weights still sum to 1 and still look reasonable. Then write a reconstruction check and watch it fail.

Hint

Reconstruction is three multiply-adds per axis. Assert it in a debug build for a few points per triangle; the cost is nothing compared to the hours it saves.

Solution

With the pairing rotated, the point (5,5) reconstructs as (5.2, 5.8) — the number from §3.5, which is what the harness asserts.

The transferable lesson is about choosing invariants. "Sums to 1" is an invariant that a broken implementation satisfies, so it has almost no diagnostic power. Reconstruction is satisfied by exactly one implementation. When picking something to assert, prefer the property that determines the answer over one that merely constrains it.

2.3.2Predict, then checkwarm-up

Before running anything, write down the weights you expect at: each vertex; the midpoint of each edge; the centroid; a point reflected across an edge to just outside. Then use the demo's probe to check.

Solution

Vertices (1,0,0) and rotations. Edge midpoints have the opposite weight 0 and the other two 0.5 each. Centroid (⅓,⅓,⅓). Just outside an edge, the opposite weight is slightly negative and the other two sum to slightly more than 1.

The exercise is really about the last case: many people expect the weights to clamp or the formula to break down outside, and it simply does not. That continuity is what makes extrapolation work.

2.3.3Make the weights incrementalmedium

Lesson 2.2 §3.5 made the edge functions incremental — constant steps per pixel, no multiplies. The weights are those values times a constant, so they must be incremental too. Write a fill loop that carries all three weights across a scanline by addition, and check it against barycentric_at per pixel.

Hint

Multiply the integer steps by inv_area once, outside the loop, and step the floats. Then ask the question that matters: does the float version drift across a wide triangle? Compare against the exact integer version at the end of each row.

Solution sketch

It works, and it is what Lesson 2.4 does. The interesting finding is the drift: stepping floats reintroduces exactly the accumulation Lesson 2.1 §3.1 objected to in DDA, where computing from integers each time does not. For a 320-pixel-wide triangle the error is far below a pixel and nobody cares; for a 4K-wide one it is worth stepping the integers and doing one multiply per pixel instead.

Measure before choosing. The right answer depends on the triangle sizes you actually draw, which is the recurring theme of this module.

2.3.4Barycentric coordinates for a tetrahedronopen

Everything here generalises: in 3-D, four points define a tetrahedron and a point inside gets four weights from the four sub-tetrahedron volumes. Work out the formula, and say what plays the role of the edge function.

Hint

Signed area came from the 2-D cross product. Signed volume comes from the scalar triple product a · (b × c), which is six times the volume of the tetrahedron they span. Everything else is the same argument with one more index.

Solution sketch

Four weights, each a sub-volume over the total, summing to 1, reconstructing the point. Useful in practice: it is how you locate a point inside a tetrahedral mesh, and it is the basis of tetrahedral interpolation for colour lookup tables.

Doing this once is worth more than it looks, because it shows that "barycentric" is not a triangle trick — it is what affine combination means in any dimension, and triangles are simply the two-dimensional case we happen to rasterise.

2.3.5Point-in-triangle without a rasterizeropen

Use barycentric_at to write a picking routine: given a mouse position and a list of triangles, find which one contains the cursor. Then extend it to return where inside — which is what a real editor needs in order to know which part of a mesh you clicked.

Solution sketch

The containment test is "all three weights ≥ 0", and it comes back with the position for free — that is the point of the exercise. Compare against what a scanline filler could have told you: nothing, without re-rasterising the whole triangle.

This is also a first taste of Module 8's editor work, and of why the same three numbers show up in ray-triangle intersection (the Möller–Trumbore algorithm returns barycentrics directly, for exactly this reason).

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • It can say where a pixel is inside a triangle, as three fractions that depend on the triangle rather than on the screen.
  • Derived, not defined. The weights came out of the sub-triangle areas the rasterizer was already computing and discarding; the only new operation is one division.
  • The defining property is reconstruction, not the sum — and the difference between those two is the difference between a check that catches the pairing bug and one that does not.
  • The geometry is predictable: 1 at a vertex, 0 on the opposite edge, ⅓ at the centroid, negative outside, and constant along lines parallel to the opposite edge.
  • The interpolation is unique, not merely reasonable: three coefficients, three conditions, one affine solution.
  • The precision is measured: exact in integers, one rounding in float, and never to be compared for equality.

And a habit worth keeping: when choosing an invariant to assert, prefer the property that determines the answer over one that merely constrains it. "Sums to 1" is satisfied by three different wrong implementations. "Reconstructs the point" is satisfied by one.

Next: 2.4 — Interpolating Attributes Across a Triangle. We have the weights; now we hang things on them. Colour first — where Lesson 1.6's linear-light argument stops being theoretical, because blending three vertex colours is exactly the operation that goes wrong when you average sRGB values. Then texture coordinates, and then the realisation that the machinery does not care what it is carrying.

It also folds the weights into fill_triangle's incremental loop, which means confronting the bias problem from §3.8: the same three numbers, biased for coverage and unbiased for interpolation, computed side by side.

        instead of (5,5). ALWAYS test RECONSTRUCTION (w0*v0+w1*v1+w2*v2 == P),
        never just the sum.
        e0+e1+e2 == area EXACTLY in integers, for every P in the plane (inside or
        out) — leave it in as an assertion.
        Geometry: 1 at its vertex, 0 on the opposite edge, 1/3 at the centroid,
        NEGATIVE outside (same test as 2.2's inside test, divided by a constant).
        Constant weight = a line PARALLEL to the opposite edge, evenly spaced,
        because that edge is a fixed base so equal area means equal height.
        Interpolation with these weights is the UNIQUE affine function matching
        the three corners (3 coefficients, 3 conditions) — not merely reasonable.
        Affine in SCREEN space, which stops being surface-correct under
        perspective: Lesson 3.2's 1/w trick.
        PRECISION (measured, 32761 points): worst |sum-1| = 2.4e-7, and the sum is
        bitwise 1.0f only ~85% of the time. NEVER compare weights for equality —
        test the INTEGER edge values, where "on the edge" is exactly 0.
        USE UNBIASED edge values for interpolation: the top-left rule's -1 bias is
        for coverage only and shifts weights by a fraction of a pixel.
