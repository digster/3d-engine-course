Module 1 — The Loop, the Pixel, and Time · Lesson 1.6

Colour, and an Honest Teaser of sRGB

We have been treating a pixel's 32 bits as four numbers between 0 and 255, and moving them around without asking what they mean. Copying bits never asks. But the moment we combine two colours — mixing, fading, averaging, or later adding up light from several lamps — the question becomes unavoidable, and the intuitive answer is wrong by a factor of more than two. This lesson shows you exactly how wrong, and is honest about the fact that we are not going to fix it yet.

1The Problem

Exercise 1.5.5 asked you to fade the ball's trail, and warned that doing the arithmetic directly on the stored 0–255 values would come out too dark. Here is that claim made concrete. Mix pure red with pure green, half and half, the obvious way:

Nothing about that is arithmetically wrong. Figure 1 shows the result, next to what the answer should be:

The muddy one is what you get by averaging the stored numbers. If your monitor is reasonably calibrated, it should look noticeably darker and browner than a genuine half-and-half mix.

So one of two things is true: either "halfway" does not mean what we think, or the numbers do not mean what we think. It is the second one — and the reason is a transformation applied to essentially every image, colour value and display on earth, which nobody mentions because it normally works invisibly.

2Building Intuition

2.1 Counting candles

Imagine a room lit by 100 candles, and call that "white" — the brightest the room gets. Now: how many candles are lit when a pixel reads 128?

The instinctive answer is 50, and it is wrong. The answer is about 22.

The stored value is not a count of candles. It is a position on a dial, and the dial is not evenly spaced. Turning the dial from 0 to halfway does not light half the candles; it lights about a fifth of them. The dial's markings are crowded together at the dark end and spread far apart at the bright end.

That is the entire lesson. Everything below is why the dial is shaped that way, exactly how it is shaped, and which of our operations the shape breaks.

2.2 Why anyone did this on purpose

It is tempting to file this under historical accident — CRT phosphors had a power-law response, the encoding compensated, we are stuck with it. That is true and it is not the interesting part, because even with no CRT in the story we would invent something very similar. The reason is your eye.

Human vision responds to ratios, not differences. Take a room with 1 candle and add another: the change is enormous, unmissable. Take a room with 100 candles and add one: nobody notices. The same absolute increase — one candle — is dramatic at the bottom and invisible at the top.

Now suppose we had 256 evenly spaced steps of actual light, so each step is 1/255 of white. Down at the dark end, step 1 is double the light of step 0 — a visible jump. Up at the bright end, step 255 is 1.004× step 254 — utterly imperceptible. We would have spent most of our 256 precious values on distinctions nobody can see, while the darks, where the eye is most sensitive, would visibly band into stripes.

So the encoding redistributes them, as Figure 2 shows. It spends many values on the dark range and few on the bright range, matching where the eye can actually tell things apart. Eight bits of encoded sRGB looks smooth; eight bits of evenly spaced light does not. It is a compression scheme for a channel with exactly 256 slots, and it is a good one.

The cost of that good decision

Storage is not arithmetic. The encoding is excellent for keeping a colour in eight bits and useless for calculating with one, because the numbers are no longer proportional to the thing they describe. Adding two encoded values does not add two lights, and averaging them does not average two lights. Every graphics bug in this lesson comes from doing arithmetic in the storage format — and every fix is the same: decode, compute, re-encode.

3The Theory

3.1 The transfer function

The curve relating stored values to light is standardised, and the standard is called sRGB. It is very nearly a power law with exponent 2.2, but the exact definition is piecewise: a short straight segment near black, then the curve.

The straight segment near zero exists because the pure power curve has infinite slope at the origin, which makes the darkest few values behave badly — both numerically and when quantised to eight bits. The constants are chosen so the two pieces meet smoothly. They are not roundable to "gamma 2.2": using pow(x, 2.2) as a shortcut is close, and close is exactly how a pipeline accumulates an error that nobody can later locate.

Figure 3 plots that function against the assumption everyone starts with — that a stored value and the light it produces are proportional.

Worked example — the two numbers to remember

What does 128 emit? The value is above the linear toe, so take the curve:

  • 128 / 255 = 0.50196 encoded
  • (0.50196 + 0.055) / 1.055 = 0.52792
  • 0.52792 ^ 2.4 = 0.2159

So mid-grey emits 21.6% of white's light. Now the reverse — what stores half?

  • 0.5 ^ (1/2.4) = 0.74915
  • 1.055 × 0.74915 − 0.055 = 0.73536
  • 0.73536 × 255 = 187.5, so 188

128 emits 21.6%; half the light is stored as 188. Both numbers are printed by the demo at startup, computed by the same code that draws the ramps — so if your build disagrees with this paragraph, one of us has a bug.

3.2 The proof you can perform yourself

You do not have to take the number on faith. There is a classic test that uses your own eye as the measuring instrument, and it works because of one physical fact: when two colours are too small to resolve individually, the light from them adds. Your eye performs an honest linear average, whatever the numbers say.

So: make a pattern of alternating pure black and pure white pixels. Half the area emits 0% and half emits 100%, so from far enough away it must look like exactly 50% light. Now put patches of solid grey next to it. Whichever patch matches is, by definition, the stored value for half the light.

Step back from the screen, or squint, until the stripes blur together.

The left block is alternating black and white lines — physically 50% light. One of the two grey patches will merge with it and the other will look obviously darker. On a correctly behaving display, 188 is the one that matches, and 128 reads as much darker than the stripes. Requires 100% browser zoom to work properly — scaling resamples the stripes and destroys the test.

If the test does not work for you

A few things can defeat it, and all of them are worth knowing about. Browser zoom other than 100% resamples the stripe pattern into grey and the comparison becomes meaningless. Some displays apply their own processing. And on a genuinely mis-calibrated monitor the matching patch may sit somewhere between the two. The direction is the robust part: 128 should look clearly darker than the stripes, never brighter. If it looks brighter, something in your display chain is applying a curve of its own.

3.3 What this breaks

Not everything. It is worth being precise, because "gamma" gets blamed for a great deal that has nothing to do with it.

OperationSafe on stored values?Why
Copying, moving, storing a pixelYesNo arithmetic happens. The bits are opaque and stay opaque.
Comparing two pixels for equalityYesThe encoding is a bijection — equal encoded values mean equal light.
Choosing a colour by eye and using itYesYou picked it in the same space it is stored in.
Mixing / cross-fading two coloursNoThe midpoint of the numbers is not the midpoint of the light — Figure 1.
Fading to black, alpha blendingNoSame reason; the result is systematically too dark.
Scaling an image down, mipmappingNoAveraging neighbouring pixels is mixing. Downscaled images darken.
Adding light from two lampsNoLight adds linearly. Encoded values do not. This is Module 3 onwards.
Anti-aliased edgesNoAn edge pixel is a coverage-weighted mix — hence dark fringes on light text.

The pattern is exact: anything that combines two colours arithmetically is wrong on stored values; anything that merely moves them around is fine. Our engine has been doing only the second kind, which is why nothing has looked wrong yet — and the ball's fading trail in Exercise 1.5.5 was the first thing to cross the line.

Look at the bottom row: asking for 10% of the light and getting 1% is a factor of ten. This is why naive fades appear to fall off a cliff into black rather than easing out, and why a naively-faded trail vanishes long before it should. The error is worst exactly where fades spend most of their time.

3.4 Why alpha is different

A pixel has four channels and only three of them are light. It is a common and subtle mistake to run all four through the transfer function.

Red, green and blue describe how much light, so they are encoded. Alpha describes how much of the pixel is covered — a geometric fraction, an area. An alpha of 0.5 means half the pixel is occupied by the thing being drawn. That is already a linear quantity: half the area is half the area regardless of how bright anything is. There is no perceptual curve to undo because there was never one applied.

The fourth argument is the odd one out on purpose, and the comment above it in the source says why. Putting alpha through a light transform is a category error — it would make half-covered pixels report as roughly one-fifth covered, and the resulting edges would be wrong in a way that looks like a blending bug rather than a units bug.

3.5 What we are not fixing today

Here is the honest part. We now have correct mixing functions, and the demo uses them. That is not the same as having a correct renderer, and it would be misleading to let you think otherwise.

A properly linear pipeline decodes once, on the way in, does all its work in linear light, and encodes once, on the way out. Ours decodes and re-encodes around every individual operation, which is both slower and lossier — each round trip quantises back to 256 steps, so a chain of blends erodes precision that a linear pipeline would have kept.

Doing it properly requires three things we do not have:

  • A framebuffer that can hold linear values. Eight bits of linear light is precisely the banding disaster §2.2 described. Linear pipelines use 16-bit floats or more per channel, which is a different framebuffer than the one we built.
  • Somewhere to put values above 1.0. Add two lights and you can exceed white. That is not an error — it is what a bright highlight is — and an eight-bit buffer has nowhere to put it, so it clips and the information is gone.
  • A tonemapping step. Given a buffer that can exceed 1.0, something must map that range back down to what a display can show. That is a rendering decision with real artistic weight, not a conversion.

Those three together are the HDR pipeline, and they are a Module 6 lesson with the whole of PBR standing behind them. Building it now, on a renderer that cannot yet draw a triangle, would be assembling the plumbing before the house.

What you get for knowing this now, six modules early

Three things. You will recognise the symptom — dark fringes, muddy blends, fades that fall off a cliff — instead of chasing it as a blending bug. You will know that Module 3's Lambert shading is computing light, and that its numbers therefore belong in linear space. And when Module 6 introduces the HDR pipeline, it will read as the solution to a problem you have already watched happen, rather than as ceremony imposed by a standard.

4Implementation: the colour module

A new file, and a small move.

4.1 Where pack_argb lives now

pack_argb was declared in framebuffer.hpp in Lesson 1.5, for the honest reason that there was nowhere else to put it. Now there is. It moves to colour.hpp along with the channel accessors, and framebuffer.hpp includes that instead:

The dependency runs the right way: a framebuffer is a container of colours, so it may know what a colour is; a colour has no business knowing it lives in a framebuffer. Getting that direction backwards is how headers end up mutually dependent, and it is worth fixing while the fix costs one line. Physical dependency structure gets a full lesson in Module 5's refactor.

4.2 A lookup table in one direction only

Decoding takes an eight-bit value, so there are exactly 256 possible inputs and the whole function fits in a kilobyte:

That replaces a pow — tens of cycles — with a load, which matters when it happens three times per pixel. Encoding gets no such table, because its input is a continuous float with no finite set of possible values. The asymmetry is worth noticing: it is not that one direction deserves optimising more, it is that only one direction can be tabulated.

C++ in place — initialising a const table with a lambda

The [] { … }() is a lambda defined and immediately called. Its return value initialises the array, which lets the array be const — and that matters more than it looks.

The alternative is a mutable array plus an init_colour_tables() that somebody must remember to call before the first use. That design has a failure mode: forget the call, or make it after something already read the table, and you get a table of zeros — meaning every colour decodes to black, silently, with no crash to point at the cause. Initialising at the point of declaration makes the uninitialised state unreachable rather than merely unlikely.

One thing to be careful of: this is a namespace-scope object, so it is constructed before main runs, and the order of such constructions across translation units is unspecified. It is safe here because the lambda calls only a free function in the same file, never another global object. When that stops being true — and in a large engine it does — the standard fix is a function-local static, which is initialised on first use instead. Module 5 revisits this properly when the engine acquires real subsystems with startup order.

4.3 The demo as a controlled experiment

The comparison board draws each ramp twice. The two versions must differ in exactly one thing, so the drawing function takes the mixing rule as a parameter:

Uint32 (*mix)(Uint32, Uint32, float) is a pointer to a function taking two colours and a fraction. Passing engine::mix_encoded or engine::mix_linear selects the rule, and everything else about the two strips — position, size, endpoints, loop — is provably identical because it is literally the same code. That is worth the slightly hairy declaration: a comparison where the two sides went through different code paths would be a comparison of two code paths, not of two rules.

The pairs are drawn touching, with no gap:

Touching, because the eye is far better at judging a boundary than at remembering a colour across a gap. Two swatches side by side with a seam between them can be compared instantly; the same two swatches an inch apart cannot.

And the ball's trail — Exercise 1.5.5, now built — fades using whichever rule M selects, so the same question appears in motion as well as in a static strip.

The clear is back

Lesson 1.5 removed fb.clear() because the gradient covered every pixel, and warned that the moment a background stopped covering the frame the previous frame would smear. That moment is now: the comparison board leaves plenty of background visible, so the clear returns. A small thing, but it is the predicted consequence arriving exactly one lesson later, which is the best evidence that the reasoning was right rather than decorative.

6Build & Run

What you should see

The console states the lesson's two numbers, computed by the same code that draws the ramps:

And in the window, four ramps in two touching pairs:

  1. Look at the black-to-white pair first. The upper strip — mixed as stored numbers — reaches mid-grey at the halfway point. The lower one is visibly lighter through the middle, because it reaches half the light there instead. Neither is "prettier"; they answer different questions, and the lower one answers the one a renderer asks.
  2. Now the red-to-green pair. This is the one that should make you wince. The upper strip passes through a dark, muddy olive; the lower passes through a clean bright yellow. Cover the labels and ask which one looks like a mix of red and green light.
  3. Watch the seam, not the strips. The two halves of each pair touch, and the discontinuity along that line is the size of the error at every point in the ramp simultaneously.
  4. Press M. The ball's trail switches between the two fade rules. The naive fade dies away much sooner — the dots reach the background long before they should, which is §3.3's table happening in motion.
  5. Press 1. Everything from Lesson 1.4 is still running underneath.
Checkpoint — verified before publishing

Built warning-clean at -Wall -Wextra with Apple Clang against SDL 3.4.12; runs and shuts down cleanly. The colour module was driven through a 24-assertion harness confirming every number this lesson quotes:

  • srgb_to_linear_u8(128) = 0.2159 and linear_to_srgb_u8(0.5) = 188, with 188 decoding back to 0.5029.
  • The transform is strictly increasing across all 256 values, and the round trip encode(decode(v)) returns v for every one of the 256 — zero failures. That losslessness is worth knowing: it means storing a colour, decoding it, and re-encoding it unchanged costs nothing.
  • Endpoints exact: 0 → 0.0, 255 → 1.0, and back.
  • Out-of-range light saturates rather than wrapping: 2.5 → 255, −1.0 → 0.
  • The lookup table agrees with the exact function to within 1e-7 across all 256 entries.
  • Red mixed with green at t = 0.5 gives exactly (128, 128, 0) naively and (188, 188, 0) linearly, with blue 0 in both; both rules agree exactly at t = 0 and t = 1.
  • Alpha is not converted by either rule — both give 128 when mixing transparent with opaque halfway.
  • The fade table of §3.3 reproduced exactly: 225, 188, 137, 89.
  • The checkerboard identity: encoding the average of black's and white's light gives 188, which is why §3.2's test works.

All of these were independently computed in Python first and then reproduced by the C++, which is why the lesson and the engine agree to the digit.

7Common Pitfalls & Debugging

Cross-fades pass through mud — two bright colours blend through something dark
Symptom
Fading red to green goes through brown; blue to yellow goes through grey. The endpoints look right and the middle looks wrong.
Cause
Interpolating stored values. The midpoint of the numbers emits 21.6% of each channel, not 50% (§3.1).
Fix
Decode, interpolate, re-encode — mix_linear. If a whole pipeline is doing this, converting per-operation is a stopgap; the real answer is to work in linear throughout (§3.5).
Fades fall off a cliff — a smooth fade-out vanishes early
Symptom
An object fading out looks fine for the first half, then disappears abruptly. Fading in looks like it starts late.
Cause
The same error, and it is worst where fades spend most of their time. Asking for 10% brightness and storing 26 emits 1% — a factor of ten (§3.3).
Fix
Fade in linear light. Press M in the demo to see both, and note it is the tail of the fade that differs most.
Downscaled images get darker — thumbnails and mipmaps lose brightness
Symptom
An image shrunk to half size is noticeably darker than the original, and each further mip level is darker still. Most visible on fine bright detail against dark backgrounds — starfields, hair, foliage.
Cause
Downscaling averages neighbouring pixels, which is mixing, which is wrong on stored values. Averaging black and white gives 128, which emits 21.6% rather than the correct 50%.
Fix
Decode to linear, average, re-encode. This is why serious image tools have a "gamma-correct resampling" option and why it is not the default in older ones. Our mipmap generation in Module 6 does it correctly, and the lesson there points back here.
Anti-aliased text has dark fringes — light text on dark looks thin and grubby
Symptom
White text on a black background looks thinner and dirtier than it should, with grey haloes. Dark text on white usually looks acceptable, which makes the problem easy to dismiss.
Cause
An anti-aliased edge pixel is a coverage-weighted mix of the glyph and the background — mixing again. The asymmetry between light-on-dark and dark-on-light is the curve's shape showing through.
Fix
Blend the coverage in linear light. Note the distinction from §3.4: the coverage value is linear and must not be converted, but the two colours it is blending between must be.
Everything is washed out — the fix made it worse
Symptom
After "adding gamma correction", the whole image looks pale, milky, and low-contrast.
Cause
A conversion applied twice, or in the wrong direction — decoding something that was already linear, or writing linear values into a buffer the display will decode again. It is the same curve applied one time too many.
Fix
Track, for every buffer and every texture, whether its contents are encoded or linear, and write it down. Most gamma bugs are not misunderstandings of the maths but lost track of which space a value is already in. Module 6 makes this an explicit property of every texture rather than a thing you remember.
Alpha behaves strangely after converting — edges too transparent, blends off
Symptom
Semi-transparent objects are too see-through, and 50% alpha behaves like roughly 20%.
Cause
Alpha was run through the transfer function along with the colour channels. 128 became 0.216 rather than 0.502 (§3.4).
Fix
Convert three channels, not four. Alpha is coverage — a fraction of area, already linear, never encoded.

8Exercises

1.6.1Find your display's midpointwarm-up

Draw a black-and-white one-pixel checkerboard into the framebuffer, and beside it a row of solid patches from 120 to 200 in steps of 10. Step back and find which patch merges with the checkerboard. How close is it to 188?

Hint

Our framebuffer is scaled 4× to the window, so a one-pixel checkerboard becomes 4×4 blocks — step back further, or temporarily set k_fb_width/k_fb_height to 1280×720 so one framebuffer pixel is one screen pixel.

Solution

On a well-behaved display the match lands at 188, give or take one patch. A match at a markedly lower value means your display is applying a steeper curve than sRGB — common on monitors in a "vivid" or "game" picture mode, which is worth knowing before you spend an evening tuning a scene's lighting on one.

The exercise has a second point. You just built a measuring instrument out of two rectangles and your own visual system, and it measures a property of the whole display chain that no amount of reading the source could tell you. Keep the habit.

1.6.2Break the alpha rulewarm-up

In mix_linear, change the alpha argument from mix_channel_encoded to mix_channel_linear — that is, convert all four channels. Predict what happens before you build. Then check with the test harness.

Solution

A half-and-half mix of transparent and opaque now yields alpha 188 rather than 128 — the blend is more opaque than asked for, because the encode step brightens a value that was never dimmed. (Conversely, an implementation that decoded alpha would produce 55.)

The demo may barely change, because our framebuffer ignores alpha — nothing composites yet. That is the interesting part: the bug is invisible today and would appear, months later, as "transparency looks wrong" in a system nobody would connect to a colour function. It is worth seeing how quietly a units error can wait.

1.6.3Measure the round trip's costguided

Time mix_encoded against mix_linear over a few million calls at -O2. Then try replacing the lookup table with a direct call to srgb_to_linear and measure again. What does the table actually buy?

Hint

Remember Lesson 1.5's benchmarking trap: make sure the results are consumed so nothing is optimised away, take a median of several runs, and change one thing at a time.

Solution sketch

mix_linear is several times slower than mix_encoded — it does three table lookups, three interpolations, and three pow calls on the way out. Removing the table makes it worse still, since the decode side becomes three more pows.

The conclusion is not "linear is expensive". It is that converting per operation is expensive, which is precisely the argument for a pipeline that decodes once at the start and encodes once at the end (§3.5). You have just measured the reason Module 6 is built the way it is.

1.6.4Downscale an image, twiceguided

Fill the framebuffer with a fine pattern of alternating pure black and pure white pixels. Now average each 2×2 block down to a quarter-size image — once on stored values, once in linear light — and draw both. What value does each produce, and which is correct?

Solution

Each 2×2 block holds two black and two white pixels. Averaging stored values gives 128; averaging light gives 0.5, which encodes to 188. The correct result is 188, for exactly the reason §3.2's test works — half the area emitting full light is half the light.

Compare your two downscaled images against the original pattern viewed from a distance. The linear one matches; the naive one is conspicuously darker. Repeat the downscale three or four times and the naive version marches steadily towards black, which is exactly what a mipmap chain built the naive way does — and why distant foliage in older games fades to dark mush.

1.6.5Design the linear framebufferopen

Sketch what framebuffer would look like if it stored linear light instead of encoded sRGB. What type would a pixel be? What happens at put_pixel, at fill_rect, and at upload time? What breaks that currently works? Do not build it — Module 6 does — but write down the interface and the costs.

Hint

Start from the constraint that eight bits of linear light bands visibly in the darks (§2.2), and that light values can legitimately exceed 1.0.

Solution sketch

A pixel becomes four floats, or four 16-bit halfs — so the buffer is two to four times larger, and 320×180 goes from 225 KB to 450 KB or 900 KB. Full-screen clears and fills cost proportionally more memory bandwidth, which Lesson 1.5's measurements say is what dominates.

put_pixel and fill_rect get simpler, not harder: no conversion, because everything is already linear. All the complexity moves to the edges. Upload gains a conversion pass over every pixel — decode is gone, but encode now happens once per pixel per frame — and that pass is also where tonemapping belongs, since it is the moment values above 1.0 must be brought into a range the display can show.

What breaks: every colour constant in the codebase. pack_argb(126, 162, 236) means an encoded colour, and in a linear buffer it would be far too bright. You would need a distinct type — colour_srgb versus colour_linear — so the compiler catches the mixing rather than your eyes. That type distinction is the single most valuable thing to take from this exercise, and it is what Module 6 builds.

9Recap & Next

What the engine can do now that it could not before

  • It knows what its numbers mean. A stored channel value is a position on a perceptually spaced dial, not a quantity of light — and the engine can convert between the two exactly, in both directions.
  • It can mix colours correctly, and can demonstrate the difference against the naive version side by side, in a static ramp and in motion.
  • It treats alpha as what it is: a coverage fraction, already linear, never put through a light transform.
  • It has a proper home for colour — gfx/colour.hpp, with the dependency running from framebuffer to colour and not the other way.
  • You can predict a whole family of bugs on sight: muddy cross-fades, fades that die early, darkening mipmaps, fringed text. All one cause.

The idea to carry forward: the storage format is not the arithmetic format. sRGB is an excellent way to keep a colour in eight bits and a bad way to calculate with one, and the fix is always the same shape — decode, compute, re-encode. Module 6's HDR pipeline is that sentence applied to an entire renderer instead of a single mix.

Next: 1.7 — 2D Vectors, Geometrically. We have been writing x and y as separate floats and paying for it — Exercise 1.2.3's diagonal movement was 41% too fast for want of a way to say "this direction, one unit long". Vectors arrive as arrows before they arrive as components, the dot product arrives as a shadow before it arrives as a formula, and the engine gets the first piece of the maths library everything after Module 2 is built on.

  sdl3: FetchContent, pinned release-3.4.12; target SDL3::SDL3; SDL_TEST_LIBRARY OFF
  sdl3-api: bool SDL_Init/PollEvent; SDL_CreateWindow(title,w,h,flags) no x/y;
            SDL_CreateRenderer(window,name); event.key.key; <SDL3/SDL_main.h> separate
  sdl3-input: const bool *SDL_GetKeyboardState(int*) — bool, NOT SDL2's Uint8;
            SDL_SCANCODE_COUNT = 512; wheel is event-only; SDL_PollEvent pumps;
            focus loss -> SDL_ResetKeyboard() sends key-UP events
  sdl3-time: SDL_GetTicksNS() is monotonic; SDL_Delay waits AT LEAST the requested time;
            SDL_SetRenderVSync; SDL_RenderDebugText/Format = 8x8 ASCII font
  sdl3-pixels: SDL_TEXTUREACCESS_STREAMING + SDL_LockTexture (WRITE-ONLY, prior contents
            UNDEFINED, returned pitch MAY EXCEED width*4 -> copy ROW BY ROW).
            SDL_RenderTexture(r,tex,NULL,NULL): NULL dst = whole target, so a small
            framebuffer scales to the window and resize needs no code.
            SDL_SetTextureScaleMode(..., SDL_SCALEMODE_NEAREST).
            FORMAT NAMES: "8888" = packed native-endian integer, MSB first
            (ARGB8888 = 0xAARRGGBB); "32" = byte order in memory. On little-endian they
            are REVERSED: RGBA32 == ABGR8888, BGRA32 == ARGB8888 (header-verified).
  framebuffer: row-major, index = y*width + x. An x past width lands on the NEXT ROW
            (candy-striping); a stray y leaves the buffer (UB). put_pixel is
            bounds-checked; row(y) is the documented fast path; fill_rect CLIPS ONCE.
            MEASURED (M4 Pro): put_pixel vs row pointer 5.1x (-O0) / 14.8x (-O2);
            rows-outer vs columns-outer 10.9x @320x180 -> 48.8x @4K.
            BENCHMARK TRAP: measuring both loop orders THROUGH put_pixel gave 1.00x —
            its overhead swamped the effect. Differ in ONLY what is under test; use -O2.
  colour: stored channel values are sRGB-ENCODED, not light. VERIFIED NUMBERS:
            128 emits 21.6% of white's light; half the light is stored as 188;
            fades: 75%->225, 50%->188, 25%->137, 10%->89 (naive 64 for 25% emits 5.1%).
            red+green mixed at t=0.5: naive (128,128,0) dark olive, linear (188,188,0)
            bright yellow. Encode/decode round trip is LOSSLESS for all 256 values.
            Exact piecewise transform (0.04045 / 12.92 / 0.055 / 2.4) — NOT pow(x,2.2).
            256-entry LUT for decode only (encode's input is a continuous float).
            ALPHA IS COVERAGE, NOT LIGHT — never put it through the transfer function.
            SAFE on stored values: copy, compare, pick. WRONG: mix, fade, average,
            downscale/mipmap, add light, anti-alias edges.
            NOT YET FIXED: we convert per-operation, which is slow and lossy. A real
            linear pipeline needs a float/half framebuffer, headroom above 1.0, and a
            tonemap step = Module 6's HDR work.
  time-model: absolute time = Uint64 ns; dt() clamped 0.25 s; fps() display only
  loop: THE loop, settled as of 1.4 and not changing again:
            drain -> clk.tick() -> in.update() -> stepper.begin_frame(clk.dt())
            -> while (stepper.next_step()) { previous = current; simulate(current, h); }
            -> alpha = stepper.alpha() -> draw into framebuffer -> upload -> present
  fixed-step: h = 1/60 default; invariant 0 <= accumulator < h so alpha in [0,1);
            previous = current INSIDE the loop; simulate() gets h, never clk.dt();
            interpolation renders at exactly T - h; spiral guarded by dt clamp + step cap;
            never interpolate across a teleport
  input-model: poll levels, DERIVE edges; COPY SDL's array, never alias;
            scancodes for positions, keycodes for symbols
  shaders: HLSL -> SDL_shadercross (3.0.0-preview) -> SPIR-V/DXIL/MSL  [Module 4+]
  cpp: C++20, no exceptions/RTTI in core, snake_case, private members trailing _,
       .hpp + #pragma once, [[nodiscard]], anonymous namespace for TU-local helpers,
       never `using namespace` at file scope, -Wall -Wextra//W4 all fixed.
       Owning containers (std::vector) over new/delete. SDL handles still manually
       managed in main.cpp on purpose; Module 5 wraps them.
       Header dependency direction: framebuffer -> colour, never the reverse.
  build: CMake >= 3.24, out-of-source (build/); sources listed explicitly (never GLOB);
         target_include_directories(engine PRIVATE src)
  docs-tooling: shared <style> AND trailing <script> propagated by
         docs/_template/apply-shared.py — never by hand. Marker regions
         SHARED-CSS:BEGIN/END and SHARED-SCRIPT:BEGIN/END; widget JS goes OUTSIDE them.
         Run `apply-shared.py --check` before committing docs/.
