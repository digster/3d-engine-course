## The lesson's spine

Events answer "what happened"; gameplay usually asks "what is". The lesson opens with the
failure: movement driven by `SDL_EVENT_KEY_DOWN` inherits the OS **key repeat** — one move,
~0.5 s of nothing, then lurching. Worked out numerically: 16 movements in the first second
versus 60 when polling, and since repeat rate is a user preference (down to ~2/s), two players
on identical hardware get an 8× speed difference.

Resolution: **poll levels, derive edges.**

```
down     =  keys_[k];
pressed  =  keys_[k] && !keys_prev_[k];
released = !keys_[k] &&  keys_prev_[k];
```

One source of truth, two snapshots, three answers — so `key_down` and `key_pressed` cannot
contradict each other, and queries are pure (asking does not consume an edge).

Also covered: scancode vs keycode (the AZERTY bug, with a keyboard diagram showing WASD's
positions spelling ZQSD); the drain-then-sample ordering rule; and the honest limits — a tap
between samples is invisible, and the wheel/text have no pollable state at all.

## Verified, not assumed

Checked against SDL headers at the pinned `release-3.4.12`:

- `const bool *SDL_GetKeyboardState(int*)` — **bool** in SDL3, not SDL2's `Uint8`.
- `SDL_SCANCODE_COUNT = 512`; letters run from `A = 4`, so `W = 26` (used in the worked example).
- `SDL_GetMouseState` returns `SDL_MouseButtonFlags`, writes `float` x/y; `SDL_BUTTON_MASK(n)`.
- `event.key.{down,repeat}` are `bool`; `event.wheel.{x,y}` are `float` + a `direction` flag.
- `SDL_PollEvent` → `SDL_WaitEventTimeoutNS(e,0)` → `SDL_PumpEventsInternal`, i.e. polling pumps.
- Traced `SDL_SetKeyboardFocus` → `SDL_ResetKeyboard` in `src/events/SDL_keyboard.c:350`: the
  stuck-key-after-alt-tab fix is delivered **as key-up events**, so it only reaches you if you
  drain before sampling. That turned an ordering convention into a demonstrable consequence.

## The lesson's spine

1.2's box moved 6 px per *frame*, so its speed was a property of the monitor (180 / 360 / 864 px/s
at 30 / 60 / 144 fps — the 144 Hz player's game is 4.8× faster). The fix is one multiplication,
and the lesson is mostly about the fix's limits.

Three measurement decisions first: **monotonic** not wall (a wall clock corrected by NTP hands you
a *negative* dt), **nanoseconds** not milliseconds (±20% measurement error at 300 fps; truncates
to zero above 1000 fps), and **`Uint64` not `float`** for absolute time.

Then the payoff and its boundary. `Σ(v·dtᵢ) = v·T` is exact — but only because `v` factors out of
the sum. Anything accelerating is integrated with explicit Euler, whose error is `½·g·T·h`:
**proportional to the step**, so the frame rate remains an input to the physics. That single fact
is the entire argument for 1.4, and the lesson derives it rather than asserting it.

Closes by pricing each naive patch (clamp / floor / smooth / cap) — none restores determinism —
and naming the spiral of death (cost per step ÷ time simulated per step > 1).

## Verified, not assumed

- Traced `SDL_GetTicksNS` → `SDL_GetPerformanceCounter` → `CLOCK_MONOTONIC_RAW` (the Unix source
  comments that it chose that clock as "not subject to adjustment by NTP") / `mach_absolute_time` /
  `QueryPerformanceCounter`. The header never says "monotonic"; the implementation does.
- `SDL_Delay`/`SDL_DelayNS` wait *at least* the requested time; `SDL_DelayPrecise` busy-waits to
  get closer. Measured `SDL_Delay(10)` ≈ 11.8 ms in our own harness, which is the doc comment
  demonstrating itself.
- `SDL_SetRenderVSync`, `SDL_RENDERER_VSYNC_DISABLED`, `SDL_RenderDebugTextFormat` (8×8 ASCII
  bitmap font, current draw colour), `SDL_SetRenderScale`.
- **The float-freeze claim was compiled and run**, not argued: `86400.0f + (1.0f/500)` evaluates to
  exactly `86400.0f`. ulp(86400.0f) = 7.8 ms.
- Every worked number checked in Python first: the 30/60/144 fps table, the dt-cancellation table,
  and the Euler error table (0, 2.5, 3.75, 4.375, 4.9167 m) against the closed form `g·T·(T−h)/2`.

## The lesson's spine — three broken attempts, in order

Deliberately structured as a derivation rather than a presentation, because each naive fix breaks
something new and that sequence *is* the understanding:

1. **Constant step, no accumulator.** Physics is now identical everywhere — and the game runs at
   (fps ÷ 60)× speed. Same 4.8× spread between 30 and 144 fps that 1.3 opened with, just relocated.
2. **The accumulator.** Bank real time, spend whole steps, carry the change. Correct speed *and*
   identical physics. Invariant: after the loop, `0 <= accumulator < h`.
3. **…and it judders.** The render is stale by the accumulator, which *varies* 0→h. Derived
   numerically: at 100 fps / 60 Hz the staleness sawtooths 10.0, 3.33, 13.33, 6.67, 0.0 ms with
   period 5 (`0,1,0,1,1` steps per frame).
4. **Interpolation.** `alpha = accumulator / h`; draw `lerp(previous, current, alpha)`. Proof that
   this renders at exactly `T − h`: the accumulator cancels. A lag swinging 0→h becomes a constant
   h. **Smoothness is consistency, not immediacy** — on average interpolation is *further* behind.
5. **The spiral guard**, plus honesty about what it costs (permanent slow motion, reported).

Closes with choosing `h`, and a warning that fixed-timestep determinism is same-binary/same-machine
only — FMA contraction, x87 intermediates, `libm`, vectorisation. Necessary for lockstep, nowhere
near sufficient.

## Verification

`fixed_step` driven through a 15-assertion harness: the 7-steps-over-12-frames pattern; `alpha`
inside `[0,1)` across 700 irregular frames including zero-length ones; sim time trailing real time

116.67 ms dropped; 100 consecutive one-second frames still terminating at 8 steps each; `set_rate`

The demo's payoff modelled before coding: with a 60 Hz step the ball's apexes are **236, 204, 171,
137 at 2000, 240, 60 and 20 fps — identical**. Changing the *sim* rate does change them (10 Hz: 56,
−196), which is the integrator error, now under our control.

Build warning-clean at `-Wall -Wextra`; runs and shuts down cleanly.

## Decisions

- **`fixed_step` is a class, not four lines in the loop.** The accumulator has an invariant
  maintained by exactly one subtraction; inline, that subtraction is one `continue` from being
  skipped and the failure is an infinite loop *inside a frame*.
- **On hitting the step cap, drain the excess** rather than just returning — otherwise `alpha > 1`
  and the renderer silently extrapolates exactly when the machine is struggling.
- **`clamp_rate` written as `if (!(rate >= min))`** so NaN is caught (every NaN comparison is false).
- **The demo box bounces, never wraps.** Wrapping is a teleport, and interpolating a teleport draws
  the box sliding backwards across the screen. Made it Exercise 1.4.4 instead.
- **Demo state moved into a `sim_state` struct** — interpolation forces it, since "the previous
  value of everything" is only expressible if "everything" has a name. Flagged in the lesson as a
  preview of the pressure that produces the ECS in Module 5.

## The lesson's spine

`SDL_RenderFillRect` is a fine way to draw a rectangle and a useless foundation for a renderer,
because a renderer is a machine for deciding each pixel's colour. So: allocate, agree on a meaning,
write, upload.

The one structural idea is `index = y * width + x`, derived rather than asserted (complete rows
above contribute `y × width`; then step `x`). Its corollary is the lesson's failure mode: **an x
past `width` is not an error** — in an 8-wide buffer `(8,2)` computes 24, which *is* `(0,3)` — so an
overshooting run reappears on the left of the next row down. Candy-striping. A stray `y`, by
contrast, leaves the buffer and is UB, where the crash is the *lucky* outcome because the stripe can
survive review.

Then: what 32 bits of colour are, presenting through a streaming texture, and two ways to write a
pixel.

## Verified, not assumed

- `SDL_LockTexture` is **write-only** with **undefined** prior contents — SDL's header explicitly
  says to keep the master copy at application level, which is exactly what the framebuffer is.
- The lock's **pitch may exceed `width * 4`** (driver row padding), so the upload copies row by row.
  One whole-buffer `memcpy` shears the image on precisely the machines that aren't yours.
- `SDL_RenderTexture(r, tex, NULL, NULL)` — NULL dst is "the entire rendering target", so a 320×180
  buffer scales to the window and **window resize now needs no code at all**.
- **Format naming**, from `SDL_pixels.h`: `8888` names are packed into a native-endian integer
  (MSB first); `32` names are byte order in memory. On little-endian they are reversed, and the
  header proves it: `RGBA32 = ABGR8888`, `BGRA32 = ARGB8888`. We store `Uint32` and build with
  shifts only, so `ARGB8888` matches on every platform and endianness never enters the engine until
  an image loader does (Module 6).

## The measurement that lied — the best thing in this lesson

Two benchmarks, both real:

- `put_pixel` vs a row pointer: **5.1× at `-O0`, 14.8× at `-O2`**. The optimiser cannot prove the
  bounds check redundant, so it keeps emitting it; and the *ratio* grows under optimisation because
  the useful work gets cheaper while per-call overhead does not.
- Rows-outer vs columns-outer: **10.9× at 320×180, rising to 48.8× at 4K** (64-byte line = 16
  pixels; the penalty grows as the buffer outgrows the caches).

But the first version of that second experiment measured **1.00× — no difference at all**, which
would have been a fascinating false result. The cause: both loops went through `put_pixel`, whose
overhead swamped the memory effect entirely. **The instrument was louder than the signal.** Rewriting
both paths to use row pointers, so the only difference was access order, revealed the 10.9× gap.

This mattered concretely: my `fill_rect` comment already asserted the cache claim, and for about ten
minutes I had a measurement contradicting a comment I had shipped. Both are now correct and the
comment cites the numbers.

- **320×180 framebuffer, scaled 4× to the window.** Pedagogy (every pixel is a visible 4×4 block)
  and a real technique (fixed internal resolution, dynamic-resolution scaling) at once. Resize
  handling disappeared from the loop entirely.
- **`SDL_LockTexture` over `SDL_UpdateTexture`.** SDL's docs prefer it for streaming, and it forces
  the pitch lesson to be concrete rather than theoretical.
- **Two-tier API on purpose:** `put_pixel` checked, `row(y)` the documented fast path with the row
  index still clamped. Safe by default, fast when asked — the shape the drawing API keeps.
- **No `clear()` in the demo,** because the gradient covers every pixel. Flagged in-code as a real
  optimisation *and* a real trap for whoever next changes the background.
- **The texture stays manually managed in `main.cpp`** alongside window and renderer, consistent
  with the 0.5 arc; the framebuffer's `std::vector` is the RAII contrast, which is now the C++-in-
  place callout.

## The lesson's spine

Copying bits never asks what they mean; *combining* them does. Mix red and green half-and-half the
obvious way and you get `(128,128,0)`, a dark olive, where the answer is `(188,188,0)`, a bright
yellow.

The two numbers to remember: **128 emits 21.6% of white's light**, and **half the light is stored
as 188**. Both are printed by the engine at startup, computed by the same code that draws the ramps
— so a build that disagrees with the prose indicates a real bug.

Structured as: why the encoding exists (the eye responds to ratios, so evenly spaced light would
waste 128 of 256 codes on the bright half where nothing is distinguishable and band visibly in the
darks) → the exact piecewise transform → a test the reader can perform on their own display → what
it breaks → why alpha is exempt → what we are deliberately *not* fixing.

## The honesty section, which is the point of the lesson

§3.5 says plainly that having correct mixing functions is not having a correct renderer. We convert
per operation, which is slower and lossier than a real pipeline — each round trip requantises to
256 steps. Doing it properly needs a float/half framebuffer, headroom above 1.0, and tonemapping;
that is Module 6's HDR work. Building it now, on a renderer that cannot draw a triangle, would be
plumbing before the house.

## Verified, not assumed

A 24-assertion harness reproduced every number the lesson quotes, after they were derived
independently in Python:

- `srgb_to_linear_u8(128) = 0.2159`; `linear_to_srgb_u8(0.5) = 188`; 188 decodes back to 0.5029.
- **Strictly increasing across all 256 values**, and the encode/decode round trip is **lossless for
  every one of the 256** — zero failures.
- Out-of-range light saturates (2.5 → 255, −1.0 → 0) rather than wrapping.
- The LUT matches the exact function to within 1e-7 across all entries.
- Red ⊕ green at t=0.5 gives exactly (128,128,0) naive and (188,188,0) linear; both rules agree
  exactly at t=0 and t=1.
- **Alpha is not converted** by either rule.
- The fade table reproduced exactly: 225, 188, 137, 89.
- The checkerboard identity: encoding the average of black's and white's light gives 188.

## The interactive test

A real gamma test built from device pixels: alternating one-pixel black and white lines beside
solid 128 and 188 patches. It works because light from unresolvable detail adds *linearly* in the
eye, so the stripes are physically 50% light whatever the numbers say.

The implementation detail that matters: `repeating-linear-gradient` with **hard stops** — both
colour stops at the same position — or the browser interpolates and the stripes blur into solid
grey, silently destroying the test. Verified the stops programmatically and the rendering with a
device-scale screenshot. The page tells the reader it needs 100% zoom.

## The derivation, which is the point of the lesson

The brief was explicit: "the dot product taught as a shadow — a projection — long before any formula
appears". So the lesson goes intuition → derivation → formula → code, and never the reverse.

The standard derivation of `a·b = ax·bx + ay·by` uses the **law of cosines**, which is beyond the
trigonometry this course assumes. The route taken instead needs only two things, both drawable:

1. **Shadows add** — projection is linear, which Figure 5 shows by laying two arrows tip to tail and
   dropping perpendiculars.
2. **Components ARE projections onto the axes** — so `x̂ · b = |b|cos α = bx` by definition.

Three lines, no law of cosines, and it explains *why* the formula has that shape rather than
asserting that it does.

## Verified, not assumed

A 34-assertion harness, with every figure computed in Python first:

- Arithmetic checked at **compile time** via `static_assert` — the operators really are `constexpr`.
- `(3,4)·(4,3) = 24` reached **both ways**: components, and `|a|cos θ × |b| = 4.8 × 5` with
  θ = 16.2602°. The derivation, checked numerically.
- Sign table: 50 / 0 / −25 for aligned / perpendicular / opposite; and
  `dot(v, perpendicular(v))` is **exactly** 0 — the products cancel before rounding can occur.
- The diagonal: `|(1,−1)| = 1.41421`, raw step **127.27922** vs normalised **90.00000**.
- `normalised({0,0})` returns `(0,0)` and **not** NaN, with the naive `0/√0` confirmed NaN in the
  same test so the guard is demonstrably earning its keep.
- `sizeof(vec2) == 8`, the measurement behind the pass-by-value rule.

One test failure was **my test's fault**, not the code's: I wrote the expected raw-step length as
127.279 against a 1e-4 tolerance when the true value is 127.27922.

## The lesson's spine

A checkpoint still needs one new idea, and this is it: **a simulation that advances in steps does
not see the spaces between them.** So "do these two boxes overlap?" is a question about an
*instant*, while collision is a fact about an *interval*. Every answer the naive test gives is
correct; it is answering the wrong question.

The quantitative payoff is the part worth keeping:

```
overlap window along an axis = size_a + size_b        (Minkowski sum in miniature)
a discrete test is reliable only while |v_axis| * h < that
```

Our numbers: an 8 px window and a 260 px/s cap, so at 60 Hz the safe ceiling is **480 px/s**. The
bug is not merely untested — it is **unobservable** on the development machine, and it is there.
At 10 Hz it fires on the opening serve. That gap between "my tests pass" and "this is correct" is
the transferable lesson, and it is sharper stated with arithmetic than with exhortation.

## Verified, not assumed

Everything numeric in the lesson came out of a scratch harness, not out of confidence:

- `reflect((3,4), (0,−1)) = (3,−4)` — agrees with the obvious sign flip, which is the *point* of
  the check; and `reflect((0,5), 45° wall) = (5,0)`, which no component negation can produce.
- The worked step: `t = 0.190476`, speed 105→112, velocity `(109.5525, −23.2861)`, final
  `x = 22.8685`. The naive test on the identical step reports no collision and concedes a point.
- Determinism: bit-identical over 20 000 steps on a shared seed; **diverges at a different step
  size** (60 Hz vs 120 Hz separate within two simulated seconds) — `h` is an input too.
- The game is a game: a perfect tracker beats the AI 11–4, longest rally 61 hits, the ball reaches
  its speed cap, and it never leaves the court in 300 000 steps.

Two of the harness's own first assertions were wrong rather than the code — one stepped the sim
once when the ball had not yet reached the paddle, the other compared two matches at a moment when
both happened to be parked mid-court between points. Worth remembering that a failing check is a
claim about the *checker* until proven otherwise.

## The KaTeX bug — six lessons old, found by writing the first equation

The loader `<script>` tags sat immediately **below** `<!-- SHARED-SCRIPT:END -->` in the template,
so `apply-shared.py` never propagated them. Every lesson shipped with KaTeX's CSS and no renderer.

It survived because the symptom — raw TeX on the page — is *identical to the documented
CDN-unreachable fallback*, so it reads as working-as-intended; and because lessons 1.1–1.7 contain
**zero** `.eq` blocks, so there was nothing to fail. 1.8 is the first lesson with display math.

Fixed by moving the block inside the region and deleting the now-duplicate standalone copies from
`conventions.html` and `math-toolbox.html` (hand-authored, so they had their own). The general
shape: **a fallback indistinguishable from the failure it guards against will hide that failure
indefinitely.** Check the positive signal — `.katex` count equals `.eq` count — not the absence of
an error.

## Two verification techniques that earned their keep

- **`getBoundingClientRect()`, never `getBBox()`**, for label spill and collision checks. `getBBox`
  is in *local* coordinates, so every `<text>` inside a `<g transform="translate(…)">` is compared
  against the wrong origin — three confident false positives before I noticed.
- **Splice listings from the real files** (`@@LISTING:src/game/pong.cpp@@` + a small script). Five
  full-file listings, ~700 lines; retyping any of them would make drift from the compiled source a
  matter of luck. The highlighter round-trip check then confirms the escaping survived end to end.

Figure 3 needed a real redraw: the overlap band was drawn 78 px wide where the geometry said 52,
which wrongly swallowed a ball and destroyed the figure's whole argument. Rebuilt to an exact
horizontal scale with a separate `ball.x` axis below the picture, so "the ball's left edge lands
either side of the 8 px band, never in it" is something you can *see* rather than take on trust.

- **`src/game/` exists now**, three modules before the Module 5 refactor needs it. The boundary

- **The naive collision test stays in the shipped code**, behind `state::swept_collision` and

- **Tunnelling is demonstrated with 1.4's existing `[1-4]` sim-rate keys** — no new controls, and

- **The PRNG seed lives inside the state**, not in `SDL_rand`'s hidden global — otherwise the
