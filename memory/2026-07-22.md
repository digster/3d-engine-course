## The lesson's spine

**Derived, not defined** — which is the curriculum's own framing and happens to be literally true.
2.2's rasterizer computes three edge functions per pixel, uses their *signs* for the inside test,
and discards their *magnitudes*, which were areas. 2.3 adds one division and stops discarding.

The centre-of-mass origin (Möbius, 1827) is the intuition; the three sub-triangles that tile the
original are the mechanism. Everything else — sum to 1, 1 at a vertex, 0 on the opposite edge,
negative outside, constant along parallels — follows.

## The best thing in the lesson: which invariant to assert

A **rotated vertex pairing** produces weights that are all in `[0,1]` and **still sum to exactly
1** — and describe a different point. Verified: the correct `(0.4, 0.3, 0.3)` reconstructs
`(5.0, 5.0)`; rotated by one it gives `(5.2, 5.8)`.

So "they sum to 1" has almost no diagnostic power: it passes for all three wrong rotations.
**Reconstruction** — `w0·v0 + w1·v1 + w2·v2 == P` — passes for exactly one. That generalised into
the lesson's takeaway habit: *when choosing an invariant to assert, prefer the property that
determines the answer over one that merely constrains it.*

I nearly shipped this bug myself while planning, which is why it got its own section, its own
worked example, its own pitfall and its own exercise.

## Verified, not asserted (18 checks)

- Worked example: `w = (0.4, 0.3, 0.3)`, reconstructs `(5,5)` exactly.
- Reconstruction over **3,721 points** spread well beyond the triangle: worst error 0.000015 px.
- Sum precision over **32,761 points**: worst `|sum−1|` = **2.4e-7**, and bitwise `1.0f` only
  **27,876 / 32,761 ≈ 85%** of the time. Both halves reported — the first alone would imply "close
  enough to compare", the second is why you must not.
- "All three weights ≥ 0" is identical to `fill_triangle`'s inside test over **5,041 points**.
- Iso-lines: `w0` varies by **exactly 0.0** along a line parallel to the opposite edge.
- Vertex / edge-midpoint / centroid special cases all as predicted.
- Degenerate input returns zeros, not NaN.

## Demo redesign

First version of the iso-line view drew contours wherever `w_i ∈ [0,1]`, which sprawls into a
rectangular blob well past the triangle and buries the shape it describes. Restricted to the
interior and added the outline: now it reads as three families of evenly spaced parallel lines,
one per vertex — the triangle's own grid, which is exactly the picture the section argues for.

The probe draws its sub-triangles using **the same pairing `barycentric_at` uses**, so the picture
and the maths cannot drift apart. Same discipline as 2.1's pixel inspector reading back the real
routine's output.

## Caught by tooling

- `apply-shared.py` lint: an inline `fill="var(--axis-z)"` on an SVG `<text>` — the documented
  trap where CSS silently wins. Fixed with the `.lbl-z` class.
- `check-page.js`: two Figure 2 labels sitting on sub-triangle boundaries. Fixed by computing each
  region's **centroid** from its vertices instead of eyeballing — the same discipline 2.2's
  Figure 1 needed after its extended edge lines turned out not to pass through the vertices.

Both tools have now caught something in every lesson since they were written. Worth the cost.

## Decisions

- **Scope held against 2.4.** The demo visualises the *coordinate* (a w0 ramp, an iso-line grid)
  rather than blending three vertex colours — that is 2.4's job and needs 1.6's linear-light care.
- **`barycentric_at` is standalone, not folded into the fill loop.** Keeps the lesson about the
  idea; Exercise 2.3.3 previews the incremental version and asks the student to measure float
  drift across a wide triangle.
- **Uniqueness stated and proved** (three coefficients, three conditions, one solution) — students
  otherwise file this under "a reasonable blend" rather than "the only affine one".
